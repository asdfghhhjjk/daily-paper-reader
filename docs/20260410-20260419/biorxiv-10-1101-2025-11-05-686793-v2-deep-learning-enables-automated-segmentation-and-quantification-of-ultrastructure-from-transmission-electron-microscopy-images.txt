Abstract 24 

Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) has become an essential technique for observing 25 

subcellular ultrastructure, and is widely used in both clinical diagnosis and biomedical research. 26 

However, analysis of TEM data remains extremely labor-intensive and often inconsistent across 27 

operators due to the lack of dedicated computational methods. Here, we present TEAMKidney, a 28 

deep learning framework for accurate and scalable measurement of ultrastructures in TEM images 29 

across species, magnifications, and instrument platforms. We collected 12,991 TEM images from 30 

patients with multiple kidney diseases and from different animal models. By combining a self-31 

training-based semantic segmentation stage with a TEM-tailored panoptic segmentation model, 32 

we address two major challenges in TEM data analysis: the lack of accurately labeled training data 33 

and the difficulty of achieving high segmentation accuracy for complex ultrastructure. Application 34 

of TEAMKidney to both human and animal images successfully reveals disease-associated 35 

changes in two critical glomerular ultrastructures: the glomerular basement membrane and 36 

podocyte foot processes. In addition to significantly outperforming existing tools, TEAMKidney 37 

shows close agreement with pathological expert measurements used in clinical assessment 38 

protocols. By reducing dependence on manual tracing while preserving expert-level accuracy, 39 

TEAMKidney demonstrates that deep learning can substantially reduce the burden of image 40 

analysis in both clinical pathology and biomedical research settings. 41

Introduction 43 

Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) provides high-resolution visualization of cellular 44 

ultrastructure beyond the resolving capabilities of light microscopy, immunofluorescence (IF) 45 

staining, immunohistochemistry, or molecular assays 1, 2 (Fig 1A). Beyond its extensive use in 46 

biomedical research and drug discovery, TEM remains an important diagnostic tool in many 47 

clinical areas, including infectious diseases, oncology, ophthalmology, neurology, and especially 48 

renal pathology 3-5 .49 

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) affects over 37 million people in the US 6 and 850 million 50 

worldwide 7, 8 . It has been recognized as a leading public health burden in the US. By 2027, the 51 

direct healthcare costs for kidney diseases in the US were projected to reach $173.4 billion 9.52 

Diabetic kidney disease and glomerular disease account for more than 50% of CKD cases 10, 11 and 53 

are the leading cause of kidney failure 12, 13 . These disorders encompass a broad spectrum of 54 

conditions characterized by distinct patterns of glomerular ultrastructural damage, which are 55 

associated with different therapeutic strategies and clinical outcomes. In the past decade, TEM has 56 

become an essential and often decisive component of the diagnostic modalities for kidney 57 

diseases 14 . Some major nephrology and pathology centers now utilize TEM as a routine diagnostic 58 

tool, applying it to more than 80% renal biopsies 14, 15 .59 

Despite its value, significant barriers limit the broader adoption of TEM. The technique 60 

remains highly labor-intensive 14 and producing a full pathology report can take up to 26 working 61 

days on average 5, primarily due to the need for expert renal pathologists to analyze the 62 

ultrastructure, such as glomerular basement membrane (GBM) and podocyte foot processes (PFP). 63 

Measurement of GBM and PFP widths is widely used for both research and clinical diagnosis, yet 64 

researchers and renal pathologists still rely primarily on manual measurement of TEM images (Fig 65

having experienced pathologists meticulously annotating the ultrastructure of glomeruli in 315 110

Results 124 

The Overview of the TEAMKidney Framework 125 

TEAMKidney is a deep learning-based automated framework for TEM image analysis, designed 126 

to precisely quantify ultrastructure, such as GBM and PFP widths (Fig. 1G). The framework 127 

comprises three key components: (1) integrating HRNet with self-training for initial semantic 128 

segmentation (Fig. 1H), (2) developing a novel model for panoptic segmentation of glomerular 129 

ultrastructure (Fig. 1H), and (3) performing post-segmentation analysis to robustly quantify 130 

detailed ultrastructure, such as GBM width and PFP width (Fig. 1I-K). 131 

To train and validate our framework across diverse pathological conditions and species, 132 

our study conducted an analysis of both animal and human models under normal and diseased 133 

states. For the mouse, the Integrin linked kinase podocyte-specific conditional knockout (ILK 134 

cKO) 35, 36 was used for model training, whereas the type IV collagen subunit alpha 3 knockout 135 

(Col4a3 KO) model was reserved as an independent evaluation dataset to assess model 136 

generalizability. For rats, the passive Heymann nephritis model (PHN) 37 was used, which was 137 

originally developed to characterize Membranous nephropathy (MN). For human samples, we 138 

focused on the comparison among healthy donors, diabetic kidney disease (DKD) 38, 39 and Fabry 139 

disease 40, 41 . For each species and disease model, a corresponding normal control dataset was 140 

included to enable both training and validation. 141 

This framework was implemented to automatically segment, label, and measure 142 

ultrastructure in TEM images, thereby eliminating the need for manual tracing and reducing 143 

observer variability. This design allows the framework to address diverse measurement tasks, 144 

including interspecies comparisons, disease-associated ultrastructural changes, and quantitative 145 

evaluation of experimental and clinical datasets. 146

Two-Stage Segmentation Model for Improved Fine Ultrastructure Recognition 148 

Due to the high magnification and resolution of grayscale TEM images, ultrastructural features 149 

often appear irregular and have unclear boundaries. In addition, TEM images are acquired across 150 

multiple magnification levels and spatial resolutions, resulting in substantial scale variation of 151 

ultrastructural components within and across datasets. These challenges prevent the direct 152 

adaptation of existing segmentation software for TEM image analysis (Fig. 1D-F) 31-33 . To address 153 

this, we design a novel deep learning model, Glom2Mask, that fuses both semantic and panoptic 154 

segmentation approaches, enabling precise segmentation of ultrastructural features across species 155 

and imaging scales. 156 

In the semantic segmentation stage (Fig. 2A), we evaluated three backbone networks for 157 

comparison: DeepLabV3 42-44 , PSPNet 45 , and HRNet 46 . All three models are effective in capturing 158 

global context and fusing multi-scale features. Overall, the models achieved comparable 159 

performance (Fig. 2B). Among them, HRNet attained the highest Dice scores across most 160 

categories and demonstrated markedly greater training efficiency. Although the average Dice 161 

scores of the three methods were similar, HRNet exhibited the most robust and consistent 162 

performance. Therefore, we selected HRNet as the backbone for our panoptic segmentation model 163 

in the second stage (Fig. 2C). In contrast, DeepLabV3 often failed on low-quality images (Fig. 2C, 164 

Supplementary Fig. 1), resulting in unreliable segmentation, while PSPNet tended to misclassify 165 

unrelated ultrastructure (Fig. 2C, Supplementary Fig. 1), particularly mistaking epithelial cells on 166 

glomerular capillaries for foot processes. 167 

Although our semantic segmentation model accurately recognized major ultrastructure, 168 

direct post-segmentation analysis to identify individual instances, such as PFP and slit diaphragms, 169

Concordance of TEAMKidney Predictions with Ground Truth Across Species 200 

To evaluate how segmentation accuracy affects ultrastructure measurement, we generated ground 201 

truth labels for a validation dataset comprising 48 images from 8 mouse glomeruli, 107 images 202 

from 12 rat glomeruli, and 91 images from 5 human glomeruli. All validation images were 203 

excluded from model training. GBM and PFP annotations were manually labeled by three human 204 

experts to generate precise ultrastructure masks. Then, the post-segmentation quantification 205 

algorithm was applied to both the TEAMKidney predictions and the expert-generated ground truth 206 

masks in this validation dataset. Quantified GBM and PFP width values obtained from both ground 207 

truth labels and TEAMKidney predictions were compared across mouse, rat, and human samples 208 

at the individual image level. No statistically significant differences were observed between the 209 

ground truth and TEAMKidney measurements for either GBM or PFP width across all three 210 

species (Fig. 2F), demonstrating strong concordance and reliability of the proposed framework. 211 

These results indicate that our method provides highly reliable GBM and PFP width measurements 212 

across diverse datasets. 213

changes reflect disease progression and serve as indicators of treatment efficacy during drug 217

in the glomerular filtration barrier, we applied our automated method to measure two well-219 

established mouse models and a rat model of glomerular diseases (Fig. 3A, B). In the mouse 220 

models, we used Col4a3 KO, an animal model of Alport syndrome 48 and ILK cKO mice, an animal 221 

model of focal segmental glomerulosclerosis (FSGS), to represent two distinct mechanisms of 222

protein, which is required for podocyte adhesion to the GBM (Fig. 3A, C). Consistent with these 226 

pathological features, our automated analysis revealed significantly increased GBM widths in 227 

Col4a3 KO mice compared with wild-type (WT) controls. Similarly, PFP widths were markedly 228 

widened for the ILK cKO mouse model. These quantitative differences align with the expected 229 

ultrastructural alterations associated with abnormal GBM of the Alport mouse model and podocyte 230 

injury in the ILK cKO FSGS mouse model (Fig. 3C, D). 231 

We next examined the rat PHN model of immune-mediated podocyte injury 37 . In this 232 

model, rats were injected with anti-Fx1a antibody to induce the formation of immune complexes 233 

that deposit beneath podocytes and trigger complement-mediated podocyte injury, leading to 234 

progressive PFP effacement and GBM thickening (Fig. 3B, E). Automated measurements 235 

faithfully captured these ultrastructural changes: compared with WT animals, PHN rats showed a 236 

striking increase in GBM width (Fig. 3F). These differences are consistent with classical TEM-237 

based characterizations of PHN, an animal model of human membranous nephropathy, confirming 238

Identifying Ultrastructural Remodeling in Human Kidney Diseases 242 

TEM-based ultrastructural quantification is commonly used as diagnostic evidence in clinical renal 243 

pathology practice. To investigate the translational utility of our framework in human kidney 244 

disease states, we analyzed TEM images from patients with DKD and Fabry disease (Fig. 4A). 245 

DKD is characterized by hyperglycemia-induced hyperfiltration, GBM thickening, diffuse 246 

mesangial matrix expansion, leading to podocyte stress and progressive glomerular injury, PFP 247 

effacement, glomerular hypertrophy, and eventually podocyte loss and severe nodular 248 

glomerulosclerosis 49-51 . Fabry disease results from GLA mutations that cause accumulation of 249 

globotriaosylceramide (Gb3) in podocytes and subsequent structural damage, PFP effacement, 250 

podocyte detachment, and global or segmental glomerulosclerosis 52, 53 (Fig. 4A). Representative 251 

TEM images predicted by our TEAMKidney model demonstrate marked distinct pathological 252 

features in both disease cohorts compared with normal controls (Fig. 4B). 253 

Quantitative measurements revealed disease-specific ultrastructural alterations (Fig. 4C). 254 

In DKD patients, GBM thickness was significantly increased. Notably, the mean PFP width in 255 

DKD showed only a slight increase compared with normal samples. The relatively preserved PFP 256 

width in our DKD cohort warrants further investigation, as it may reflect the disease stage of the 257 

included patients or capture an early phase where GBM pathology precedes overt podocyte 258 

architectural disruption. Longitudinal studies correlating PFP measurements with clinical disease 259 

staging would help clarify the temporal evolution of these ultrastructural changes 39, 54 . In contrast, 260

Agreement of TEAMKidney and Manual Measurements Across Species 314 

Manually tracing and labeling each ultrastructural component is not feasible in practice. Instead, 315 

pathologists and biomedical scientists typically quantify ultrastructure by measuring multiple 316 

regions within an image to obtain representative values (see Methods). To assess discrepancies, 317 

we compared TEAMKidney measurements with manual measurements across normal, DKD, and 318 

Fabry disease (male and female) from human cohorts (Fig. 6) and animal models (Supplementary 319 

Fig. 5). For GBM widths, TEAMKidney quantification showed no significant differences from 320 

manual measurements in all groups (Fig. 6A, Supplementary Fig. 5), confirming that the 321 

framework preserves accuracy for this ultrastructural feature. Similarly, no significant differences 322 

were detected in PFP counts between TEAMKidney and manual counting across all groups (Fig. 323 

6B, Supplementary Fig. 5), indicating that the framework maintains biological fidelity in 324 

enumerating PFP. 325 

We observed a significant discrepancy in PFP width measurements between the two 326 

methods, with mean values from TEAMKidney consistently lower than those obtained by manual 327 

measurement. This difference likely arises from two factors. First, due to the irregular shape of 328 

PFPs, in the manual approach, PFP width is not measured directly but instead estimated by dividing 329

the GBM length by the PFP count. GBM length is often overestimated, particularly in disease 330 

models with pronounced morphological alterations. Second, manual measurements include the slit 331 

diaphragm as part of the PFP, which inflates the estimated PFP width. In contrast, the 332 

TEAMKidney measurement strategy segments individual PFPs and slit diaphragm separately; 333 

therefore, the slit diaphragm is excluded from the PFP measurement, resulting in higher accuracy 334 

compared with the manual method (Fig. 6C, Supplementary Fig. 5). 335 

Together, these results demonstrate strong concordance between TEAMKidney and 336 

manual measurements, establishing the framework as a reliable substitute for labor-intensive 337 

tracing. By accurately replicating expert-level quantification across both healthy and diseased 338 

human samples, the method enhances reproducibility and offers a scalable solution for 339 

ultrastructural kidney assessment. 340

Discussion 366 

In recent years, a global shortage of pathologists has been widely reported 59, 60 , affecting even large 367 

medical centers and resource-rich regions. The preparation and interpretation of tissue samples for 368 

TEM imaging require additional specialized training, particularly for renal pathologists, further 369 

exacerbating this workforce constraint. To address the urgent needs in nephrology research and 370 

clinical pathology, our team of experienced renal pathologists, nephrologists, biologists, and 371 

computational scientists designed and implemented TEAMKidney. Precise measurement of the 372 

GBM and PFP provides a much-needed solution to address labor-intensive and often subjective 373 

assessments of glomerular diseases. GBM and PFP are not only essential ultrastructure for 374 

diagnosing and monitoring chronic kidney diseases, but they also represent two distinct classes of 375 

TEM features. GBM is characterized by its continuous structure with a relatively regular shape 376 

and ambiguous boundaries throughout the image. In contrast, PFP consists of small, isolated 377 

structures with clear boundaries and irregular shapes. Accurate segmentation and quantification of 378 

these structures demonstrate the capability of the framework to extend beyond nephrology and 379 

renal pathology to general TEM image analysis across other fields. 380 

High-quality labeled data is critical for training deep learning models, but manual 381 

annotation of TEM images is extremely time-consuming and requires specialized pathological 382 

expertise. To overcome this challenge, we adopted semi-automated pre-labeling strategies to 383 

accelerate the annotation workflow and implemented a multi-stage quality control process, 384 

ensuring that each label was independently reviewed and refined. These steps significantly reduced 385 

inter-operator variability and improved the overall reliability of the training data. Combined with 386 

a self-training strategy, our framework achieves high accuracy with only a few hundred finely 387 

annotated images, making it feasible to adapt to other tasks efficiently. 388

Previous TEM analysis methods have primarily focused on segmenting a single 389

on U-Net based or semi-automated approaches (Table 2), which reduce the need for large amounts 391 

of training data but are well known for their limited generalizability to diverse morphologies. 392 

Because these methods typically use data from a single source and a single species, their practical 393 

applicability in animal disease modeling and clinical settings is further constrained. In addition to 394 

collecting and generating diverse datasets across multiple sources, species, and disease states, our 395 

specifically designed panoptic segmentation model offers two key advantages over directly 396 

applying generic computer vision models to this task. First, semantic segmentation provides 397 

spatially consistent, region-level guidance, reducing noisy or fragmented instance predictions 398 

during pseudo-labeling. Second, the trained HRNet model serves as the backbone for the panoptic 399 

segmentation network, enabling direct transfer and reuse of feature representations learned during 400 

semantic segmentation. This strategy not only accelerates model convergence but also improves 401 

feature consistency between semantic and panoptic tasks, thereby enhancing boundary precision 402 

and instance recognition. Overall, this hierarchical training strategy achieves greater stability and 403 

accuracy, particularly for densely packed ultrastructural components such as podocyte foot 404 

processes. 405 

TEM images inherently exhibit substantial variation in magnification and spatial resolution 406 

across different species, disease models, and experimental setups. Such differences often lead to 407 

inconsistencies in the visual scale and morphological representation of ultrastructural components, 408 

posing a major challenge for deep learning models trained on limited magnification ranges. To 409 

address this issue, we employed a series of strategies at multiple stages to enhance the model’s 410 

robustness to magnification variability. First, we collected TEM samples acquired at different 411

by mean-std normalization and tiled into [size, e.g., 1024×1024, 2624*2624] with 50% overlap. 657 

Scale bars and metadata were parsed to record the pixel-to-nm factor (e.g., 4.122 nm/pixel for 658 

mice, 3.88 nm/pixel for rats) used for unit conversion. To avoid leakage, splits were patient/animal-659 

level stratified into train/validate with an 80/20 ratio while preserving class balance per species 660 

and disease model. All preprocessing scripts are provided (see Code availability). 661 

Hybrid Data Preparation . To improve the model’s adaptability across images of different 662 

resolutions, we adopted a stage-wise data preparation and training strategy. High-resolution TEM 663 

images were first used to pretrain the backbone network, enabling the model to learn fine-grained 664 

structural features of GBM and PFP. Subsequently, lower resolution images were used for fine-665 

tuning to encourage scale-invariant feature learning and improve cross-magnification 666 

generalization. During both training stages, various data augmentation techniques were applied, 667 

including random scaling, random cropping, horizontal and vertical flipping, and random 668 

adjustments of brightness and contrast. These augmentations helped prevent overfitting and further 669 

enhanced the model’s robustness to variations in image scale and quality. 670

Glom2Mask: TEM Image Panoptic Segmentation Model Architecture 683 

Our proposed Glom2Mask model is designed based on the Mask2Former panoptic segmentation 684 

framework, enabling comprehensive segmentation of GBM and PFP in TEM images. Given the 685 

distinct structural characteristics of these components, we categorize GBM as a "stuff" class for 686 

semantic segmentation and PFP as a "things" class for instance segmentation. This formulation 687 

ensures precise differentiation between the continuous structure of the GBM and the discrete PFP 688 

instances within the glomerular filtration barrier. 689 

To enhance segmentation accuracy, we integrate HRNet as the backbone for high-690 

resolution feature extraction. HRNet is well-suited for this task due to its ability to preserve spatial 691 

details throughout the network, which is essential for capturing fine structural differences in TEM 692 

images. Unlike traditional backbones that downsample feature maps at early stages, HRNet 693

It is made The copyright holder for this preprint (which this version posted April 17, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.11.05.686793 doi: bioRxiv preprint

maintains high-resolution representations, allowing for better delineation of GBM boundaries and 694 

accurate identification of individual PFP structures. 695 

Our model follows a transformer-based approach, where multi-scale features extracted by 696 

HRNet are processed through multi-head attention mechanisms to generate high-quality 697 

segmentation masks. The network outputs a set of learned queries, each corresponding to a GBM 698 

region or a PFP instance, enabling robust panoptic segmentation across diverse TEM datasets, 699 

including mouse, rat, and human kidney samples. 700 

By leveraging HRNet for high-resolution feature extraction and Mask2Former for unified 701 

panoptic segmentation, our model effectively segments GBM and PFP structures, providing the 702 

necessary groundwork for precise automated width measurement in kidney disease analysis. 703

Model Training for the Deep Learning Framework 705 

Our training process consists of two stages: a self-training-based semantic segmentation stage and 706 

a panoptic segmentation stage using Glom2Mask. The first stage aims to generate additional 707 

pseudo-labeled data while simultaneously learning a high-resolution backbone network. In the 708 

second stage, we utilize the HRNet trained in the first stage as the backbone of a modified 709 

Mask2Former model for panoptic segmentation, thereby improving both training efficiency and 710 

segmentation accuracy. 711 

Self-Training for Semantic Segmentation. In the first stage, we employ a self-supervised learning 712 

approach to HRNet-based semantic segmentation. 713 

For cases with a limited number of annotated images (e.g., 19, 48, or 64 labeled samples), 714 

we employed a self-training strategy to improve segmentation performance. Self-training is a 715 

semi-supervised learning technique that utilizes both labeled and unlabeled data. Initially, the 716

GBM Width Measurement. The second stage of our proposed method consists of a Python image 772 

processing algorithm that takes input of the GBM and PFP segmentation tiles predicted by the 773 

deep learning model to measure the mean GBM width and PFP width. We will describe the GBM 774 

width measurement first here. The mean GBM width for each tile is then calculated as its total 775 

GBM area divided by its total GBM central length according to the following equation 776 

𝐺𝐵𝑀 𝑤𝑖𝑑𝑡 ℎ = 𝐺𝐵𝑀 𝑎𝑟𝑒𝑎 

𝐺𝐵𝑀 𝑐𝑒𝑛𝑡𝑟𝑎𝑙 𝑙𝑒𝑛𝑔𝑡 ℎ

777 

Where the area of the GBM is straightforward to compute, it involves directly counting the 778 

number of pixels in a section of the GBM. The process for calculating the length of the GBM is as 779 

follows: First, we obtain the skeleton of the GBM using the skeletonize method with the Python 780 

package scikit-image. The general skeleton obtained through the skeletonize method, in addition 781 

to the central length of the GBM, also includes many small branching points (Fig. 1J). To eliminate 782 

these branching points, we construct an undirected cyclic graph of the GBM skeleton using the 783

Python package NetworkX. Here, there are two scenarios: if the GBM forms a loop structure, we 784 

iteratively prune nodes with an in-degree of one from the undirected cyclic graph of the GBM 785 

skeleton until no nodes with an in-degree of one remain. We then calculate the perimeter of the 786 

resulting graph to determine the central length of the GBM; if the GBM forms a strip-like structure, 787 

we calculate the shortest distance between any two endpoints and identify the two endpoints with 788 

the greatest distance as the central length of the GBM. Lastly, the specimen mean GBM width was 789 

converted from pixels to distance with a conversion factor derived from the TEM image scale bar, 790 

for example, 3.88 nm/pixel for rat model data. 791 

PFP Width Measurement. Following this, the PFP width calculation is more direct. Each PFP 792 

instance is represented by a unique integer starting from 1, with the background assigned a value 793 

of 0. 794 

Next, we use morphological operations in OpenCV to dilate the GBM, enabling us to obtain 795 

its contour. In this processed image, the background remains coded as 0, and the contour positions 796 

are coded as 1. 797 

Finally, we calculate the width of each PFP by multiplying the PFP instance matrix by the 798 

matrix of the dilated GBM contours. This operation yields the width by computing the pixel length 799 

of PFPs. Similarly, the specimen PFP widths were converted from pixels to distance with a 800 

conversion factor derived from the TEM image scale bar, for example, 3.88 nm/pixel for rat model 801 

data. 802
