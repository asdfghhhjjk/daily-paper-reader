# Abstract 22 

Pancreatic cancer progression is orchestrated by dynamic shifts in immune and stromal 23 

cellular ecosystems, yet the temporal and spatial principles governing these transitions 24 

remain poorly understood. Here, we present an agentic computational pathology 25 

framework that leverages large language models to orchestrate modular biomarker 26 

inference and spatiotemporal reasoning directly from routine H&E histology. Our 27 

approach, ROSIE (RObust in Silico Immunoflu orescence), combines deep -learning -28 

based multiplex inference with LLM -driven agent logic that emulates pathologist -level 29 

reasoning, enabling transparent and reproducible analysis of complex tissue 30 

microarchitectures. 31 

Applying this workflow to pancreatic in traepithelial neoplasia (PanIN) progression in KSC 32 

transgenic mice (n=24, ages 4 –12 weeks), we generated 10.44 million single -cell profiles 33 

and identified a temporally ordered immune trajectory comprising three spatially distinct 34 

immune -stromal states: (1) early immune -surveillance niche : sharply bounded window of 35 

adaptive immune activation and antigen -presentation enrichment; (2) transitional mixed 36 

state : declining lymphoid activity, emerging exhaustion programs, and early 37 

EMT/angiogenesis signals; (3) str omal -dominant terminal state : fibroblast expansion, 38 

vascular remodeling, and immune silence. 39

# Introduction 45 

Pancreatic cancer remains one of the most lethal malignancies, driven in part by the clinically 46 

silent evolution of precursor lesions that undergo extensive immune and stromal reprogramming 47 

long before overt malignant transfo rmation 1–4. Although early inflammatory activation and 48 

prog ressive fibroinflammatory expansion have been described, the temporal and spatial 49 

mechanisms that coordinate this transition remain incompletely defined and are an active area of 50 

investigation 5–10 .51 

High -dimensional assays such as single -cell sequencing and multiplexed imaging have revealed 52 

key aspects of tumor –immune interactions, but their cost, throughput limitations, and tissue 53 

requirements make them impractica l for cohort -scale or longitudinal studies 11 –18 . In contrast, 54 

routine H&E slides are abundant, clinically embedded, and capture the full tissue architecture, yet 55 

their molecular content remains largely untappe d19,20 .56 

Recent advances in computational pathology demonstrate that deep learning can extract latent 57 

molec ular, architectural, and spatial information directly from H&E images 14,21 –23 . Yet despite 58 

these capabilities, existing approaches lack an integrated framework capable of reconstructing 59 

cell -state transitions, pathway remodeling, and niche -level organization across developmental 60 

time 24 –27 . No current computational pathology method models temporal progression, and although 61 

evolutionary and spatial -omics studies underscore the importance of temporally resolved tissue 62 

analysis, they do not provide H&E -based solutions 7,12 .63 

At the same time, most computational pathology pipelines remain single -task systems, optimized 64 

for isolated objectives such as weakly supervised WSI classification, mutation prediction, or 65 

spatial feature extractio n1,19,25,28 . These models l ack the modularity, transparency, and cross -stage 66 

coordination required for large -scale, multi -timepoint tissue analysis. As a result, current 67 

workflows cannot unify biomarker inference, spatial organization, and temporal dynamics within 68 

a single reproduci ble framework 7,12,29 . Parallel develop ments in large language models (LLMs) 69 

highlight the potential of agent -like orchestration for complex biomedical tasks, enabling dynamic 70 

code generation, modular execution, and multi -step reasoning 30 –32 , yet such capabilities hav e not 71 

been systematically applied to computational pathology. Parallel work in the LLM ecosystem 72 

shows that agentic toolchains and state -driven orchestration frameworks are still actively evolving 73 

and have not yet been systematically applied to computation al pathology 33,34 . Together, these gaps 74 

motivate the need for a flexible, node -based architecture capable of coordinating heterogeneous 75 

analytical modules while maintaining interpretability and scalability across multi -timepoint 76 

datasets. 77 

Here, we introduce a n LLM -orchestrated computational pathology framework that int egrates 78 

multi -timepoint H&E slides into high -resolution temporal and spatial maps of pancreatic cancer 79

orchestrated framework . Nod e 1 partitions WSIs into standardized 512×512 patches 137 

using adaptive grid tiling and tissue detection. Node 2.1 applies ROSIE to infer 138 

50 -channel protein feature maps and compute spatial coordinates for each detected cell. 139 

Node 2.2 performs HED -based nucle us enhancement, watershed segmentation, and 140 

region -property extraction to generate per -cell morphological descriptors. Together, these 141 

nodes produced a dataset of approximately 10.44 million cells from KSC mice aged 4 –12 142 

weeks, providing a dense and spatia lly resolved representation of the evolving tumor 143 

microenvironment. Node 3 integrates these annotations to construct a Timed Petri Net 144 

model of cell -state transitions, while Node 4 performs spatial niche analysis by combining 145 

cell identities with DBSCAN cl ustering and pathway scoring. This modular architecture 146 

enables end -to -end analysis from raw histology to temporal and spatial modeling. 147

3.1 A coordinated shift in immune -state dynamics during pancreatic cancer 319 

progression 320 

Our findings reveal that immune -state transitions during pancreatic cancer development 321 

follow a highl y coordinated and temporally ordered trajectory. Early lesions exhibit a 322

distinctly immune -engaged microenvironment, characterized by abundant lymphoid 323 

populations —including CD4 ⁺ and CD8 ⁺ T cells, NK cells, B cells, and dendritic cells —and 324 

strong antigen -presentation and activation signatures. This early immune activation 325 

aligns with the sentinel inflammatory responses described in the earlier longitudinal 326 

study 35 , which identified TSPO ⁺ tumor -associated macrophages as key initiators of early 327 

lesion -associated inflammation. However, our single -cell and pathway -level analyses 328 

extend these observations by demonstrating that early immune engagement is not diffuse 329 

but spatially organized into compact, immune -enriched niches that cluster around 330 

emerging epithelial structures. 331 

As lesions progress, this coordinated immune architecture gradually deteriorates. 332 

Ly mphoid signatures decline, exhaustion programs rise, and the microenvironment 333 

becomes increasingly dominated by CD68 ⁺ myeloid cells, monocyte/MDSC -like 334 

populations, and fibroblast -rich stromal compartments. These shifts reflect a broad 335 

reprogramming of imm une function rather than simple changes in cell abundance. The 336 

emergence of suppressive and remodeling states mirrors the global pathway transitions 337 

we observe, suggesting that immune decline and stromal activation are tightly coupled 338 

processes that unfold in parallel. 339 

By late stages, the microenvironment transitions into a stromal -dominant, immune -silent 340 

state characterized by strong EMT, angiogenesis, fibrosis, and proliferative activity. 341 

Immune niches become sparse and functionally quiescent, indicating a collapse of 342 

coordinated immune surveillance. Together, these findings position immune -state 343 

reprogramming as a central axis of pancreatic cancer evolution and highlight how immune 344 

activation, suppression, and stromal expansion are embedded within evolvin g spatial 345 

microenvironments that shape the trajectory from precursor lesions to invasive disease. 346 

3.2 Early immune engagement and transient activation of anti -tumor programs 347 

Early pancreatic lesions occupy a brief but clearly defined window of heightened i mmune 348 

engagement, during which multiple lymphoid and innate compartments mount 349 

coordinated responses to emerging epithelial transformation. Biomarker -defined cell 350 

compositions ( Fig ure 3–4) reveal that CD4 ⁺ and CD8 ⁺ T cells, NK cells, B cells, and 351 

dendritic cells dominate the microenvironment at 4 –6 weeks, forming the principal 352 

immune compartments during the earliest stages of disease. Dynamic change -rate 353 

analysis further highlights transient surges in CD8 ⁺ T cells, dendritic cells, and neutrophils, 354 

suggesti ng rapid recruitment and activation of anti -tumor programs as lesions first arise. 355 

Pathway -level signatures reinforce this interpretation: antigen -presentation, 356 

interferon -associated signaling, and immune -activation programs are strongly induced 357 

during thi s early interval, consistent with robust immune surveillance. Spatially, these 358 

molecular features are mirrored by the organization of immune -enriched niches that 359 

cluster tightly around nascent ductal structures ( Fig ure 7), indicating that early immune 360 

reco gnition is not only transcriptionally active but anatomically focused. 361

3.5 Biological and clinical implications for interception and therapeutic 419 

timing 420 

The immune -state transitions uncovered by our framework have direct implications for 421 

understanding pancreatic cancer evolution and for identifying windows of clinical 422 

vulnerability. The early presence of biomarker -defined lymphoid engag ement and 423 

antigen -presentation activity suggests that precursor lesions are initially embedded within 424 

an immune -active microenvironment capable of recognizing and responding to epithelial 425 

transformation. This observation is consistent with previously repor ted biomarker profiles 426 

in early pancreatic lesions, where TSPO ⁺ tumor -associated macrophages were identified 427 

as key initiators of sentinel inflammation 35 . Our results extend this concept by showing 428 

that early immune activation is not only detectable but quantifiable at single -cell and 429 

pathway resolution, and that it is spatially organized within immune -active niches that 430 

closely track emerging ductal abnormalities. 431 

As lesions progress, the coordinated shift toward mixed and ultimately 432 

suppressive/stromal microenvironments provides a mechanistic basis for the loss of 433 

immune surveillance and the emergence of a tumor -permissive niche. The expansion of 434 

CD68 ⁺ myeloid cells, monocyte/MDSC -like populations, and fibroblast -rich stroma —435 

together with rising EMT, angiogenesis, and fibrosis pathways —suggests that therapeutic 436 

strategies targeting myeloid suppression, stromal remodeling, or fibroblast –immune 437 

crosstalk may be most effective during this transition phase. Conversely, the early 438 

immune -active window identified in Figure 3 –4 and reinforced by niche -level analysis in 439 

Figure 7 may represent an opportunity for immune -modulating interventions aimed at 440 

reinforcing endogenous surveillance before suppressive programs become entrenched. 441

3. 7 Limitations and future directions 487 

While our framework provides a high -resolution view of immune -state transitions during 488 

pancreatic cancer progression, several limitations warrant consideration. First, ROSIE’s 489 

biomarker inferences are derived from dee p-learning models trained on multiplex 490 

immunofluorescence data; although major lineages are robustly predicted, rare or 491 

transitional phenotypes may require orthogonal validation using targeted antibody panels 492 

or spatial transcriptomics. Second, our analysi s is based on cross -sectional sampling of 493 

lesions at different developmental stages rather than longitudinal tracking of individual 494 

lesions, limiting our ability to resolve clonal evolution or stochastic immune fluctuations 495 

over time. Third, although our spatial niche analysis captures the emergence of 496 

immune -active → mixed → suppressive/stromal microenvironments, the causal 497 

interactions among epithelial, myeloid, and fibroblast compartments remain to be 498 

experimentally dissected. 499 

Future work integrating lon gitudinal imaging, perturbation studies, and multimodal spatial 500 

assays will help refine the mechanistic basis of immune -state transitions and clarify how 501 

specific cellular interactions drive the shift toward suppressive microenvironments. In 502 

parallel, expa nding ROSIE to incorporate global context features from foundation models 503 

may further improve the robustness of biomarker inference across diverse staining 504 

conditions. Ultimately, translating these computational insights into clinical workflows will 505 

requir e prospective validation to determine how spatially resolved immune states can 506 

guide early detection, risk stratification, and the timing of interception strategies. 507

4.2 LLM -orchestrated modu lar computational pathology pipeline 525 

A modular, multi -node computational pathology pipeline was developed, in which Large 526 

Language Models (LLMs) orchestrate task execution under human supervision. The 527 

pipeline was implemented using LangGraph (v1.1.2) 34 —a state -graph orchestration 528 

framework that supersedes linear chain -based approaches by enabling cyclic execution, 529 

conditional branching, and persistent state management across nodes —which manages 530 

node transitions, data -flow dependencies, and execution state across the multi -node 531 

workflow. 532 

This architecture supports an asynchronous development cycle in which nodes can be 533 

authored independently, executed chronologically, or dynamically reconfigured to 534 

optimize computational flow. Each node is defined through a semantically explicit task 535 

template that encodes the user's intent, ensuring that the operational logic, data 536 

dependencies, and expected outputs are unambiguously specified. For instance, 537 

whereas the initial pipeline follows a linear path, the structural i ndependence of Node 2.1 538 

(morphological feature extraction) and Node 2.2 (biomarker inference) allows for 539 

parallelized execution, significantly reducing total computational latency for large -scale 540 

cohorts. 541 

The pipeline comprises the following explicitly def ined nodes: 542  

> •

Node 1 (Adaptive Tiling): Performs automated segmentation of SVS files into 543 

standardized 512 × 512 patches, employing intensity -based heuristics and 544 

tissue -mask refinement to exclude non -informative background regions. 545  

> •

Node 2.1 (Spatial Feature Extraction): Computes per -cell coordinates via a 546 

hybrid pipeline of color deconvolution and watershed segmentation, ensuring 547 

robust nuclear delineation across diverse tissue contexts. 548  

> •

Node 2.2 (Biomarker Inference): Applies ROSIE models to assign cell -typ e549 

labels and molecular signatures, generating a high -dimensional representation of 550 

the tissue microenvironment 23 .551  

> •

Node 3 (Tempora l Dynamics): Aggregates cellular outputs across 552 

developmental stages to construct transition models, inferring immune -state 553 

trajectories over a 4 –12 -week continuum. 554  

> •

Node 4 (Niche Construction): Integrates pathway scores with spatial clustering 555 

to map molec ular programs onto spatially resolved microenvironments. 556

4. 4 Human -in -the -loop model selection and execution oversight 568 

To ensure peak inferential acc uracy, the workflow incorporates a human -in -the -loop 569 

(HITL) model selection layer. During the development phase, researchers can deploy 570 

multiple Large Language Model (LLM) variants against the same task template. Each 571 

node's execution logic is generated on -demand by leveraging the LLM's code -generation 572 

capabilities: given a standardized task template, the LLM synthesizes executable Python 573 

modules that implement the required computational step. Before execution, these auto -574 

generated modules can be inspected, modified, or validated by the researcher to ensure 575 

alignment with analytical intent. 576 

Through the framework's interactive dashboard, intermediate outputs from these modules 577 

are monitored in real time, enabling deterministic comparison of model behavior and 578 

empirical selection of the optimal LLM before proceeding to downstream nodes. This 579 

modular flexibility —combined with version -locked execution, explicit data -dependency 580 

tracking, and human -validated code synthesis —ensures that the transition from 581 

explorato ry image analysis to high -order niche construction is auditable, scalable, and 582 

methodologically rigorous. 583 

LLM model specification and reproducibility. Code generation across all pipeline nodes 584 

was performed using two LLM backends evaluated in parallel: Cla ude 3.5 Sonnet 585 

(Anthropic, version 20241022) and Llama 2 (7B) (Meta AI, quantized GGUF variant 586 

served locally via Ollama). Model selection was based on qualitative assessment of 587 

generated code across representative data subsets, evaluating syntactic correc tness, 588 

adherence to task template specifications, and alignment of outputs with expected 589 

biological results. Claude 3.5 Sonnet demonstrated consistently higher code quality and 590 

was selected as the primary model for all production pipeline runs. Llama 2 (7B ) was 591 

retained as a lightweight, locally deployable alternative for nodes requiring rapid iteration. 592 

All LLM inference was performed with temperature set to 0 to ensure deterministic code 593 

output. Task templates used as LLM inputs followed a structured prom pt format encoding 594 

node -specific objectives, input/output specifications, and execution constraints; 595 

representative examples are available from the corresponding author upon reasonable 596

4. 8 Spatial niche analysis 651 

Spatial microenvironments were inferred by integrating ROSIE -derived pathway scores 652 

with per -cell spatial coordinates obtained from the seg mentation pipeline. For each 653 

whole -slide image, DBSCAN was applied to the set of detected cell centroids to group 654 

spatially contiguous cells into preliminary clusters. These clusters were subsequently 655 

refined using K -means merging to stabilize niche bounda ries and ensure consistent 656 

granularity across slides. Each resulting niche was assigned a molecular signature based 657 

on aggregated pathway scores and cell -type composition, enabling classification into 658 

immune -active, mixed immune –stromal, or suppressive/str omal categories. 659 

Niche -level maps were generated for all eight SVS slides, revealing a consistent spatial 660 

trajectory across disease progression. Early lesions were dominated by compact 661 

immune -active niches enriched for antigen -presentation and immune -activ ation programs. 662 

Intermediate stages exhibited mixed niches characterized by destabilized lymphoid 663 

programs and emerging myeloid and fibroblast activity. Late lesions were enriched for 664 

stromal -dominant niches displaying elevated EMT, angiogenesis, and fibro sis signatures. 665 

These spatial patterns provided the structural basis for the immune -active → mixed → 666 

suppressive/stromal transitions described in the Results and Discussion, demonstrating 667

Conclusion 690 

This study sho ws that routine H&E slides, when processed through a LLM -orchestrated 691 

computational pathology framework , can yield a high -resolution, 10 -million -cell view of 692 

pancreatic cancer evolution. By integrating ROSIE -based biomarker inference with 693 

temporal modeling and spatial niche analysis, we uncover a tightly ordered trajectory: 694 

early immune -active niches collapse into mixed states and ultimately into 695 

stromal -dominant, suppressive microenvironments. These transitions reflect a696 

coordinated loss of adaptive survei llance and the rise of myeloid and fibroblast programs 697 

that shape malignant progression. Our results establish a scalable, interpretable 698 

framework for extracting molecular and spatial insights directly from standard histology, 699 

opening new opportunities for large -cohort studies and for identifying actionable windows 700 

for early interception. 701
