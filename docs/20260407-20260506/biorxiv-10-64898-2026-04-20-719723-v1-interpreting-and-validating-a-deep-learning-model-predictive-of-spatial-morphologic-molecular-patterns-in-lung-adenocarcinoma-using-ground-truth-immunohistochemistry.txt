Abstract 52 Lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD), the most common subtype of non–small cell lung cancer, 53 exhibits profound histological and molecular heterogeneity. While genomic profiling has 54 identified key oncogenic drivers and immune signatures, its use is limited by cost, technical 55 demands and tissue availability. In addition, spatial transcriptomics provides spatially resolved 56 molecular insights but remains challenging and time-consuming. To address this gap, we 57 developed XpressO-Lung, an explanatory deep learning model that predicts gene expression 58 heterogeneity spatially in tumor and its microenvironment on hematoxylin and eosin based 59 diagnostic (Dx) whole-slide images (WSIs) by learning associations between tissue morphology 60 and the corresponding bulk-transcriptomic data. Utilizing 200 LUAD cases from The Cancer 61 Genome Atlas, XpressO-Lung predicted spatial expression patterns of NAPSA, TP53I3, CD8A, 62 TTF1, KRT7, CDKN2A, FOXO1, KEAP1, RB1 and TP53 on Dx-WSIs with AUCs ranging 63 from 0.64 to 0.92. The predicted spatial gene expression patterns aligned with the known 64 morphologic interactions of the tumor and its microenvironment, capturing biological events 65 directly on Dx-WSIs. These spatio-morpho-molecular associations were further validated using 66 immunohistochemistry on an external set of clinical samples at Dartmouth Health, demonstrating 67 concordance between model-predicted spatial patterns and observed histomorphologic features. 68 By coupling predictive performance with spatial interpretability of gene expression on Dx-WSIs, 69 the XpressO-Lung model bridges histopathology and bulk-transcriptomics, enabling explainable 70

Introduction 88 Lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) is the most prevalent histological subtype of non-small cell lung 89 cancer (NSCLC), accounting for approximately 40% of all lung cancer diagnoses 1,2 . Originating 90 from glandular epithelial cells in the distal airways, LUAD is characterized by considerable 91 histological and molecular heterogeneity, often presenting with distinct architectural growth 92 patterns such as lepidic, acinar, papillary, micropapillary, complex glandular, and solid subtypes. 93 3. This heterogeneity extends beyond the malignant epithelial cells to include the surrounding 94 tumor microenvironment (TME), which comprises stromal, immune, and vascular elements that 95 significantly influence tumor progression and treatment response. Genomic diversity is also 96 prominent, with frequent oncogenic driver mutations in genes such as EGFR, KRAS, ALK, 97 

STK11, and TP53 4–6 . Current guidelines, including the WHO and CAP lung cancer biomarker 98 reporting protocols, recommend reflex testing for ALK and ROS1 rearrangements, while the 99 NCCN and IASLC-CAP-AMP guidelines further advocate broad molecular profiling to include 100 

EGFR , KRAS , BRAF , MET , RET , NTRK , and PD-L1 as part of the routine diagnostic workup 101 7.These alterations inform therapeutic strategies, particularly the use of tyrosine kinase inhibitors 102 and immune checkpoint inhibitors, both of which have substantially improved outcomes for 103 subsets of LUAD patients 8. Nevertheless, a substantial subset of LUAD patients show 104 unpredictable responses to therapy and widely varying prognoses, underscoring the need for 105 more comprehensive molecular characterization. 106 A major obstacle to advancing precision medicine in LUAD is the high degree of inter- and 107 intra-tumoral heterogeneity, which includes variation in both cancer cell states and TME 108 composition. Even among patients with identical histological subtypes or driver mutations, 109 differences in gene expression programs can significantly influence tumor progression, immune 110

Addressing this complexity requires an expanded 111 biomarker landscape and scalable tools capable of capturing transcriptomic diversity across 112 LUAD cohorts. 113 Spatial transcriptomics technologies have emerged as powerful tools for mapping gene 114 expression across tumor sections, enabling in situ profiling of both cancer and non-cancer 115 compartments with high resolution 10 . However, their technical demands, high costs, and limited 116 clinical availability pose significant barriers to widespread implementation. Moreover, these 117 methods often require specialized protocols and non-standard tissue preservation techniques, 118 limiting their applicability to large-scale or retrospective studies 11 . As a result, there is growing 119 interest in computational approaches that can infer molecular features from more accessible 120 sources, such as routine histopathology slides. 121 Hematoxylin and eosin (H&E)-stained diagnostic (Dx) whole slide images (WSIs) offer a rich 122 but underutilized source of phenotypic data. Recent developments in computational pathology 123 have leveraged deep learning (DL) models to extract biologically relevant features from WSIs, 124 demonstrating the ability to classify LUAD subtypes 12 , predict driver mutations 13 , infer tumor 125 microenvironment composition 14 , and even approximate transcriptomic signatures 15 . These 126 models, often based on convolutional neural networks (CNNs) or attention-based architectures, 127 have shown strong performance across several predictive tasks. However, a persistent challenge 128 in these approaches is their lack of interpretability 16 .129 Most DL models in computational pathology function as “black boxes,” providing slide-level 130 predictions without clear explanations of how these decisions are made or which tissue regions 131 contribute most to the outcome 17,18 . Materials and Methods 169 

Data Collection and Preprocessing 170 Dx-WSIs of LUAD from 200 patients were obtained from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA-171 LUAD) via the Genomic Data Commons (GDC) portal 22 . These WSIs were processed using the 172 segmentation module in XpressO 20 which leverages the CLAM framework 23 to identify tumor-173 enriched regions by assigning attention scores to image patches and selecting the most 174 informative regions for analysis (Figure 1). The WSIs were tiled into non-overlapping 256×256 175 pixel patches at 20× magnification 24 . The resulting patches were passed through the feature 176 extraction module in XpressO 20 , which uses a pretrained Vision Transformer (ViT-L/16) 25 177

These embeddings enabled the downstream classification of gene 180 expression as described in the following sections. 181 

Collection and Processing of RNA-seq Data 182 Corresponding bulk RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) data for the same 200 LUAD patients were 183 downloaded from the TCGA-LUAD portal in the form of fragments per kilobase of transcript per 184 million mapped reads (FPKM). To prepare the dataset for gene expression classification, FPKM 185 values were binarized into “high” and “low” categories using XpressO’s custom binarization 186 script 20 . For each gene, the median expression value across all patients was used as the threshold 187 with samples with expression values greater than or equal to the median were labelled as high-188 expression (“1”), while those below the median were labelled as low-expression (“0”). The 189 interquartile range (IQR) of the expression of 10 genes of interest are displayed in Figure 2. 190 Following binarization, the TCGA-LUAD dataset was split into training, internal-validation 191 (used during training to tune hyperparameters and monitor for overfitting) and held-out 192 independent testing subsets using a predefined XpressO script 20 . Binarization was performed 193 across the full cohort prior to dataset splitting to preserve cohort-wide gene expression 194 variability, enabling the model to learn consistent and robust relationships between 195 histomorphologic features and expression patterns in WSIs. Binary expression labels were 196 assigned at the slide level, allowing each WSI to be categorized as high or low expression for a 197 given gene. These labelled subsets were then used to train and evaluate the deep learning model 198 for gene expression prediction using WSIs. 199

Model Training 200 Gene expression prediction was performed using the classification module in XpressO, which is 201 based on the CLAM-SB architecture 23 (Figure 1). For each gene, a weakly supervised model 202 was trained to classify binarized expression status using the top-ranked (k=10) patch embeddings 203 from each tissue-slide in the training set. Training was conducted using binary cross-entropy 204 loss, with the Adam optimizer (learning rate = 2×10 −4 ) over 200 epochs. The model was trained 205 to weigh instance-level features using attention-based pooling and output a slide-level prediction 206 for each gene’s expression class. 207 

Model Evaluation and Metrics 208 The trained model was evaluated on an held-out independent testing set (i.e. 10% of the total). 209 Evaluation metrics included area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC-ROC), 210 accuracy, precision, recall and F1 scores, along with the 95% confidence intervals for each of 211 them. These metrics were computed separately for each gene (expression) prediction by the 212 model on the independent test set. 213 

Visualization of the Distribution of High and Low Gene Expression on WSIs 214 Gene expression heatmaps were generated for the testing set of WSIs using the XpressO’s 215 heatmap module to visualize attention scores derived from the model. For each slide, patch-level 216 attention scores corresponding to class probabilities (p_0 and p_1) were computed, where p_0 217 represents the predicted class (high or low expression) and p_1 represents the non-predicted class 218 (low or high expression). Discussion 509 In this study, we present XpressO-Lung, an explainable deep learning model that projects gene 510 expression heterogeneity spatially in LUAD tumors and their microenvironment on H&E-stained 511 Dx-WSIs using their corresponding bulk-transcriptomics data. The model was validated and 512 interpreted using immunohistochemistry on an external set of clinical samples at DH and was 513 found to capture histomorphologic patterns predictive of both immune and epithelial gene 514 expression signatures within LUAD tissues. Our results demonstrate that the model not only 515 recapitulates known LUAD biomarkers, i.e., NAPSA , TTF1 , KRT7 , CDKN2A , CD8A , TP53I3 ,516 FOXO1, KEAP1, RB1 and TP53 but also reveals their biologically meaningful spatial expression 517 patterns either in a paired or individual format, mirroring underlying tumor and TME 518 interactions. 519
