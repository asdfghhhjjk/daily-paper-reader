Abstract 13 

Developing a universal representation space for cells which encompasses the tremendous 14 

molecular diversity of cell types within the human body and more generally, across species, 15 

would be transformative for cell biology. Recent work using single-cell transcriptomic ap-16 

proaches to create molecular definitions of cell types in the form of cell atlases has provided 17 

the necessary data for such an endeavor. Here, we present the Universal Cell Embedding 18 

(UCE) foundation model. UCE was trained on a corpus of cell atlas data from human and 19 

other species in a completely self-supervised way without any data annotations. UCE’s mod-20 

eling approach is to create a unified biological latent space that can represent cells across 21 

diverse tissues and species. This universal cell embedding captures important biological vari-22 

ation despite the presence of experimental noise across diverse datasets. An important aspect 23 

of UCE’s universality is that new cells can be mapped to this embedding space with no addi-24

# Introduction 35 

Cells are the fundamental unit of life and biologists have long conceptualized cells as members 36 

of different universal landscapes [1–4]. A notable example of this is the Waddington landscape, 37 

which presents a theoretical framework for the developmental lineages of cells as they transition 38 

from pluripotent stages such as stem cells to more terminally differentiated end points [5]. Broadly, 39 

the field of cell biology has sought to map the range of phenotypes that cells might exhibit, their 40 

interrelationships, and the shifts between these states during development and disease [6–10]. 41 

The substantial growth in the size of single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) datasets 42 

presents a fresh opportunity to revisit these questions. Detailed transcriptomic snapshots of cells 43 

are now widely available from a range of timepoints, tissues, donors, and species [11–13]. These 44 

rich, high-dimensional states are typically distilled into low-dimensional vectors or embeddings to 45 

facilitate computational analysis [14, 15]. However, existing computational approaches struggle to 46 

jointly analyze these diverse datasets. The unified representations they produce are often unable 47 

to extend to new datasets due to species-specific constraints or the presence of dataset-specific 48 

artifacts (or batch effects) which can obscure the underlying biological signal [16, 17]. 49 

Some computational methods for scRNA-seq data have managed to overcome these limita-50 

tions, but at the cost of requiring model tuning for each new dataset, thus rendering the representa-51 

tions non-universal [15,18,19]. As a result, whenever a new experiment is performed and new data 52 

is collected, it requires dedicated, resource-intensive data labeling and model training to perform 53 

even the most standard analyses, such as clustering or annotation. This process is time consuming 54 

and inefficient, and results in sub-optimal analyses based on small, limited and private datasets. 55 

Recent advances in the field of artificial intelligence have enabled general-purpose foun-56

Although recent work has applied foundation model architectures to single cell genomic data, the 62

both inefficient from a learning perspective and often relies on inaccurate biological assumptions. 65 

Here, we present Universal Cell Embedding (UCE), a foundation model for single-cell gene 66 

expression that is designed to address questions in cell and molecular biology. UCE is uniquely 67 

able to generate representations of new single-cell gene expression datasets with no model fine-68 

tuning or retraining while still remaining robust to dataset and batch-specific artifacts. Moreover, 69 

it does so while requiring no cell type annotation and no input dataset preprocessing, such as gene 70 

selection. UCE can be applied to any set of protein-coding genes from any species, even if they 71 

are not homologs of genes seen during training. UCE learns a universal, intrinsically meaningful 72 

representation of cell biology that enables insights extending beyond experimentally observed data. 73 

The representations learned by UCE display an emergent organization of cell types and states 74 

that is consistent with known biology. These cell embeddings can be used to accurately predict 75 

cell types/states without additional model retraining, showing improved performance in dataset 76 

integration against existing atlas-scale integration methods. UCE also enables the mapping of new 77 

data into a universal embedding space, already populated with annotated reference states. This 78

# Results 84 

A biologically-informed foundation model for single cell gene expression. 85 

Integrating single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) datasets is challenging for two primary 86 

reasons: scRNA-seq data does not always contain the same genes, or features, and those features 87 

are plagued by dataset-specific experimental artifacts or batch effects, which means models have to 88 

be built separately for each dataset. UCE overcomes these challenges by abstracting cells as ‘bags 89 

of RNA’ [30]. UCE (Fig. 1a) converts the RNA gene expression of a single cell into an expression-90 

weighted sample of its corresponding genes. Next, UCE represents the sample’s genes by their 91 

protein products, using a protein language model. This allows UCE to meaningfully represent any 92 

protein-coding gene, from any species based on sequence alone, regardless of whether the species 93 

had appeared in the training data. After integrating additional metadata on gene chromosomal 94 

locations, the representation is processed by a large transformer model [31]. UCE can then map 95 

any cell—from any tissue or species—into a shared universal space without further training. 96 

UCE takes as input (1) scRNA-seq count data and (2) the corresponding protein embeddings, 97 

generated by a protein language model, ESM2 [32], for the genes in the data set. The ESM2 protein 98 

language model takes amino acid sequences as an input and produces a numerical representation 99

then tokenized by converting them to the protein embedding representation of the protein that 103

them between special tokens and then sorted by genomic location. A special token representing 105 

the entire cell, the ’CLS’ token, is attached to the beginning of the cell representation [34]. This 106 

combined representation is passed into a transformer neural network. The embedding of a cell is 107 

taken as the embedding of the CLS token at the final layer of the transformer (Fig. 1a). 108 

UCE is trained in a fully self-supervised manner, without using any cell type or dataset-based 109 

annotations. While incorporating cell type labels into the model design or training could seem 110 

advantageous, most datasets lack such annotations. Ultimately, it is preferable for the organization 111 

of cells within UCE to emerge organically, independent of human labeling. 112 

UCE is a 33 layer model consisting of over 650 million parameters. UCE was trained across 113 

more than 300 datasets that are largely collected from the CellXGene corpus [35] consisting of 114 

over 36 million cells, for 40 days across 24 A100 80GB GPUs (Methods, Extended Data Table 115 

2, SI Table 2). We also performed extensive ablation experiments to validate UCE architecture 116 

choices, understand training behavior, and quantify the benefits of ESM2 protein embeddings (SI 117 

Tables 3, 4, 8, 9, 10, Supplementary figures 17, 18). The model’s weights and implementation are 118 

freely available and the model is hosted as an openly available resource for the community. 119

# Discussion 304 

UCE is a single-cell foundation model that is built from the ground up to represent cell biology 305 

across the wide array of single-cell datasets. We envision UCE as an embedding approach that 306 

enables researchers to map any new data, including entire atlases, into an accurate, meaningful and 307 

universal space. The embedding space that emerges from UCE is highly structured and diverse and 308 

aligns cell types across tissues and species. Additionally, these cell types organize themselves in a 309 

pattern that reflects existing biological knowledge. 310 

By building UCE, we enable novel analyses of scRNA-seq data. UCE’s zero-shot embed-311 

dings are an important new capability because they enable an intrinsically meaningful represen-312 

tation that can extend insights beyond the data that have already been observed and annotated 313 

experimentally. Our results demonstrate that UCE can achieve such a generalizable representation 314 

across different datasets while maintaining accuracy comparable to methods that require retraining 315 

for each specific dataset. To ensure that UCE’s embeddings, or any downstream models trained on 316 

them, remain universally shareable, we have designed UCE to be used as-is, without any model 317 

fine-tuning. This allows UCE representations to serve as a universal way of connecting any single-318 

cell gene expression dataset. 319

temper its promise and highlight directions for improvement. Current benchmarks primarily eval-321 

uate UCE’s ability to recover expert-annotated cell types, but these tasks are inherently limited 322 

by the granularity and subjectivity of available cell labels. As such, they may not fully capture 323 

the model’s capacity to represent subtler biological processes, such as cellular responses to per-324 

turbations or cross-modality integration. Moreover, like other large-scale biological foundation 325 

models, UCE operates largely as a “black box,” making it difficult to interpret how or why certain 326 

embeddings arise. Addressing this opacity will require the development of new interpretability 327 

tools that connect learned representations to mechanistic biological understanding. UCE’s training 328 

data, while extensive, is biased toward mammalian species—particularly human and mouse—and 329 

to specific tissues such as brain, raising concerns about its generalizability to underrepresented 330 

species and contexts. Methodological improvements in dataset construction could help alleviate 331 

these biases while reducing computational burden. Finally, although UCE improves alignment 332 

across datasets by capturing shared biological signal, it does not replace traditional batch effect 333 

correction methods, which remain useful for handling known experimental confounders. Simi-334 

larly, its reliance on gene sampling based on expression means that some fine-grained quantitative 335 

variation is lost, potentially limiting the resolution of certain cell state distinctions. 336

# abFigure 1: The Universal Cell Embedding Model is a large foundation model for single cell biology (a) Overview of the Universal Cell Embedding (UCE) model. UCE has a unique, biologically motivated representation of cells (blue) and training scheme (purple). Given the gene expression for a single cell, UCE samples with replacement genes that were expressed, weighted by their level of expression. Each gene is represented using a ‘token’ corresponding to its protein product. Gene tokens are represented numerically by using ESM2 protein embeddings, a 15 billion parameter protein language model that takes amino acid sequences as an input. The gene tokens are sorted by genomic location and grouped by chromosome. Chromosome groups are delineated by specific chromosome start tokens and end tokens, joined, and then passed into a transformer neural network. The embedding of the cell is determined by taking the final layer output of a special CLS token that is appended before all the other tokens. To train the UCE model, a portion of genes that were expressed are masked. The model next combines the protein embeddings corresponding to each of these genes with the embedding of the cell, and passes this joint representation through a neural network that predicts if a given gene was expressed in the cell or not. This objective function is then used to update the weights of the model. 

(b) UMAP visualizations of the universal cell embedding space. We apply UCE to embed 36 million cells, with more than 1,000 uniquely named cell types, from hundreds of datasets, dozens of tissues and eight species (Supplementary Figure 19), creating an Integrated Mega-scale Atlas (IMA) spanning the universe of cell biology.

# Methods 345 

Overview of UCE. 346 

UCE (Universal Cell Embedding) is a machine learning model for mapping single-cell gene 347 

expression profiles into a universal embedding space, denoted as U. In this space, each cell ci is 348 

represented as a demb -dimensional vector, where demb = 1280 . For all analyses we use the 1280 349 

dimensional UCE representation and not a 2 dimensional embedding such as that generated from 350 

tSNE or UMAP. Users of UCE should rely on the full 1280 dimensional representations to judge 351 

distances between cells, rather than data visualization using these methods, especially when they 352 

expect to see strong biological differences. 353 

The model takes as input a dataset D with N cells {ci}Ni=1 . Cells in D can be drawn from 354 

one or more distinct scRNA-seq experiments. Each cell ci in D is described by a gene expression 355 

vector xi ∈ NKi , where Ki is the number of genes measured in ci and can differ across D. The gene 356 

expression vectors xi ∈ NKi are not subset to those with high variance. UCE defines a function 357 

fu : {NKi → Rdemb }Ni=1 that maps each gene expression vector xi to its cell embedding vector hi.358 

Model input: Gene representation. 359 

The expression of gene g in cell ci is denoted by xig, where g represents any protein-coding 360 

gene. The corresponding token embedding pg is a pretrained embedding for the protein(s) encoded 361 

by the gene g. These embeddings are derived from a pretrained protein language model that takes 362 

an amino acid sequence as input and returns a dp-dimensional embedding vector as output. To 363 

create pg, we take the average of all proteins coded by gene g. In the context of UCE, we can 364 

formulate this as a dictionary that maps each gene g to a dp-dimensional protein embedding vector. 365 

Specifically, we employ the ESM2 model, which yields embeddings of size dp = 5120 [32, 33]. 366 

Protein language models are chosen for gene representation because they can generate uni-367 

versal representations of any protein sequence. Therefore, for new species, all that is required is 368 

to have the amino acid sequence of that species’ protein coding genes. These genes do not need 369 

to have solved structures and orthology does not need to be calculated between them and the ex-370 

isting training data. Model training ablations demonstrate that among a class of different protein 371 

language models, UCE models trained with ESM2-15B performed the best (Supplementary Fig. 372 

18). Model ablation experiments further demonstrate that the use of protein embeddings to tok-373 

enize genes has significant benefits for the more poorly represented species in the training corpus, 374 

as a small ablation model trained with protein embeddings outperformed a model with randomly 375 

initialized embeddings on all species except for human (Supplementary Fig. 18). Additionally, the 376 

use of protein embeddings enables a unique capability: embedding new species not found in the 377 

training data (Fig. 2.) 378

Transformer Architecture. 404 

Each cell sentence Si is fed into a transformer that consists of nlay layers. Each layer con-405 

tains a multi-head self-attention mechanism with nhead attention heads and a feedforward network 406 

operating over a hidden space of dimensionality dhid . We also initialize sinusoidally-varying po-407 

sitional embeddings. Gene token embeddings are compressed using a single layer MLP to demb -408 

dimensional vectors before passing through the transformer. 409 

While the transformer architecture is highly performative, it requires significant computa-410 

tional cost to train and evaluate. One important aspect of this cost is the quadratic increase in the 411 

runtime of transformers’ attention operations proportional to the number of tokens. Such a rela-412 

tionship requires that the number of genes sampled by UCE, 1024 , be relatively limited, especially 413 

given that individual cells might have more than 1024 uniquely expressed genes. Future model ar-414 

chitectures could explore increasing this context length, or using models with sub-quadratic scaling 415 

such as state space models [58] 416

Creating the IMA and dataset preprocessing. 458 

The Integrated Mega-scale Atlas (IMA) used to train UCE was created by combining scRNA-459 

seq datasets from multiple publicly available sources. The majority of IMA data (33.9 million cells 460 

and 285 datasets) is human and mouse data downloaded from CZI Cell X Gene (CxG) Census [35] 461 

version ”2023-07-10” (July 10th, 2023). Duplicate cells were removed by selecting primary cells 462 

only. The remainder of the IMA is composed of 2.3 million cells from 28 datasets, from eight 463 

different species: human, mouse, zebrafish, rhesus macaque, crab-eating macaque, mouse lemur, 464 

frog, and pig. 465 

For datasets from the CxG Census, preprocessing only involved filtering cells by minimum 466 

gene counts (200) and genes by a minimum cell counts of 10. No highly variable gene selection 467 

was applied. For datasets collected from other sources, preprocessing was not uniform (Supple-468 

mentary Note 1). 469 

For visualization of the IMA (Fig. 1b), predicting green monkey cell types (Fig. 2d), match-470 

ing new species centroids (Extended Data Table 1), and prediction of Norn-like cells (Fig. 4, 471 

Supplementary Fig. 9) a representative sample of the IMA was used in place of the full 36 million 472 

cells. This representative sample was used in order to speed up computationally intensive tasks like 473 

UMAP calculation. The sample was created by randomly choosing 10,000 cells from each dataset, 474 

without replacement. For datasets with fewer than 10,000 cells, the entire dataset was included. In 475 

total, this representative sample has 2,969,114 cells. The average number of cells per dataset in the 476 

sample is 9486. For visualization and centroid calculation, cell types in the sample were coarsened 477 

by mapping them to a set of 51 coarse cell types (Extended Data Table 5). 478

Description of other models. 512 

• Geneformer Geneformer [27] is a transformer based foundation model. Geneformer rep-513 

resents a cell as a list of genes sorted by their expression. Geneformer was trained using 514 

masked language modeling, on 30 million cells. For all analyses, the GF-12L-30M-i2048 515 

model was used. 516 

• tGPT tGPT [60] is a transformer based foundation model. tGPT represents cells, like Gene-517 

former, as a list of genes sorted by expression. However, tGPT is trained as an autoregressive 518 

langauge model, rather than a masked langauge model. tGPT was trained on 22 .3 million 519 

cells. 520 

• scGPT scGPT [28] is a transformer based foundation model. scGPT represents cells as a 521 

list of the expression values of its genes. scGPT is trained to iteratively decode those genes’ 522 

expressions when some are masked, using generative pretraining. scGPT was trained on 33 523 

million cells. For all analyses, the whole-human model was used. 524 

• scVI scVI [15] is a variational autoencoder. scVI is trained to reconstruct gene expression 525 

values using a zero-inflated negative binomial loss. scVI was used with default parameters 526 

for all experiments. scVI version 1.01+ was used. scVI input datasets were transformed 527 

only by taking the highly variable gene subset using sc.pp.highly variable genes with the 528 

flavor seurat v3, and then the scVI model was trained with default settings (number hidden 529 

variables 10). 530

Differential expression analysis of predicted Norn cells. 542 

A logistic classifier was trained to predict cell types from UCE embeddings on mouse kidney 543 

cells. This classifier was then applied to UCE embeddings from the representative sample of IMA 544 

datasets. Datasets were then split by tissue, and the datasets with the most predicted norn cells in 545 

each tissue were used for differential expression analysis. The top 13 kidney datasets, top 6 lung 546 

and top 6 heart datasets were chosen. 547 

For each individual (full) dataset, RNA counts were log normalized, and then differential ex-548 

pression was run using default settings as implemented in Scanpy [59], comparing predicted Norn 549 

cells to all other cells in the dataset. The results of these differential expression tests were used 550 

to determine the log fold change of marker genes in predicted Norn cells (Fig. 4c, Supplementary 551
