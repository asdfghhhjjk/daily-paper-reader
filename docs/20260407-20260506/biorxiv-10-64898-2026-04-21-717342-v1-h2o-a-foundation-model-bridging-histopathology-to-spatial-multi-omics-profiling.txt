Abstract 24 

Spatial omics technologies have revolutionized the molecular profiling of tissues but remain constrained by 25 

high costs and limited scalability. While hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining is ubiquitous, it lacks 26 

molecular specificity.  Here,  we  present  H2O  (Histopathology to Omics) , a generalist AI framework that 27 

bridges the modality gap between histopathology and spatial multi -omics, enabling the direct inference of 28 

spatial transcriptomics (ST) and proteomics (SP) landscapes from routine H&E images.  H2O  integrates Vision 29 

Transformers (ViT) with Large Language Models (LLM) via contrastive learning to align histological 30 

morphology with semantic molecular knowledge.  This cross -modal approach allows the model to incorporate 31 

spatial expression profiles into histological pattern recognition, effectively decoding the molecular 32 

heterogeneity underlying tissue morphology.  Trained on  a pan -tissue dataset of 1.3 million paired H&E -spatial 33 

patches across 2 5 organs  and cancer types , H2O  predicts spatial  omics  expression from histology with high 34 

concordance to sequenc ed  measurements and consistently outperforms state -of -the -art models across three 35 

cancer benchmarks.  Notably,  H2O  recovers the  MIF -CD74 /CD44  signaling axis directly from H&E images, 36 

highlighting its capacity to infer biologically meaningful cell -cell communication without molecular profiling. 37 

Applying on three  additional public cohorts  covering fetal and  paediatric  thymus tissues , human metastatic 38 

lymph node,  and  breast  cancer, encompassing human development , 3D spatial frameworks,  and  integrative 39 

multi -omics,  H2O  yield s biologically concordant insights , demonstrating superior accuracy, robustness, and 40 

generalizability  across real -world applications in diverse scenarios . H2O  converts routine histopathology into 41 

a portal for spatially resolved multi‑omics profiling by computationally generating transcriptomic and 42 

proteomic landscapes, thereby enhancing tissue phenotyping and enabling scalable, integrative tissue‑atlas 43 

con struction. 44 

Introduction 45 

Hematoxylin -and -eosin (H&E) -stained histology sections are routinely acquired and  provid es  insights into 46 

disease mechanisms , play ing  a vital role  in biomedical research and clinical practice  [1,2,3,4].  However, H&E 47 

images are fundamentally limited by their inability to capture the  molecular heterogeneity , specifically 48 

transcriptomic and proteomic landscapes , that drives disease progression . On the other hand, s patial omics 49 

profiling technologies provide spatial ly resolved mapping of transcripts, proteins and other molecul es, which 50 

are crucial in revolutioniz ing  our understanding from single biological events to the whole biological system. 51 

Emerging advancements  in  spatial  omics technologies  [5,6,7,8,9,10 ,11 ,12 ,13 ] have enabled high -resolution 52 

analysis of  cell states , but  their  widespread clinical adoption  is hindered by  high cost, technical complexity, 53 

and incompatibility with archived formalin -fixed paraffin -embedded (FFPE) samples. There is an urgent need 54 

to bridge this gap by distilling high -dimensional molecular insights into routine histopathological workflows. 55 

Recent advances in Vision Transformer (ViT)  [14 ,15 ,16 ,17 ,18 ,19 ] architectures have catalyzed the 56 

development of a growing suite of general -purpose self -supervised models for computational  histo pathology, 57 

as represented by  UNI  [20 ], Virchow  [21 ], CONCH  [22 ], PLIP  [23 ], m STAR  [24 ], and  CTransPath  [25 ]. In 58 

parallel, the adaptation of  Large  Language  Model (LLM)  [26 ,27 ,28 ,29 ,30 ,31 ] methodologies to the molecular 59 

life sciences has  propelled  the  emergence  of foundation models  (FMs)  capable of learning and interpreting the 60 

“languages” of biology , from  genomic sequences to  protein structures [32 ,33 ,34 ,35 ,36 ,37 ,38 ], such as  scBERT 61 

[39 ], scGPT  [35 ], scFoundation [40 ], scGPT -Spatial  [41 ], and CellFM  [42 ] for transcriptomics . Despite their 62 

individual successes, these models remain siloed. Vision FMs lack explicit biochemical context, while 63 

transcriptomic s FMs lack spatial morphological grounding. Integrating these two modalities represents the 64 

next frontier in biological representation learning. 65 

The rise of spatially resolved transcriptomics (ST) and proteomics (SP) has created a unique data 66 

landscape where high -resolution H&E images are inherently paired with molecular profiles.  Early attempts  to 67 

map the two modalities,  like  HisToGene  [43 ], BLEEP  [44 ],  DeepPT  [45 ] and OmiCLIP [ 46 ],  relied on  simple 68 

regression  from image feature  or retrieval mechanisms  and  fail ed  to generalize across diverse biological 69

Overview of  H2O  as a cross -modal alignment method for histopathology  and spatial omics 105 

H2O  is a multi -omics -guided histopathology foundation model designed to align whole -slide imaging features 106 

with spatially resolved transcriptomic s and proteomic s profiles through contrastive learning.  To enable 107 

comprehensive coverage of various tissues, organs, and diseases  (Extended Data Fig. 2)  across both H&E and 108 

ST modalities, we curated a diverse dataset , which  contains three major parts  for H2O training and evaluation 109 

(Fig. 1a).  Samples from  The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA)  [48 ], the  Genotype -Tissue Expression (GTEx) 110 

[49 ] project , and additional in -house breast cancer collections  (Methods)  were used to train the  histo pathology 111 

FM.  Then  we employ ed  a gene expression  FM  based on scGPT  [35 ], initialized from whole -human pretrained 112 

checkpoints and further fine -tuned on  ST data  from  the HEST -1k [47 ] dataset to encode spatial transcriptomic s113 

knowledge.  Subsequently, we trained  H2O  using  matched H&E and ST samples  from  the HEST -1k dataset. 114 

To infer  transcriptomics  profiles from a central image patch,  H2O  leverages morphological context from its 115 

local neighborhood and incorporates a Feature -wise Linear Modulation (FiLM) layer for resolution -aware 116 

feature calibration . See Extended Data Fig. 3 for ablation study of these modules . We further  collect ed  three 117 

additional datasets , HTSA, OpenST, and HTAPP, to investigate the ability of  H2O  in imparting molecular 118 

features to  H&E features .119 

A transcriptomics  FM  fine -tuned with ST data.  Inspired by the great success of single -cell foundation 120 

models in representing the cellular characteristics, we utilized one of the representative methods, scGPT, to 121 

encode the transcriptomics  features . In scGPT, the self -attention module learns intra -cellular gene -gene 122 

dependencies within a cell and inter -cellular interactions across different cells.  Trained on 33 million single -123 

cell transcriptomics data,  representations  from the model  encode cell state and are sensitive to condition -124 

specific variation, including developmental stage s, disease progression s, and treatment response s.125 

However, a domain gap exists between ST and single -cell data . Further more,  in comparison  to single -126 

cell data , ST  exhibits greater heterogeneity owing to substantial platform variation, broadly spanning 127 

sequencing -based and imaging -based technologies  [50 ]. Consequently,  directly applying the scRNA -seq 128 

trained scGPT model to the ST data will inevitably induce  improper representation of cells/spots.  To address 129 

this,  we fine -tuned the scGPT on the largest HEST -1k data , comprising 1.3 million cells/spots  span ning 130 

sequencing -based platforms (10x Visium/Visium HD) and imaging -based assays (10x Xenium) , enabling  the 131 

learned representations  to  internalize  cross -modal  context while  preserving  modality -specific statistics.  The 132 

fine -tuning process adapt ed  the  single -cell foundation model to spatial measurements and mitigate d cross -133 

modal domain shift relative to scRNA -seq while  introducing  ST  knowledge .134 

An image  FM  aligned to  ST  latent space with  contrastive  learning . Recent  work have proposed  many 135 

histo pathology foundation models specifically designed to extract morphology features from H&E images. 136 

Most of them are pre -trained on FFPE slices using self -supervised learning or multi -modal contrastive learning 137

strategies.  Nevertheless,  the domain gap between FFPE and fresh frozen samples  presents a significant 138 

challenge for histopathology foundation models. Most  histo pathology  FM s are trained primarily on FFPE 139 

tissues, which are different from the fresh frozen samples typically used in  ST  technologies. To address this 140 

challenge, we first  train ed  an  in -house  DINO -V2 -based  histo pathology  FM  using  FFPE and  fresh frozen  tissue 141 

samples  to ensure its applicability to both tissue types.  A total of  54,435,706  patches were extracted from 142 

44,992  samples spanning 32 cancer types in The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) and 40 tissue types from the 143 

Genotype -Tissue Expression (GTEx ) dataset for model training. During patch extraction, rather than utilizing 144 

all available patches from each whole slide image (WSI), a checkerboard sampling strategy was employed, 145 

with a maximum of 2,000 patches selected per WSI to ensure representative diversity  while  mitigating spatial 146 

redundancy inherent in  histopathology images  . This selective sampling serves as a safeguard against  training 147 

collapse and loss divergence, as excessive data redundancy in large -model pre -training has been shown to 148 

compromise both convergence stability and downstream generalizability  [51 ]. 149 

Following this, we employed a contrastive learning framework to align H&E image embeddings with the 150 

latent space of  ST  by maximizing similarity  between  positive  H&E -ST  pairs while simultaneously minimizing 151 

similarity  between  negative pairs  profiles . This alignment encourages the image encoder to  learn 152 

characteristics from both the  histopathology  images and transcriptomics profiles,  pr eserving morph ology 153 

characteristics while introducing  potential  omics knowledge . Crucially, it distills intra - and inter -cellular 154 

information from transcriptomic s FM , such as  gene -gene dependencies and neighborhood interactions , into 155 

the image representation, improving biological fidelity and robustness across platforms. 156 

Predicting ST profiles using fixed -size image patches fails to accommodate the intrinsic  variability across 157 

different platforms and resolutions.  To retain this information, we adopt ed  diameter -conditioned learning ,158 

where  the encoder  was  conditioned on spot size, providing platform - and resolution -aware context during 159 

training  to  improve the coupling between image features and expression magnitude and enhance cross -160 

platform transfer ability . And t o improve quantitative awareness in prediction, we adopt ed  a FiLM module to 161 

integrate auxiliary calibration information. 162 

The H2O framework.  In summary, H2O establishes a foundational cross -modal framework that anchors 163 

histopathological features into a high -dimensional molecular space, enabling the direct translation of standard 164 

H&E images into rich, molecularly informed landscapes (Fig. 1b).  By leveraging contrastive alignment and 165 

platform -specific conditioning, H2O transcends simple morphological correlations to capture the latent 166 

biological programs linking tissue architecture to gene and protein expression. This versatile representation 167 

facilitates a broad spectrum of downstream tasks (Fig. 1c), ranging from  context -aware expression prediction 168 

and  multi -modal clustering  to the elucidation of  cell -cell communication , temporal trajectories , and  3D spatial 169 

contiguity . Ultimately, H2O provides a  scalable pipeline to unlock deep biological insights and multi -omic s170 

integration from existing histological archives .171 

Evaluation of  H2O  for  spatial  transcriptomic s prediction from H&E images 172 

Accurately  predicting  ST  from H&E images  holds  great significance in  efficient and economical biology 173 

analysis.  To assess the predictive capacity of H2O, we evaluated it on 53 test samples from the HEST -1k 174 

dataset that had been withheld from both the scGPT fine‑tuning and contrastive learning phases.  We 175 

benchmarked  H2O  against  HisToGene  [43 ], BLEEP  [44 ],  and  DeepPT  [45 ],  and OmiCLIP  [46 ], by training 176 

and testing  all methods on the  clear cell renal cell carcinoma  (kidney cancer, ccRCC , 24 samples ) and  prostate 177 

adenocarcinoma (prostate cancer, PRAD , 23 samples ) data of HEST -1k, predicting 5,033 global  highly 178 

variable genes (HVGs) of this dataset . Across both benchmarks,  H2O  consistently outperformed the 179 

competitive  methods in terms of Pearson  Correlation  Coefficient (PCC)  and  Spearman  Rank  Correlation 180 

Coefficient  (SRCC)  (Fig. 2a) . It is evident that  H2O  holds an obvious advantage over other state -of -the -art 181 

methods across the evaluation metrics. 182

Validation of  H2O  ability to r eveals  molecular  expression  features and  functional  signaling  patterns 246 

The reliability of downstream analysis is crucial  for  ST  data.  In this section,  we aimed to utilize the  H2O -247 

predicted  ST  data for  several  downstream analyses , verifying the utility of the well -trained  H2O  prediction 248 

and  identifying potential  biological insights  infeasible  from the H&E histopathology images. 249 

One  central  downstream task is spatial domain recognition, which requires the image -omics model 250 

predictions  can  reliably  reproduce the molecular structure of the underlying tissue. To verify that  H2O 251 

recapitulates  experimentally validated  expression patterns, we first applied k -means clustering across varying 252 

values of k. Clusters derived from  k-means clustering after  principal component analysis (PCA)  on  H2O -253 

predicted expression  more closely resembled clusters  identified from experimental profiles  than those 254 

obtained  directly  from image embeddings  (Fig. 3a) . This proves  that  H2O  recovers molecular information 255 

absent from raw histology , leading to  predicted expression profiles that more closely match  experiment -256 

measured  patterns . Quantitatively, Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) and Adjusted Mutual Information (AMI) scores 257

H2O  generalizes to developmental systems in the  human  thymus 283 

A fundamental question for  an  image -based  molecular  predictor is  whether  its outputs reflect genuine 284 

molecular signatures and remain consistent with prior biological knowledge . To  test the reliability , we applied 285 

H2O  to the Human Thymus Spatial Atlas (HTSA) [ 57 ] (Fig. 4a).  HTSA provides a longitudinal series 286 

capturing  T cell development from fetal through early postnatal life , containing 28 samples  across a timeline 287 

spanning from 13 weeks post -fertilization to 2 years of age, with  high -resolution multiplexed imaging , spatial 288 

transcriptomics, and  matched single -cell transcriptomics  at each time point. This systematic temporal 289 

coverage makes the dataset ideally suited to assess whether  morphology -based predictions can reconstruct 290 

both immune developmental trajectories and spatial microenvironment dynamics across maturation.  The 291 

availability of histopathology images aligned to spatial omics data provided an ideal testing ground to assess 292 

whether  H2O  could reconstruct developmental processes directly from routine H&E slides. 293 

The HTSA  dataset  provides a continuous, scale - and rotation -invariant morphological common coordinate 294 

framework (CCF) for the thymus, enabling systematic comparisons across donors and stages. Leveraging this 295 

framework, we stratified  H2O  predictions into fetal and paediatric groups and quantified the expression of 296 

canonical T -cell marker genes  of the two groups  (Fig. 4b) . Fig.4c  (more in Extended Data Fig. 8)  shows the 297 

spatial distribution of  8 H2O -predicted marker genes ( CXCL1 9, IL7R , CXCL14 , CCL4, CXCL9, IL18, SPP1, 298 

CCL2 ). Correlating these distributions across developmental stages revealed that most cytokine genes 299 

maintained highly conserved spatial  organization, consistent with the notion that the thymic microenvironment 300 

remains functionally stable during T -cell maturation  [57 ] (Fig.  4d ). Importantly, three genes , SPP1, CCL2 ,301 

and  IL33 , showed substantially lower cosine similarities  between fetal and paediatric stages , suggesting 302 

developmental stage -dependent reorganization. Mapping these genes back to the CCF indicated a303 

redistribution of expression s from the capsular region in the fetal thymus to the medullary compartment in the 304 

paediatric thymus, reflecting a shift in microenvironmental niches as development progresses  (Fig. 4d) .305 

To disentangle the cellular basis of these shifts, we performed cell type deconvolution  with Cell2location 306 

[58 ] on  the  H2O -predicted  ST  profiles  using  matched  scRNA -seq data  of the same tissue type as reference 307 

(Fig. 4 e,f ). The deconvolution results  enabled us to estimate the average spatial distribution of cell populations 308 

along the CMA axes. To filter out cell types with low abundance, we applied  cut -offs  of 0.15 % and 2 %,309 

representing the minimum  relative cell  abundance required for spot inclusion. The results are visualized  in 310 

Fig. 4f, where the  spot size corresponds to the fraction of  spots  exceeding the abundance  cut -off and the  spot 311 

color indicates the mean  relative  abundance of each cell type  in each region . This analysis revealed that stage -312 

specific expression differences were largely attributable to changes in  Early T -cell Progenitors (T -ETP)  and 313 

the broader  Early  Double -Negative  stage of T cells  (T_DN( early ))  in  the medulla in both fetal and paediatric 314 

tissue , align ing  with previous reports highlighting the role of stromal and immune niche remodeling in shaping 315 

T-cell differentiation  [59 ]. For example, fibroblasts and endothelial cells are known to contribute to cytokine 316 

gradients, while TECs form the structural basis of positive and negative selection. Macrophages, in turn, 317 

regulate apoptotic clearance during thymocyte turnover, providing a functional explanation for their 318 

redistribution . Thus, by aligning cell type abundance to the predicted ST spots,  H2O  reconstructed the spatial 319 

trajectory of T -lineage differentiation, revealing both conserved programs and stage -dependent remodeling of 320 

the thymic microenvironment  (Fig. 4f) . Importantly, the recovered cytokine maps recapitulated established 321 

paradigms of thymic development,  where  T-cell migration cues remain broadly conserved across fetal and 322 

paediatric stages while stromal and innate immune components undergo localized reorganization. 323

Discussion 478 

In this study, we present H2O, a foundational framework that transcends simple morphological 479 

correlations by anchoring histopathological features into a high -dimensional molecular space via  spatial omics 480 

guidance.  Beyond outperforming existing benchmarks in predictive accuracy and cross -dataset generalization, 481 

H2O delivers superior biological resolution by recovering coherent molecular hierarchies and organized 482 

niches often invisible to standard histology. Specifically, through high -fidelity clustering and intercellular 483 

communication analysis, H2O successfully elucidated active signaling circuit s, such as the immune 484 

modulators  MIF -CD44  and  MIF -CD74 , demonstrating its unique capacity to reconstruct functionally 485 

connected networks rather than isolated molecular markers. 486 

The robustness of H2O  was  further underscored by its exceptional generality across diverse  temporal, 487 

spatial, and multi -omic s contexts.  In developmental biology, H2O successfully preserved the intricate 488 

molecular trajectories of embryonic maturation, accurately capturing time -resolved gene expression programs 489 

from histological snapshots.  Extend ing  to three -dimensional landscapes, H2O generated volumetric molecular 490 

maps from serial sections of metastatic lymph nodes, delineating continuous invasion fronts and immune 491 

niches that remain invisible in 2D analysis. Beyond transcriptomics,  the  contrastive alignment paradigm  of 492 

H2O  successfully  generalizes to spatial proteomics (SP), enabling high -fidelity RNA -protein profiling even 493 

from modality -limited samples. In breast cancer, this multimodal integration resolved functionally distinct cell 494 

states that were otherwise obscured in single -modality data. Collectively, these results demonstrate that H2O 495 

transcends specific biological contexts, providing a unified framework to decode complex tissue organization 496 

across dimensions and molecular layers. 497 

A central challenge in spatial foundation models is reconciling the disparate biological scales across ST 498 

platforms, where identical pixel dimensions can represent anything from subcellular resolution to multi -499 

cellular aggregates.  To navigate this technical heterogeneity, H2O explicitly incorporates multi -scale 500 

supervision through two key mechanisms. First, by  conditioning on platform -specific information such as spot 501 

diameter , H2O  adaptively modulates its feature interpretation to match the specific resolution of  different 502 

dataset s. Second, by  integrating neighboring patch context , H2O moves beyond local  morphological cues to 503 

capture broader tissue architecture , such as vascular niches and immune gradients  that govern spatially diffuse 504

gene expression. Our findings underscore that the synergy between diameter -conditioned modulation and 505 

spatial context is essential for mitigating platform -specific variability, ensuring that image -based predictions 506 

remain biologically faithful across the diverse landscape of spatial omics technologies. 507 

Despite these advances, several limitations remain. Although  H2O  leverages large -scale ST datasets  for 508 

pretraining , coverage of rare tissues and underrepresented conditions is limited, which may constrain 509 

performance in specific contexts. Further expansion with single -cell atlases and bulk transcriptomic s resources 510 

could improve zero -shot generalization. Moreover,  H2O  currently functions as a predictive framework rather 511 

than a generative one . Future integration with generative models may enable synthesis of full transcriptomic s512 

or proteomics  maps at whole -slide scale. 513 

Another important limitation is that our current implementation does not explicitly operate at cellular 514 

resolution. Instead,  H2O  predictions are derived from a fixed 224× 224 -pixel patch together with eight 515 

immediate neighbors, providing contextual but not truly single -cell -level information. While this patch -plus -516 

context strategy effectively captures local and regional structure, it may miss molecular heterogeneity at the 517 

level of individual cells. Future work should explore multi -resolution learning  with  single -cell resolution as  a518 

basis , aggregating features across spatial scales, and directly integrating expression profiles from single -cell 519 

spatial  multi -omics datasets once sufficient curated resources become available for foundation model training. 520 

Such  improvements  could enhance both the granularity and accuracy of predictions, ultimately bridging 521 

cellular and tissue -level organization within a unified framework. 522 

In addition, while our extension of  H2O  to  SP  highlights the feasibility of translating histology into 523 

protein -level predictions, it  remains  limited. Unlike  ST  prediction, which benefits from pretrained encoders 524 

trained on large -scale ST datasets,  SP  translation in  H2O  relies only on a lightweight multilayer perceptron 525 

(MLP) without access to comparably rich pretraining. This restricts its ability to fully capture the complexity 526 

of protein regulation and pathway -level organization. Developing dedicated proteome -aware encoders or 527 

multimodal pretraining strategies that jointly leverage transcriptomic s and proteomic s datasets will be crucial 528 

to advance beyond proof -of -principle and achieve more reliable protein -level prediction from histology. 529 

Furthermore , expanding transcriptome -guided training to integrate additional omics layers, such as 530 

metabolomics, could establish multi -omic s image foundation models with broader applications. 531 

In summary,  H2O  establishes the concep t of a  spatial omics -guided image foundation model, in wh ich 532 

histopathology image  embeddings are trained under direct  molecular supervision  to produce spatial  multi -533 

omics prediction . This paradigm enables scalable, generalizable, and biologically interpretable representations 534 

of tissue organization, moving beyond morphology alone to recover conserved programs, dynamic transitions, 535 

and functional signaling networks. By transforming routine H&E slides into molecularly annotated maps, 536 

H2O  pave the way for next -generation computational  histo pathology, bridging basic biology and clinical 537 

precision medicine. 538

OpenST -Metastatic Lymph Node.  This dataset contains 19 serial sections (S2 -S38) from a single metastatic 552

Model Training 567 

We first fine -tuned scGPT on  a subset of  HEST -1k to adapt its transcriptomic s representations to  ST  context. 568 

Then , we  trained a Contrastive Language -Image Pretraining  (CLIP ) -based foundation model  on the HEST -569

Benchmark 660 

To evaluate the  expression gene ration  ability of  H2O  from histopathology images, we benchmarked it against 661 

four state -of -the -art methods , including  OmiCLIP , HisToGene, BLEEP, and DeepPT. Together with  H2O ,662 

these five approaches were systematically  trained and  assessed on the  two  HEST -1k subsets, including HEST -663 

1k-ccRCC and HEST -1k-PRAD, using the  four  evaluation metrics defined above. To further examine the 664 

generalizability of these methods, we additionally tested them on the  additional  HEST -1k-IDC -LymphNode 665 

dataset, using models pretrained on the  HEST -1k-ccRCC and  HEST -1k-PRAD benchmarks. 666 

HisToGene.  HisToGene was originally designed to operate on 112×112 image patches. To ensure fair 667 

comparison with other methods, where the patch size directly influences the amount of information conveyed, 668 

we adapted HisToGene to use 224×224 patches while keeping the rest of the architecture unchanged. 669 

BLEEP.  BLEEP leverages contrastive learning to construct a low -dimensional joint embedding space from a 670 

reference dataset containing paired image and gene expression profiles at micrometer resolution. It employs a 671 

ResNet50 pretrained on ImageNet as the image encoder and a fully connected network as the expression 672 

encoder. We adopted the preprocessing pipeline and architectural framework of BLEEP.  The training  set  for 673 

training or fine -tuning other methods are used  as the reference dataset  for BLEEP .674 

DeepPT.  DeepPT represents the first stage of the ENLIGHT -DeepPT framework, which predicts genome -675 

wide tumor mRNA expression directly from histopathology slides. In this study, we adapted DeepPT for 676 

application to ST data. DeepPT employs a ResNet -50 backbone pretrained on ImageNet as the image encoder. 677 

To reduce the dynamic range of expression values and mitigate batch - and experiment -specific discrepancies 678 

in library size, we  followed  the normalization scheme described in the original DeepPT paper. 679
