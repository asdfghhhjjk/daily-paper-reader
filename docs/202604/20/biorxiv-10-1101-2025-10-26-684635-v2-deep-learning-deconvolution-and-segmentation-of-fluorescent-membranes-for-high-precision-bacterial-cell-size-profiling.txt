ABSTRACT 13 

14 

Evolutionary studies in bacteria have emphasized genetic and metabolic diversity, while cell-size 15 

variation has received less attention. Here we introduce MEDUSSA, a high-throughput method 16 

for precise bacterial cell-size profiling based on automatic segmentation of fluorescent membrane 17 

images, well suited to studying cell-size diversity. The approach uses deep-learning-based mem-18 

brane deconvolution, segmentation models fine-tuned for fluorescent-membrane images, and er-19 

ror-corrected cell measurement to extract accurate sizes from individual bacterial cells regardless 20 

of shape, size, chaining, or clustering. Our method overcomes limitations of phase-contrast seg-21 

mentation, yielding reliable single-cell dimensions. We applied MEDUSSA to six strains of Priestia 22 

megaterium and found over twofold differences in cell volume across strains, largely driven by dif-23 

ferences in cell width. We identified a partially-functional PBP1 allele that underlies the reduced 24 

width of one strain. Together, these results demonstrate the power of comparative analyses in 25 

bacterial cell biology and expand the toolkit to investigate the evolution of bacterial cell size. 26

INTRODUCTION 27 

28 

Bacterial cells are often perceived as uniformly small, which has led to a relative lack of attention 29 

to the evolution of bacterial cell size. Yet, careful microscopic observation has revealed an incred-30 

ible diversity of bacterial shapes and sizes, with cell volumes spanning multiple orders of magni-31 

tude (Schavemaker & Munoz-Gomez, 2022; Schober et al., 2025). Even among closely related 32 

species, cell size can vary significantly. For instance, strains from two Bacillus species, B. thuringiensis 33 

and B. pumilus , can differ in volume by nearly an order of magnitude (Lopez-Garrido et al., 2018), 34 

suggesting that cell size is an evolvable trait capable of changing over relatively short evolutionary 35 

timescales. 36 

37 

Despite this diversity, our appreciation of bacterial cell size variation at finer evolutionary scales 38 

remains limited. Systematic studies have focused on mutant libraries in a few model strains (Juillot 39 

et al., 2021; Ursell et al., 2017). However, comparative studies analyzing the size of different bac-40 

terial strains or species are rare (Delaby et al., 2025; Gray et al., 2019), and most available data 41 

come from independent studies, often using different imaging methods and manual measurements 42 

of a few representative cells cultured in different conditions. While this highlights bacterial size 43 

diversity, methodological inconsistencies make cross-study comparisons difficult. As a result, we 44 

lack a clear picture of how bacterial cell size vary among closely related species and strains. 45 

46 

Recent advances in image analysis pose a unique opportunity for the standardization of methods 47 

to measure bacterial cell size and may help to address these challenges. In particular, the incorpo-48 

ration of machine-learning into image analysis pipelines have enabled the automatic identification 49 

and precise delineation of boundaries of individual bacterial cells in microscopy images. This pro-50 

cess, known as image segmentation, generates masks of individual cells that can be used for accu-51 

rate cell size measurements. The U-Net, a convolutional neural network architecture originally 52 

designed for the analysis of biomedical images (Falk et al., 2019; Ronneberger et al., 2015), is 53 

behind some of the most powerful image segmentation algorithms currently available, such as 54 

StarDist (Schmidt et al., 2018), DeLTA (Lugagne et al., 2020), Cellpose (Stringer et al., 2021), 55 

MiSiC (Panigrahi et al., 2021), Omnipose (Cutler et al., 2022), DeepBacs (Spahn et al., 2022) and 56 

DistNet (Ollion et al., 2024), to name a few. More recently, vision transformers (Dosovitskiy et 57 

al., 2020) have enabled the development of foundation models (Kirillov et al., 2023; Zou et al., 58 

2023), which support strong off-the-shelf performance and facilitate fine-tuning on custom data. 59

Several models have leveraged large public microscopy datasets to achieve high flexibility, includ-60 

ing microSAM (Archit et al., 2025), Cellpose-SAM (Pachitariu et al., 2025) and CellSAM (Marks 61 

et al., 2025). These tools are significantly improving the precision and automation of bacterial cell 62 

segmentation, paving the way for more consistent and reproducible cell size measurements. 63 

64 

Here, we have fine-tuned and benchmarked some these models for segmenting fluorescent mem-65 

branes. Building on this, we developed a pipeline for extracting precise single-cell size information 66 

from fluorescent membrane images, which allows the characterization of individual cell sizes re-67 

gardless of clustering or chaining patterns. We applied this pipeline, that we call MEDUSSA 68 

(MEmbrane DeconvolUtion and Segmentation for Size Analyses), to investigate cell-size diversity 69 

across six strains of Priestia megaterium , a Gram-positive bacterium commonly used for physiological 70 

studies and biotechnological applications (Logan & Vos, 2015). We found significant differences 71 

in volume between the strains—spanning more than twofold—underpinned by consistent differ-72 

ences in cell length and width. We further link width differences between two of the strains to a 73 

specific mutation in ponA , which encodes penicillin binding protein 1 (PBP1), resulting in a hypo-74 

morphic PBP1 allele. 75

Limitations of phase contrast segmentation for single cell studies. 78 

Deep-learning-based segmentation algorithms are versatile and can segment different types of im-79 

ages, as long as they are trained with appropriately labeled datasets for the specific task. However, 80 

phase-contrast has become the default imaging modality for bacterial segmentation, as it provides 81 

excellent contrast between cells and background without the need for staining or genetic manipu-82 

lation (Cutler et al., 2022; Ducret et al., 2016; O'Connor et al., 2022; Ollion et al., 2024; Paintdakhi 83 

et al., 2016; Stylianidou et al., 2016; Ursell et al., 2017; Van Valen et al., 2016). Despite its ad-84 

vantages, phase contrast images have limitations for cell size estimation. Firstly, they lack clear 85 

visual references to precisely define cell boundaries. Secondly, phase contrast does not allow the 86 

distinction of cells that remain attached after division, complicating the identification of individual 87 

cells in bacterial species that form cellular chains (Holt et al., 1994; Syvertsson et al., 2016). For 88 

instance, Bacillus subtilis and related species form heterogeneous populations in which single cells 89 

coexist with long chains consisting of dozens of cells (Kearns & Losick, 2005; Margulis et al., 90 

1998). Because the separation of individual cells within a chain is not visible in phase-contrast 91 

images, state-of-the-art segmentation models optimized for phase-contrast segmentation, such as 92 

the Omnipose “bact_phase_omni” model (Cutler et al., 2022), segment chains as very long indi-93 

vidual cells, making it difficult to extract information at the single cell level (Fig. 1a,c). 94 

95 

This issue can be circumvented by staining the cell membranes with lipophilic fluorescent dyes 96 

such as FM 4-64 (Pogliano et al., 1999) or Nile Red (Strahl et al., 2014), which allows the visuali-97 

zation of the septal membranes that separate individual cells within chains (Fig. 1b,d). The fluo-98 

rescence intensity profile of FM 4-64-stained membranes shows a sharp peak that can be traced 99 

around the cell (Fig 1e,f), providing an unambiguous guide to delimit the cell boundaries. We 100 

therefore explored the performance of different segmentation models on fluorescent membrane 101 

images. 102

High-throughput segmentation of fluorescent membranes. 117 

We fine-tuned existing deep-learning segmentation models for segmenting fluorescent membrane 118 

images of bacterial cells, and benchmarked their performance. We focused on Cellpose3 (Stringer 119 

& Pachitariu, 2025), Omnipose (Cutler et al., 2022), and the SAM-based foundation models mi-120 

croSAM (Archit et al., 2025) and Cellpose-SAM (Pachitariu et al., 2025). Omnipose has been re-121 

ported to perform better on elongated cells than other segmentation algorithms (Cutler et al., 122 

2022). The models were fine-tuned using over seven-thousand FM 4-64-stained B. subtilis cells 123 

annotated with JFilament active-contour software (Fig. 1g) (Smith et al., 2010), which allows the 124 

semiautomated generation of snakes that track the fluorescent membrane peak around the cell, 125 

thereby diminishing user annotation bias. JFilament snakes precisely delimit cell contours and pro-126 

vide accurate cell dimensions (Supplementary Fig. 1). The training dataset included single cells, 127 

cells within chains and cells within clusters (see Methods for details). We tested the fine-tuned 128 

models with images of three bacilli of different sizes: B. subtilis , B. thuringiensis , and Priestia megaterium 129 

(Fig. 2a; Supplementary Fig. 2a). We also tested Morphometrics, a non-deep-learning segmentation 130 

method that has shown good performance on fluorescent membrane images (Ursell et al., 2017). 131 

Cellpose-SAM showed the best performance in benchmarking tests (mean F1 at IoU 0.8, 132 

0.86±0.08), followed by Omnipose (0.76±0.16), Morphometrics (0.64±0.22), Cellpose3 133 

(0.59±0.17) and microSAM (0.30±0.16) (Fig. 2a). Because segmentation algorithms are known to 134 

often fail on elongated cells, we further tested the performance of the fine-tuned models on a 135 

Lysinibacillus strains that naturally produces long cells under laboratory conditions (Supplementary 136 

Fig. 2b). Only Omnipose segmented long cells appropriately. The other models oversegmented 137 

them. Since our goal was to develop a pipeline for cell size analysis across different strains and 138 

species, many of which may naturally form long cells, we further analyzed Omnipose segmentation 139 

to improve its performance. 140 

141 

The fine-tuned Omnipose model produced segmentation masks lacking aberrations, truncations, 142 

and protrusions for isolated cells and individual cells within a chain, maintaining a proper rod-143 

shaped morphology. However, it struggled with clustered cells, often producing indented or trun-144 

cated masks (Supplementary Fig. 2a). We reasoned that this limitation might reflect the broader 145 

and less well-defined membrane signal in raw fluorescent images, particularly in densely packed 146 

regions (Hardo et al., 2024). To test whether improving membrane definition could enhance seg-147 

mentation accuracy, we retrained Omnipose using deconvolved images. Deconvolution reduces 148 

out-of-focus blur and produces sharper membranes (Agard, 1984; Scalettar et al., 1996). When 149 

evaluated on deconvolved membrane images, the retrained model, FMSeg_Omni (hereafter 150

To facilitate the segmentation of fluorescent membrane images acquired with different micro-186 

scopes, including systems without deconvolution capabilities, we trained an image restoration 187 

model to predict deconvolution from non-deconvolved images (FM2FM model; Fig. 3a). When 188 

direct deconvolution of membrane images is possible, it can be used directly. FM2FM provides an 189 

alternative for datasets in which this is not feasible. FM2FM was trained using the CARE frame-190 

work (Weigert et al., 2018), in which a supervised U-Net predicts pixelwise fluorescence values 191 

while accounting for spatial context (i.e. neighboring pixel intensities). The fluorescence profiles 192 

of the predicted images closely matched those of deconvolved images (Fig. 3a-c), and the model 193 

could generate images amenable for segmentation from different microscopes (Supplementary 194 

Fig. 5). 195

To assess whether deconvolution prediction affects segmentation, we compared cell size parame-209 

ters—width, length, cross-sectional area, surface area and volume (see next section and methods 210 

for details)—extracted from masks generated using deconvolved and FM2FM-predicted images. 211 

We used multiple size metrics to verify that information relevant to downstream analyses is pre-212 

served across processing scenarios, as single metrics often fail to predict how image-derived meas-213 

urements propagate into downstream results (Gunawan et al., 2025). We also compared shape 214 

descriptors not directly related to cell size, such as convex hull area, eccentricity and solidity. The 215 

distributions were very similar, with no statistically significant differences (Fig. 3d), indicating that 216 

membrane deconvolution prediction from raw membrane images has a minimal effect on segmen-217 

tation and does not impact cell size calculations. 218 

219 

Additionally, we trained a fluorescence translation model to infer deconvolved membranes from 220 

cytoplasmic fluorescence (FP2FM model; Supplementary Fig. 6), which might be useful when 221 

membrane staining is not feasible. Previously developed CARE restoration models for the predic-222 

tion of superresolution membranes can also be used (Spahn et al., 2022). Although these ap-223 

proaches expand the applicability of the method, they may introduce biases on cell size measure-224 

ments (Supplementary Fig. 6). 225

MEDUSSA, an analytical pipeline for extracting cell size from fluorescent membrane im-231 

ages. 232 

We designed an algorithm to extract precise 2D cell dimensions—width and length—and extrap-233 

olate 3D measurements such as membrane surface area and volume (Fig. 4a). Starting from 234 

FMSeg-generated masks, we applied a distance transform to compute, for each pixel, the shortest 235 

distance to the mask periphery. We then overlaid a medial skeleton on this transform and sampled 236 

the radius along the skeleton to obtain cell width across the long axis. Radii at the skeleton end-237 

points were used to estimate the size of the hemispherical caps, yielding length and width profiles 238 

for each cell. Assuming rotational symmetry and imaging in the medial focal plane, we estimated 239 

volume and surface area by summing cylindrical segments along the skeleton and adding hemi-240 

spherical ends. Additional details are provided in the Methods section. 241

Integrating segmentation with size calculations revealed two key factors critical for reliable meas-243 

urements (Fig. 4b-d; Supplementary Fig. 7; Supplementary Fig. 8). The first is that the size of the 244 

segmentation masks is sensitive to the focal plane (Fig. 4b,c; Supplementary Fig. 7). For rod-245 

shaped cells, width is maximal at the mid-cell focal plane but decreases above and below it (Fig. 246 

4c) (Ursell et al., 2017). It is therefore critical to ensure that size measurements are taken from 247 

mid-cell focal planes. However, due to minor aberrations in microscopy slides, cells within a field 248 

of view often reside in slightly different focal planes (Fig. 4b; Supplementary Fig. 7). To address 249 

this, we acquired z-stacks and projected them onto a single plane before predicting deconvolution 250 

with FM2FM and segmentation (Fig. 4b). This approach yielded size estimations equivalent to 251 

those obtained from mid-cell focal planes (Fig. 4c), enabling accurate measurements across entire 252 

microscopy field, regardless of focal plane differences. 253

Second, we observed that the FMSeg model consistently generated masks slightly wider than 276 

ground truth masks (Fig. 4d; Supplementary Fig. 8a). This bias propagated into surface area and 277 

volume calculations (Supplementary Fig. 8b). Fortunately, the overestimation was independent of 278 

cell size, allowing us to apply a systematic correction (Fig. 4d; Supplementary Fig. 8b). We devel-279 

oped a size correction pipeline that allowed us to predict the “true” cell size based on the dimen-280 

sions of FMSeg-generated masks (Supplementary Fig. 8; see methods for details). We validated 281 

this approach by correcting the segmentation outputs for B. subtilis , B. thuringiensis and P. megaterium 282 

cells used in our benchmarking tests (Fig. 2a). As shown in Fig. 4d, FMSeg-mask width distribution 283 

was slightly shifted compared to that obtained from ground-truth masks generated using JFila-284 

ment. After applying the correction, both distributions became nearly identical (Fig. 4d). 285

To assess the accuracy of our cell size measurements, we compared estimates from our pipeline 287 

with those obtained from plunged-frozen B. subtilis imaged by cryo-electron microscopy (cryoEM), 288 

a technique that provides high-resolution images of cells preserved in their native physiological 289 

state. We focused on sporulating B. subtilis cells, as we could access sets of cryoEM images of these 290 

cells, kindly provided by Elitza Tocheva (Tocheva et al., 2013), and Elizabeth Villa and Kanika 291 

Khanna (Khanna et al., 2021; Khanna et al., 2019; Lopez-Garrido et al., 2018). To test robustness 292 

across different cell sizes, we measured both wild type cells and cells lacking PBP1 (Δ ponA ), which 293 

are ~25% thinner than wild type cells (Popham & Setlow, 1996). Our pipeline reproduced the 294 

difference in width between wild-type and Δ ponA cells observed by cryo-EM, although the abso-295 

lute width estimates were on average ~14% lower than those obtained by cryo-EM (Fig. 4e). This 296 

discrepancy may reflect a systematic bias in our fluorescence-based measurements, but it could 297

DISCUSSION 411 

412 

Here, we introduce MEDUSSA, an image-analysis pipeline that extracts accurate single-cell size 413 

measurements from fluorescent-membrane microscopy images, and demonstrate its utility for dis-414 

secting mechanisms of cell-size diversity among bacterial strains. The workflow combines deep-415 

learning–based image restoration for deconvolution prediction with automated segmentation to 416 

delineate cell boundaries from membrane signal, followed by mask analysis to estimate cell size. 417 

Because each step is modular, the restoration (FM2FM), segmentation (FMseg), and measurement 418 

components can be used independently to suit specific study needs (Supplementary Fig. 9). Ap-419 

plied across strains and species, MEDUSSA yields cell-size measurements that can be further in-420 

terrogated to identify underlying mechanisms. 421 

422 

MEDUSSA is well suited for comparative studies because it addresses key shortcomings of previ-423 

ous methods. First, fluorescent membrane imaging enables precise delineation of cell boundaries 424 

by tracking the peak membrane signal around each cell (Fig. 1). This supports consistent training-425 

set annotation and yields more reliable cell-size estimates than phase-contrast-based approaches 426 

(Hardo et al., 2024) (Fig. 1). Second, membrane dyes reveal septal membranes, allowing clear sep-427 

aration of daughter cells that remain attached after division and thus enabling reliable length meas-428 

urements for both isolated cells and chains—something that is difficult with phase contrast (Fig. 429 

1) (Syvertsson et al., 2016). Third, the pipeline corrects focal-plane differences among cells within 430 

the same image, which would otherwise bias measurements because focal-plane shifts alter mask 431 

size (Fig. 4b-c) (Ursell et al., 2017). Specifically, projecting fluorescence images from a z-stack and 432 

applying a deconvolution-prediction restoration algorithm (FM2FM) produces segmentation-433 

ready images that yield mid-focal-plane masks. Thus, this standardized protocol of restoration, 434 

segmentation, and measurement converts membrane-fluorescence images into population-level 435 

size distributions. 436 

437 

A trade-off is the need for fluorescent labeling. Several membrane and cell-wall dyes are routinely 438 

used in bacterial cell biology (Greenspan & Fowler, 1985; Herman et al., 1994; Kuru et al., 2012; 439 

Lucidi et al., 2024; Monteiro et al., 2015; Pogliano et al., 1999; Sharp & Pogliano, 1999). Most 440 

experiments presented here use FM 4-64, a lipophilic styryl fluorophore that associates with the 441 

outer leaflet of the cytoplasmic membrane without disrupting membrane function (Pogliano et al., 442 

1999). FM 4-64 is broadly applicable across bacterial species and compatible with live-cell imaging 443 

(Calvez et al., 2019; Dalebroux et al., 2015; Ma et al., 2009; Maloney et al., 2009; O'Hara et al., 444 

2017; Ojkic et al., 2016; Shin et al., 2015; Specht et al., 2013; Studer et al., 2016; Sun & Margolin, 445 

> .CC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 International license made available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted April 15, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.10.26.684635 doi: bioRxiv preprint

1998; Tsuge et al., 2008; Yeh et al., 2010). However, our segmentation model also works with 446 

other labels—including the membrane-permeable MitoTracker Green and with GFP fused to a 447 

membrane protein (Fig. 2d; Supplementary Fig. 4)—and, in principle, with any dye that provides 448 

uniform signal along the cell periphery. In addition, we provide a transformation model, FP2FM, 449 

that predicts membrane-like boundaries suitable for segmentation from cytoplasmic fluorescence, 450 

adding flexibility (Supplementary Fig. 6). Altogether, the available dyes, combined with the trans-451 

formation tools provided here and elsewhere (Spahn et al., 2022), extend the applicability of the 452 

MEDUSSA method to a broad range of species. For direct comparisons, we recommend using 453 

the same staining modality and image transformation procedures, because different labels or trans-454 

formation models may slightly affect size estimations. For example, FM 4-64 labels the outer leaflet 455 

of the cytoplasmic membrane, whereas GFP fused to a membrane protein is displaced toward the 456 

cytoplasmic side. Consequently, boundaries inferred from GFP-based signals may appear slightly 457 

inward relative to those inferred from FM 4-64, leading to systematic differences in estimated cell 458 

size (Supplementary Fig. 12). 459 

460 

Although MEDUSSA addresses several limitations associated with cell-size estimation from 461 

phase-contrast images, it does not overcome challenges inherent to the imaging of small bacterial 462 

cells by optical microscopy. A single pixel in a fluorescence microscopy image may amount to 10% 463 

of the width of a typical bacterial cell, so even minor segmentation imprecisions can translate into 464 

appreciable differences in measured size. For reliable comparisons between strains and replicates, 465 

it is therefore important to quantify sufficiently large numbers of cells, and compare the size dis-466 

tributions of the populations. Bacterial cell size also depends on growth conditions (Schaechter 467 

et al., 1958), and biological variation between cultures can be substantial depending on media and 468 

growth stage (Bakshi et al., 2021; Cylke & Banerjee, 2023; Kellogg & Levin, 2022). We therefore 469 

recommend to keep growth conditions as constant as possible to obtain biologically meaningful 470 

and reproducible results. 471 

472 

Segmentation accuracy also depends on image quality. In particular, images with low signal-to-473 

noise ratios can result in lower segmentation accuracy (Supplementary Fig. 13). To mitigate this 474 

limitation, we trained an additional CARE restoration model (FM2FM-HiSNR; see methods for 475 

details) that predicts high signal-to-noise deconvolved membranes across a range of input signal-476 

to-noise levels. Under our imaging conditions, restoration with this model improves the segmen-477 

tation quality of low signal-to-noise images (Supplementary Fig. 13), although extremely low signal 478 

levels will ultimately limit segmentation accuracy. 479

In addition, projection artifacts can lead to cell-size misestimation from fluorescent images, an 481 

effect that may be particularly pronounced for smaller cells (Hardo et al., 2024; Ursell et al., 2017). 482 

Hardo et al. reported marker-dependent biases, with membrane fluorescence tending to underes-483 

timate cell width and cytoplasmic fluorescence tending to overestimate it, and with proportionally 484 

larger effects in thinner cells (below ~1 μm) than in thicker cells. To probe for this effect under 485 

our imaging and analysis conditions, we compared cell width estimates derived from independently 486 

segmented membrane and cytoplasmic fluorescence across a controlled width range ( ponA under 487 

IPTG; Supplementary Fig. 14). If projection artifacts were prominent under our conditions, we 488 

would expect cytoplasmic-fluorescence-based estimates to be relatively larger than membrane-489 

based estimates for thin cells, with the discrepancy decreasing as cells become thicker. However, 490 

width scaled almost identically when estimated from membrane and cytoplasmic fluorescence 491 

across the tested width range (~0.6-1.2 μm) (Supplementary Fig. 14b). These results suggest that 492 

projection artifacts, as described by Hardo et al., do not measurably bias our width estimates under 493 

these conditions, although they may become more relevant in cells thinner than those examined 494 

here or under different imaging settings. 495 

In this study, we applied MEDUSSA to quantify the size of six strains of P. megaterium . Our 496 

measurements reveal remarkable diversity in cell size both among strains and among individual 497 

cells within a strain (Fig. 5). Although all strains produce comparatively large cells by bacterial 498 

standards, some produce cells that are, on average, over twofold larger in volume than others. We 499 

also observed that some strains formed extremely elongated cells during exponential growth, 500 

which were not observed in stationary phase. To our knowledge, filamentation has not been 501 

reported previously in P. megaterium —likely because membrane dyes are not routinely used in 502 

microscopy. The mechanism underlying this filamentation, and whether such filaments occur in 503 

nature or are artifacts of laboratory culture, remains to be determined. 504 

Cell width in bacteria is generally assumed to be tightly controlled within a species, with little cell-505 

to-cell variation in steady state cultures (Campos et al., 2014; Chien et al., 2012; Ojkic & Banerjee, 506 

2021; Ojkic et al., 2022; Sauls et al., 2019; Taheri-Araghi et al., 2015). Our dataset, however, reveals 507 

substantial width variation both within and between strains. Within strains, width values were 508 

approximately normally distributed, and some strains showed wide cell-to-cell variation; for 509 

example, in strain KM during stationary phase, the thinnest cells were up to twofold thinner than 510 

the thickest cells (Fig. 5). The coefficient of variation for width reached up to 0.15 (Supplementary 511

It is The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted April 15, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.10.26.684635 doi: bioRxiv preprint

Fig. 11)—more than twice that of B. subtilis —suggesting that mechanisms of width homeostasis 512 

in this bacterium are less precise than in B. subtilis (Nordholt et al., 2020; Sauls et al., 2019). 513 

Between strains, width distributions were shifted, with median widths ranging from ~0.9 to 1.4 514 

μm (Fig. 5; Supplementary Fig. 11a), indicating variation in width regulation across strains. We 515 

further examined differences between the thick strain DSM 319 and its Lac – derivative WH320, 516 

which is ~0.5 μm thinner. Genome comparison revealed an alanine-to-aspartic acid substitution 517 

in the transglycosylase domain of PBP1. An alignment of PBP1 sequences from 854 strains 518 

indicates that alanine at position 214 is largely conserved (~75%); ~24% carry serine at this 519 

position, and fewer than 1% contain other residues (e.g., cysteine, threonine, valine) 520 

(Supplementary Data in Zenodo repository). Notably, no sequence harbors an aspartic acid or any 521 

other charged residue at this position, suggesting that introducing charge there impairs PBP1 522 

activity. Because PBP1 mutants yield thinner cells in B. subtilis (Popham & Setlow, 1996), it is 523 

plausible that the hypomorphic PBP1 allele in P. megaterium WH320 contributes—at least in part— 524 

to its reduced width relative to the DSM 319 parental strain. 525 

Unlike the mutation in lacZ , the ponA mutation in P. megaterium WH320 is a G àT transversion. 526 

Because EMS typically induces G·C àA·T transitions (Sega, 1984), it is unlikely that the ponA 527 

mutation arose directly from EMS mutagenesis. Instead, we suspect that the ponA mutation in 528 

strain WH320 was either already present in the DSM 319 clone used for EMS treatment or was 529 

acquired during subsequent propagation of WH320 before we received the strain. 530 

Overall, these results illustrate how MEDUSSA can be used to explore within- and between-strain 531 

diversity in bacterial cell size and to reveal differences that can be pursued to identify underlying 532 

mechanisms. Future work will expand our strain set and survey additional species and 533 

environmental isolates to advance our understanding of the evolution of bacterial cell size and 534 

contribute to the emerging field of evolutionary cell biology (Jiang et al., 2015; Lynch et al., 2014). 535

single-cell segmentation for model training and improved robustness of the segmentation model 593 

to such confounding artifacts. 594

Extracting cell size information from masks of rod-shaped cells. 694 

For rod-shaped bacteria, we assumed a sphero-cylindrical geometry with hemispherical caps (Ojkic 695 

et al., 2019). First, we computed the minimum Euclidean distance from each pixel inside the mask 696 

to the closest pixel on the mask boundary (Borgefors, 1986), which is commonly referred to as the 697 

distance transform. Next, we generated a one-dimensional centerline (commonly known as “skel-698

eton”) (Zhang & Suen, 1984), overlaid it on the distance transform map, and determined the dis-699 

tance value at each skeleton pixel, thereby obtaining the local radius of the mask along the skeleton 700 

line (Fig. 4a). An alternative one-dimensional representation is the medial axis (Blum, 1967), also 701 

known as the “topological skeleton” (Supplementary Fig. 15). We compared size estimates ob-702 

tained with the two representations and found significant differences in some size metrics in a set 703 

of E. coli cells (Supplementary Fig. 15d). Inspection of individual masks revealed that some medial-704 

axis representations tended to over-branch (Supplementary Fig. 15e). If common across many 705 

cells, such over-branching would lead to inaccurate size estimates. We therefore proceeded with 706 

the skeleton representation. 707 

708 

To estimate the extend of the hemispherical caps, we took the distance between each skeleton 709 

endpoint and the corresponding mask tip as the cell radius at the skeleton endpoints. This yielded 710 

precise estimates of mask length and with profiles at every pixel along the skeleton. Assuming 711 

rotational symmetry and that the masks derive from medial focal-plane images, we estimated cell 712 

volume and surface area by adding the volumes and lateral surface areas of cylindrical segments 713 

generated by circularly projecting the radius of the mask at each pixel of the skeleton, plus the 714 

hemispherical caps (Fig. 4a). This approach accurately handles elongated masks, provides local 715 

morphological information of cell morphology, and is robust to cellular curvature. While tailored 716 

to rods, the framework can be adapted to other morphologies. 717

Dealing with branching skeletons 719 

Due to the mask geometry, it is possible that the skeleton branches into multiple endpoints (Sup-720 

plementary Fig. 15c). To handle both branched and non-branched cases, we developed the fol-721 

lowing workflow. First, we treat the skeleton as a graph, with each pixel considered a node. As-722 

suming connectivity only between neighboring pixels (horizontal, vertical, or diagonal), we define 723 

edges for pairs of pixels whose Euclidean distance is less than 1.5 pixels, thereby retaining con-724 

nected neighbors. Next, we implement a Depth-first search (DFS) algorithm to find the longest 725 

possible path connecting the opposite endpoints in the graph. If the skeleton is not branched, this 726 

path corresponds to the skeleton. For branched skeletons, multiple candidate paths exist. For each 727 

candidate path we compute the cell size metrics described in the next section and take the median 728 

of each metric as the cell size (Supplementary Fig. 15). 729

Measurement transformation 752 

We segmented 864 single cells of Escherichia coli stained with FM 4-64 using either JFilament 753 

(ground-truth) or FMseg. Cells were from early exponential phase (OD 600 = 0.1-0.3) cultures in 754

> .CC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 International license made available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted April 15, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.10.26.684635 doi: bioRxiv preprint

necessary parameters to transform the data between the automatic segmentation results to the 761 

ground truth results. A linear regression would be a direct way to do this, extracting the values for 762 

the slope ( m) and intercept ( n) necessary to transform the data. While linear regression returns a 763 

coefficient of error that would allow us to simulate distributions to sample m and n, there is the 764 

chance that we may over or under-estimate these values, as well as sample pairs that do not make 765 

sense in the context of our data. Thus, we leveraged the PYMC library (Abril-Pla et al., 2023) and 766 

its use of Bayesian statistics for sampling the necessary parameters, which are m, the fit slope, n,767 

the fit intercept, and σ, the standard deviation of our sampled observations. Since our data behaves 768 

linearly, we used a Bayesian Generalized Linear Model (GLM), assuming that our parameters 769 

(slope, intercept, size metric, and its standard deviation) follow certain types of distributions. For 770 

σ, we assume a Half-Cauchy distribution, which only returns positive, non-zero values, which is 771 

the only type of values a standard deviation can have. For m and n, we sample from normal distri-772 

butions with mean 0 and standard deviation 20, giving us a wide range of possible parameters. 773 

Then, for computing the observed values, we also assume a normal distribution. PYMC performs 774 

a series of draws to fit the data, and thus after successive draws we end with the distributions of 775 

m, n and σ. In our case, we performed four chains of 3000 draws each, giving us a total of 12000 776 

sampled values. We can then randomly sample pairs from these distributions in order to transform 777 

the data, as well as to include the uncertainty of the transformation in the analysis (Supplementary 778 

Figure 8b). For the examples shown in Figure 5, Figure 6, and Supplementary Figure 11, we sam-779 

pled 250 pairs of m and n for each individual cell measurement, thus allowing us to compute new 780 

metrics. 781
