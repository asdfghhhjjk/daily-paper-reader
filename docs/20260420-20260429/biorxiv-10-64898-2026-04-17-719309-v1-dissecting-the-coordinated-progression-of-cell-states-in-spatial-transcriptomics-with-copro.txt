Abstract 25 

Spatial transcriptomics enables the study of how cells coordinate their molecular states within 26 

tissue, providing insight into both normal function and disease processes. A key challenge is to 27 

identify gene expression programs that vary continuously across space and are coordinated 28 

between cell types. We present CoPro , a computational framework for detecting the spatially 29 

coordinated progression of cellular states. CoPro can operate in both supervised and unsupervised 30 

modes to identify gene programs that co-vary within or between cell types, and to disentangle 31 

multiple overlapping spatial patterns. CoPro can be applied to single-cell-level spatial 32 

transcriptomics datasets, including MERFISH, SeqFISH+, Xenium, and histology-imputed 33 

transcriptomic data. We demonstrate the utility of CoPro with data collected from colon, brain, 34 

liver, and kidney tissues. In the colon, CoPro separates epithelial differentiation along the crypt 35 

axis from spatially localized inflammatory signals. In the aging liver, it identifies multiple aging-36 

associated cellular programs superimposed on anatomical zonation. In the brain, the flexible kernel 37

Introduction 44 

Tissue function emerges from spatially coordinated interactions among neighboring cells, which 45 

shape one another’s molecular state through direct contact, secreted signals, and shared 46 

microenvironments. These interactions are not only fundamental to maintaining homeostasis, they 47 

also drive pathological processes. Spatial transcriptomics 1 offers an opportunity to study these 48 

interactions in situ, capturing the organization of cells into domains and microstructures that 49 

collectively determine tissue function and dysfunction. 50 

> 51

Most computational methods for analyzing spatial transcriptomics data focus on segmenting 52 

tissues into discrete spatial domains, often referred to as spatial niches 2. These domains are 53 

typically defined based on gene expression patterns 3, cell type composition 4–6 , cell-cell proximity 7,54 

or multimodal features 8. While this approach has been useful for initial data visualization and 55 

exploration, it may impose artificial categorization on what might be continuous spatial transitions. 56 

In this work, we show that, in many cases, discrete domains can be better described by the 57 

superposition of continuous axes of coordinated variation across cell types, such as differentiation 58 

gradients, metabolic zonation, and inflammatory responses, that can coexist within the same spatial 59 

neighborhood. We develop a framework to dissect these overlapping axes in a cell type-specific 60 

manner, resolving how distinct gene programs in different cell types co-progress along shared 61 

spatial gradients, with or without prior biological guidance. 62 

> 63

Many recent methods have begun to address continuous spatial variation. These include 64 

MERINGUE 9, DIALOGUE 10 , SpaceFlow 11 , ONTraC 12 , and GASTON 13 . However, these methods 65 

do not address the problem of identifying continuous, multi-axis spatial coordination of gene 66 

programs across multiple cell types at single-cell resolution. There remains a need for a 67 

computational framework that simultaneously preserves continuous single-cell spatial resolution, 68

> 71

Another key analytical goal of spatial transcriptomics is the integrative analysis of multiple 72 

samples with spatial information. Comparing patterns across samples, whether it is for the 73 

validation of findings or the elucidation of cross-sample heterogeneity, requires a principled 74 

approach to multiple-sample integration. However, most existing methods are designed for single-75 

sample analysis and do not explicitly support multi-sample settings 14,15 . Among methods that do 76 

accommodate multiple samples, many rely on spatial registration, assuming that tissue regions 77 

with similar histological appearance correspond to biologically comparable states 16–18 . CoPro 78 

relaxed this assumption and adopted an alternative approach by projecting all cells from different 79 

samples onto a biologically defined axis derived from shared gene expression programs. This 80 

approach enables interpretable cross-sample integration and reproducibility assessment in a 81 

manner that is robust to tissue morphology variation. We demonstrate the application with two 82 

examples, the first in a study of colon injury and the second in a study of liver aging. 83 

> 84

CoPro explicitly models and detects continuous coordinated progression between spatially 85 

proximal cells. CoPro can operate in both supervised and unsupervised modes. In its supervised 86 

form, the user specifies a gene program of interest in one cell type, and CoPro identifies gene 87 

programs in other cell types that spatially co-vary with it. In unsupervised mode, CoPro discovers 88 

axes of coordinated variation de novo across cell types. We demonstrate supervised CoPro in the 89 

kidney tissue, whereas unsupervised CoPro is used for all other tissues. 90

Overview of CoPro framework 93 

We assume as input a spatial transcriptomics dataset in which each cell or pixel has been assigned 94 

to a broad cell type, based on transcriptomic clustering, morphological features, or external 95 

annotations (Fig. 1a). CoPro then models the coordinated variation of molecular programs between 96 

these cell types across space, with the goal of identifying cell type-specific gene expression 97 

patterns that progress jointly between spatially proximal cells. 98

CoPro dissects spatial transcriptomic data into overlapping spatial gradients, enabling the 160 

separation of distinct but coexisting spatial programs within the same tissue ( Fig. 1d ). It supports 161 

both unsupervised and supervised modes. In the unsupervised setting, it identifies axes of spatial 162 

coordination de novo, using only the expression matrix and the spatial position of cells. In the 163 

supervised setting, the user can provide a known or inferred spatial trend for one cell type, and 164 

CoPro will identify gene programs in other cell types that are spatially aligned with it, thereby 165 

uncovering spatially coupled cellular programs across types ( Fig. 1e ). These complementary 166 

modes can also be combined; for example, using unsupervised discovery to identify candidate axes, 167 

followed by supervised refinement or transfer to new datasets ( Fig. 1f ). 168 

> 169

For cell types with well-defined differentiation gradients, CoPro’s inferred progression scores 170 

often recapitulate known expression-driven programs. However, its primary advantage lies in 171 

leveraging spatial context to uncover coordinated cellular transitions that would otherwise remain 172 

hidden. In particular, CoPro detects subtle or ambiguous transitions that lack sufficient signal for 173 

non-spatial approaches, and recovers spatial trends in rare or plastic cell types by borrowing 174 

information from their spatial coupling with more abundant populations. This cross-cell-type 175 

“spatial borrowing” is especially valuable in settings such as targeted gene panels, where gradient-176 

informative genes may be sparse, and enables coherent spatial trends to be identified and compared 177 

across datasets. 178 

> 179

By default, CoPro uses a Gaussian kernel with a bandwidth σ (the scale parameter controlling the 180 

effective range of spatial interactions) chosen to maximize the mean first-component kernel-181 

normalized correlation across cell-type pairs (Methods). In tissues with multiple overlapping 182 

spatial trends, CoPro iteratively identifies orthogonal axes of co-progression of decreasing 183 

correlation strength. This allows spatial transcriptomic data to be decomposed into a set of 184 

interpretable, non-redundant gradients. Each axis is defined by cell-type-specific gene weights and 185 

corresponding spatial progression scores, which can be visualized to reveal coordinated variation 186 

across the tissue. These axes provide a common coordinate system that can be used to align 187

Because the optimization in (1) may capture spurious correlations that arise by chance, we 193 

implement a permutation-based procedure to assess the statistical significance of each identified 194 

axis. Specifically, the tissue is partitioned into non-overlapping spatial patches (default side length 195 

equal to twice the kernel bandwidth σ), and for each cell type being tested, spatial bins are 196 

randomly shuffled and cells at each bin’s locations are replaced by same-type cells sampled from 197 

the mapped target bin, while spatial coordinates are held fixed. This patch-based design preserves 198 

within-cell-type spatial autocorrelation while breaking cross-cell-type spatial coupling, thereby 199 

generating an appropriate null distribution ( Methods ). 200

Benchmark analyses via simulations 202 

To place CoPro in the context of existing approaches, we compared its conceptual scope and 203 

capabilities to representative methods for spatial transcriptomic analysis ( Supplementary Table 204 

1). Domain clustering approaches identify discrete spatial niches; trajectory-based methods such 205 

as ONTraC infer continuous spatial ordering based on cell type composition; SpaceFlow identifies 206 

transcriptional gradients at the neighborhood level; and DIALOGUE identifies multi-cell-type 207 

programs but does not explicitly model spatial organization at single-cell resolution. CoPro 208 

addresses a distinct analytical need: the identification of spatially coupled continuous programs of 209 

co-progression across cell types. It resolves overlapping gradients rather than partitioning tissue 210 

into discrete regions, and incorporates spatial proximity directly into the optimization at single-211 

cell resolution. Because these methods address different analytical objectives, direct benchmarking 212 

is inherently limited: strong performance by one method on a task it was designed for does not 213 

imply superiority over methods designed for different goals. Accordingly, in the simulation studies 214 

below, we compare CoPro against methods with the most overlapping goals, specifically, the 215 

ability to estimate continuous spatial cell scores—while using Supplementary Table 1 to provide 216 

a broader conceptual comparison. We emphasize that benchmark results should be interpreted in 217 

light of each method's design intent. 218

> 219

Here, we designed a series of simulation studies based on realistic gene expression profiles from 220 

the Liver Cell Atlas 23 . These simulations assess CoPro's ability to recover known spatial patterns 221 

in scenarios of increasing complexity: two cell types with a single gradient, three cell types with 222 

varying proportions, and finally multiple orthogonal axes of tissue organization. 223 

> 224

Our simulation framework generates spatial transcriptomics data by assigning cells from real 225 

single-cell RNA-seq datasets to spatial locations under controlled patterns of spatial organization 226 

(Fig. 2a ; Methods ). Rather than simulating gene expression de novo, this framework preserves 227 

realistic gene expression levels and gene–gene correlations by using observed single-cell profiles, 228 

which are matched to spatial positions according to specified spatial gradients. This design allows 229 

explicit control over whether and how cell types exhibit coordinated progression across space. In 230 

particular, we can simulate null scenarios in which spatial autocorrelation is present within 231 

individual cell types but not shared across types (i.e. the absence of co-progression), as well as 232 

alternative scenarios in which two or more cell types are spatially coupled and co-progress together. 233 

By preserving the statistical structure of real transcriptomic data while systematically varying 234 

spatial coordination patterns, this framework enables controlled evaluation of methods across a 235 

wide range of settings. 236 

> 237

An important consideration for interpreting benchmark results is that each spatial simulation draws 238 

only a subset of cells from the full single-cell atlas, so the gene expression patterns in the spatial 239 

sample will inevitably deviate slightly from those in the original dataset. This sampling effect sets 240 

a natural ceiling on how well any method can recover the ground-truth gradient — even a perfect 241 

method operating on the sampled data cannot exceed the correlation between the gradient 242 

reconstructed from the sampled cells and the original gradient. We therefore benchmarked all 243 

methods against this theoretical performance ceiling rather than against the original gradient 244 

directly ( Methods ). 245 

> 246

We first evaluated methods in the simplest scenario involving two cell types with a single 247 

coordinated spatial gradient. Using hepatocytes and endothelial cells, we simulated spatial patterns 248 

where both cell types exhibited correlated spatial organization reflecting a shared tissue gradient. 249

After applying Gaussian kernel smoothing to generate spatially coherent patterns, we observed 250 

clear spatial structure in the smoothed cell scores, with cells clustered by their position along the 251 

gradient axis ( Fig. 2a ). Of note, unlike many existing simulation frameworks that assume a strong 252 

global gradient (e.g. a linear, blocked, or other smooth change across the entire tissue), our 253 

simulation strategy produces more complex, spatially localized patterns that are harder to dissect. 254 

> 255

In the two-cell-type simulations, we first evaluated CoPro’s performance with or without true 256 

coordinated progression between cells ( Fig. 2b ). Since CoPro always tries to find the optimal 257 

coordinated axis, its estimated correlation value is greater than zero even when there is no global 258 

co-progression. However, our patch-based permutation method yields the correct null distribution 259 

that successfully distinguishes real co-progression patterns from the null. When true coordinated 260 

progression exists, CoPro reliably recovers the underlying spatial gene expression gradient, even 261 

when there is no strong global gradient ( Fig. 2c ). In comparison, naïve permutation of spatial 262 

coordinates that do not account for within-cell-type autocorrelation fails to distinguish alternative 263 

from the null. 264 

> 265

We then compared CoPro's performance to DIALOGUE, SpaceFlow, ONTraC, and GASTON, 266 

which can each estimate a continuous spatial pattern ( Fig. 2c ). We note that DIALOGUE 267 

discretizes spatial coordinates into grids and identifies gene programs that are correlated across 268 

cell types within each grid cell. To ensure fair comparison, we optimized DIALOGUE's grid size 269 

parameter across a range of values, selecting the configuration that maximized performance for 270 

each simulation. In SpaceFlow and GASTON, there is no global hyperparameter that governs the 271 

spatial bandwidth for the continuous cell score inference. CoPro substantially outperformed other 272 

methods across five independent simulation runs (mean Pearson r = 0.93, Fig. 2c, Supplementary 273 

Table 2 ). DIALOGUE, SpaceFlow, and GASTON recovered substantially less of the ground-truth 274 

gradient — DIALOGUE tending to resolve overly fine-grained patterns and SpaceFlow and 275 

GASTON tending toward coarser ones. These results highlight CoPro's advantages in both 276 

accuracy and reliability for detecting spatially coordinated gene programs. 277

Complex tissues often exhibit multiple independent axes of organization. For example, the 292

Discussion 714 

> 715

We present CoPro, a computational framework for detecting spatially coordinated progression of 716 

cellular states across cell types in spatial transcriptomic data. Several distinguishing features make 717 

CoPro well-suited to questions that existing tools cannot readily address. First, CoPro operates at 718 

single-cell resolution and does not require cells of different types to be co-aggregated into shared 719 

spatial bins, which prevents data loss for rare or spatially sparse cell populations. Second, the 720 

spatial kernel-restricted CCA objective explicitly models intercellular coupling driven by spatial 721 

proximity, enabling CoPro to identify interpretable multi-dimensional tissue gradients. 722 

Importantly, CoPro adaptively selects the spatial kernel bandwidth from the data, allowing cell– 723 

cell interactions to be captured at the appropriate spatial scale without manual tuning. Third, the 724 

model and estimation procedure effectively separate multiple orthogonal axes of coordinated 725 

variation that overlap in space, as demonstrated by our decomposition of crypt morphology and 726 

acute injury responses in Day 3 colon, and of distinct aging-associated transcriptional programs in 727 

aged liver. Fourth, the transfer of gene weights from a reference condition to additional samples 728 

defines a common biological axis across samples without relying on spatial registration, making 729 

cross-sample comparison possible even in tissues with high morphological variability. 730 

> 731

Applying CoPro to four tissue types demonstrated these capabilities across diverse biological 732 

settings. In the mouse colon undergoing DSS-induced injury, CoPro recovered the epithelial crypt 733 

differentiation axis at homeostasis and an orthogonal acute early stress response for samples taken 734 

at early disease. Transferring the homeostatic crypt-villus gene weights to later disease timepoints 735 

allowed us to quantify the progressive disruption of intercellular spatial alignment during peak 736 

inflammation and its differential recovery at Day 21. Mapping samples taken during injury to a 737 

common CoPro axis allowed the quantification of disease severity across mice, validated by 738 

manual annotation. 739

In the liver, CoPro robustly recovered hepatocyte lobular zonation from histology-imputed 741 

transcriptomic data at 8-μm resolution—a setting where conventional clustering and trajectory 742

orthogonal paired-cell RNA-seq data. In aged mutant liver, CoPro decomposed three biologically 744 

distinct aging-associated programs superimposed on the canonical zonation axis and revealed that 745 

aging produces both the emergence of spatially structured senescence states and a progressive 746 

blurring of zonal boundaries. 747 

> 748

In the brain, CoPro enabled the dissection of overlapping spatial gradients that are difficult to 749 

resolve using existing approaches. In the striatum, where both D1 and D2 medium spiny neurons 750 

exhibit continuous transcriptional variation, standard dimensionality reduction and spatial methods 751 

tend to recover a single dominant gradient. By leveraging flexible kernel design, CoPro 752 

decomposed this variation into distinct dorsal–ventral and medial–lateral axes, each associated 753 

with coherent and biologically interpretable gene programs. These results demonstrate that CoPro 754 

can resolve multiple, spatially overlapping axes of organization within the same cell populations, 755 

providing a more nuanced view of brain tissue architecture than approaches that collapse variation 756 

into a single manifold. 757 

> 758

In the kidney, CoPro’s supervised mode enabled the inference of coordinated transcriptional 759 

programs across anatomically linked but transcriptionally distinct cell types. Using the known 760 

ordering of nephron segments as a reference axis, CoPro identified a corresponding vascular 761 

program that co-progresses with epithelial position along the corticomedullary axis. This analysis 762 

recovered the full continuum of vascular specialization from cortical to medullary endothelium 763 

without requiring prior annotation of vascular subtypes. Notably, the inferred vascular axis 764 

recapitulated known molecular markers established through extensive experimental studies, 765 

demonstrating that CoPro can leverage spatial coupling to infer coordinated molecular transitions 766 

across cell types. 767 

> 768

Analysis of the colon injury dataset also illustrates the key design choices underlying CoPro's 769 

multi-sample integration strategy. For biological or technical replicates with limited sample-to-770 

sample variation, multiple samples can be jointly analyzed to identify shared axes of spatially-771

When substantial sample-to-sample variation is expected, e.g. differing disease severity across 773

be transferred to the remaining samples to define a common interpretable axis. This shared axis 775

> 778

We acknowledge that the transfer-based integration strategy has inherent limitations. By projecting 779 

all samples onto a common axis derived from a single reference, the approach may obscure tissue-780 

specific patterns that exist in individual samples but deviate from the reference program. The 781 

transfer strategy anchors integration to biologically meaningful gene weights rather than raw 782 

expression values, but at the cost of imposing a common reference frame on all samples. More 783 

broadly, CoPro inherits limitations common to linear factor models: the CCA objective captures 784 

linear co-variation, so strongly nonlinear co-progression patterns may require future extensions. 785 

Additionally, the quality of the recovered axes depends on the accuracy of input cell type 786 

annotations, and computational cost scales with the number of cell pairs in the kernel, which may 787 

require subsampling strategies for very large datasets. 788 

> 789

The CoPro framework is not inherently limited to transcriptomic features. The spatial kernel-790 

restricted CCA objective operates on any low-dimensional cell-level representation, and could in 791 

principle be applied to features extracted from other data modalities that are defined at cellular or 792 

spatial resolution. One particularly promising direction is the direct application of CoPro to 793 

morphological features derived from H&E-stained tissue images. Computational pathology 794 

methods increasingly produce high-quality, spatially resolved cellular embeddings from routine 795 

histology slides, and CoPro could leverage these embeddings to detect coordinated morphological 796 

progression across cell types without requiring any transcriptomic measurement. This would open 797 

the framework to the vastly larger body of existing H&E data and could serve as a complement or 798 

precursor to spatial transcriptomic profiling. Extending CoPro to such modalities, and to settings 799 

combining morphological and transcriptomic features in a single analysis, represents a natural 800 

avenue of interest for future work. 801

Permutation-based significance testing 1021 

To assess statistical significance of each canonical axis, CoPro implements a spatial permutation 1022 

procedure. The tissue is partitioned into non-overlapping spatial bins (default side length equal to 1023 

twice the kernel bandwidth σ). Bins are then randomly shuffled, and for each cell type being tested, 1024 

the cells at each bin’s locations are replaced by same-type cells sampled from the mapped target 1025 

bin, while spatial coordinates remain fixed. This preserves cell-type identity and coarse spatial 1026 

structure at the bin scale while disrupting the fine-grained spatial coupling between cell types that 1027 

CoPro detects. The kernel-restricted CCA is re-estimated on the permuted data to build a null 1028 

distribution, and p-values are computed as the fraction of permutation replicates exceeding the 1029 

observed normalized correlation. 1030

orientation of components within each module. Module zonation profiles were visualized by 1324

profiles were summarized as the mean NES across module members, with standard errors and one-1326
