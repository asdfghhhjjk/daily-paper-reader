Abstract. Semantic segmentation of histopathology images under class imbalance is typically addressed through frequency-based loss reweight-ing, which implicitly assumes that rare classes are difficult. However, true difficulty also arises from morphological variability, boundary am-biguity, and contextual similarity—factors that frequency cannot cap-ture. We propose Dynamic Focal Attention (DFA), a simple and efficient mechanism that learns class-specific difficulty directly within the cross-attention of query-based mask decoders. DFA introduces a learnable per-class bias to attention logits, enabling representation-level reweighting prior to prediction rather than gradient-level reweighting after predic-tion. Initialised from a log-frequency prior to prevent gradient starva-tion, the bias is optimised end-to-end, allowing the model to adaptively capture difficulty signals through training, effectively unifying frequency-based and difficulty-aware approaches under a common attention-bias framework. On three histopathology benchmarks (BDSA, BCSS, CRAG), DFA consistently improves Dice and IoU, matching or exceeding a difficulty-aware baseline without a separate estimator or additional training stage. These results demonstrate that encoding class difficulty at the represen-tation level provides a principled alternative to conventional loss reweight-ing for imbalanced segmentation. 

Keywords: Class imbalance · Attention mechanism · Pathology seg-mentation · Transformer · Difficulty-aware learning 

## 1 Introduction 

Semantic segmentation of histopathology images underpins tissue characteri-sation, tumour delineation, and biomarker extraction in computational pathol-ogy [3,16,12]. Transformer-based architectures [7,6] achieve strong overall perfor-mance yet consistently underperform on clinically critical classes—a gap widely attributed to class imbalance. However, imbalance alone is incomplete: difficulty is also governed by morphological variability, boundary ambiguity, and contex-tual similarity, factors largely orthogonal to class frequency.

Limitations of existing approaches. Data-level strategies [2,14] alter train-ing distributions without introducing semantic information, while loss-level meth-ods [15,21,8,20] reweight gradient magnitudes after prediction, only once class-specific representations have already been formed. All assume rarity reliably proxies difficulty, yet these factors are largely orthogonal to pixel frequency [2,11] (Section 2.2). Critically, none can direct additional representational capacity to difficult classes during feature aggregation itself .

Attention as a principled intervention point. SAM [13] and SAM-Path [22,5] reformulate segmentation as mask classification via learnable class queries inter-acting with image features through cross-attention [4,6], directly shaping per-class feature aggregation—a natural site for difficulty-aware intervention. Yet standard attention treats all queries uniformly. Structured logit biases [19,18,23] are effective when task-specific priors are available, but none encode class-level semantic difficulty , which is crucial for addressing the diverse challenges in histopathology segmentation. 

Our approach. We propose Dynamic Focal Attention (DFA) , which introduces a learnable class-specific scalar bias bc into cross-attention logits (Fig. 1), enabling difficulty-aware feature aggregation at the representation level. Rather than reweighting gradients after prediction, DFA provides class-conditional logit adjustment within attention, modulating how strongly features are aggre-gated for each class. To prevent early gradient starvation on rare classes, we initialise bc using a centred log-frequency prior; thereafter the bias is updated via the segmentation objective to emphasise more on difficult-to-segment classes. We additionally formalise two variants—class-frequency (CFFA) and hard-class (HCFA)—forming a progression from static to dynamic difficulty estimation. 2.2 The Frequency–Difficulty Disconnect 

Frequency-based class-balancing assumes pixel frequency reliably reflects seg-mentation difficulty, yet empirical evidence across all three benchmarks con-tradicts this assumption (Fig. 2). In BCSS, Inflammatory and Necrosis share near-identical frequency yet differ by 22 .0 Dice points; Other—the most fre-quent minority class—scores lower than the rarer Necrosis ( 71 .8% vs. 77 .4% ), directly inverting the rarity–difficulty ranking. In BDSA, the rarest class (Su-perficial Cortex, 3.0% ) outperforms the more frequent Leptomeninges ( 6.4% ) by 

13 .2 Dice points. Only CRAG, with near-balanced classes, falls where frequency loosely correlates with difficulty. These observations demonstrate that true difficulty is governed by morpho-logical variability, boundary ambiguity, and inter-class contextual confusion [2], which are largely independent of pixel frequency—motivating learning class dif-ficulty directly from optimisation dynamics.

## 3 Experiments and Results 

3.1 Experimental Setup Datasets. We evaluate on three histopathology benchmarks: ( i) BCSS [1]: breast cancer, five classes (Tumor, Stroma, Inflammatory, Necrosis, Other), 40 ×,

1024 2 patches; ( ii ) BDSA [9]: brain tissue, five classes (Background, Gray Mat-ter, White Matter, Leptomeninges, Superficial Cortex), 10 ×, 1024 2; ( iii ) CRAG [10]: colorectal adenocarcinoma, two classes (Non-gland, Gland), 20 ×, 1536 2.

Architecture and training. All models use SAM-Path with ViT-B (SAM en-coder) and HIPT (pathology encoder), trained with AdamW ( η = 10 −4, batch 4,cosine annealing) for 30 /20 /60 epochs on BCSS/BDSA/CRAG on NVIDIA A100 GPUs. Hyperparameters: γ = 2 for all focal variants; α = 0 .25 , μ = 0 .0625 on BCSS/BDSA and α = 0 .125 , μ = 0 on CRAG (SAM-Path defaults); β = 0 .8,6 Lakmali Nadeesha Kumari and Sen-Ching Samson Cheung 

λ = 10 −4, ηb = 10 −2 for DFA. All were fixed across datasets after a small grid search on BCSS, and we observe stable performance without extensive tuning. 

Baselines. We compare five configurations that differ only in attention bias: (i) Base w/o FL : Eq. 7 without focal term; ( ii ) Base w/ FL : SAM-Path default (Eq. 7); ( iii ) CFFA : frequency-guided (Eq. 2); ( iv ) HCFA : two-stage hard-class (Eq. 3); ( v) DFA (Ours) : single-stage dynamic (Eq. 4).

BCSS. DFA achieves the highest mean Dice ( 0.7826 ), surpassing the focal-loss baseline by +4 .9 points and HCFA by +0 .5. Inflammatory improves by +15 .2

points over the baseline w/o FL, confirming DFA targets true difficulty rather than frequency; it also yields the best Dice on Other ( 0.7581 ), whose difficulty is underestimated by frequency-based methods. 

BDSA. Without focal loss the base model collapses on Leptomeninges and Superficial Cortex (Dice = 0 .000 ); focal loss recovers them to 0.6191 and 0.7508 .DFA further improves both to 0.6908 and 0.7808 , and achieves the best White Matter score ( 0.9556 ). 

CRAG. Even when imbalance is minimal, DFA achieves the highest mean Dice ( 0.8858 ), outperforming HCFA by +0 .5 points, demonstrating that DFA captures difficulty structure beyond frequency. 

Efficiency. As a single-stage method, DFA reduces wall-clock training time by approximately 40% relative to two-stage HCFA while matching or exceeding its Dice on all benchmarks. 

3.3 Analysis of Learned Attention Biases 

The learned biases δ at convergence (Fig. 3) provide a model-derived diffi-culty signal: hard classes acquire positive bias (e.g., Inflammatory +0 .410 ; Lep-tomeninges +0 .434 ) while easy classes acquire negative bias (e.g., Tumor −0.524 ;Background −0.720 ), with scatter plots confirming a strong negative correlation between baseline Dice and δc. Crucially, biases diverge from frequency-based initialisation: Inflammatory ( fc = 7 .8% ) receives +0 .410 vs. Necrosis +0 .068 de-spite near-identical frequency, closely reflecting their 22 .0-point Dice gap. This suggests δc captures sources of difficulty such as boundary complexity and con-textual confusion that cannot be inferred from class frequency alone.

## 5 Conclusion 

These results confirm that focal loss alone is insufficient, and frequency-based reweighting is unreliable: CFFA fails when rare classes are not inherently hard, and HCFA requires a costly pre-training stage. DFA resolves all three by learning class-specific difficulty continuously within cross-attention, consistently outper-forming all baselines across three benchmarks with only C additional scalar parameters. The warm-start prior prevents gradient starvation, the self-gating factor provides implicit curriculum regulation, and encoding difficulty at the representation level yields model-wide improvements over post-hoc gradient cor-rections.
