Abstract 24 

Centrosome abnormalities (CA) are a hallmark of epithelial cancers, yet their spatial complexity and 25 

phenotypic heterogeneity remain poorly resolved due to limitations in conventional image analysis. 26 

We present CenSegNet (Centrosome Segmentation Network), a modular deep learning framework 27 

for high-resolution, context-aware segmentation of centrosomes and epithelial architecture across 28 

diverse tissue types. Integrating a dual-branch architecture with uncertainty-guided refinement, 29 

CenSegNet achieves state-of-the-art performance and generalisability across both 30 

immunofluorescence and immunohistochemistry modalities, outperforming existing models in 31 

accuracy and morphological fidelity. Applied to tissue microarrays (TMAs) containing 911 breast 32 

cancer sample cores from 127 patients, CenSegNet enables the first large-scale, spatially resolved 33 

quantification of numerical and structural CA at single-cell resolution. These CA subtypes are 34 

mechanistically uncoupled, exhibiting distinct spatial distributions, age-dependent dynamics, and 35 

associations with histological tumour grade, hormone receptor status, genomic alterations, and 36 

nodal involvement. Structural CA levels are additionally associated with overall survival, supporting 37 

the clinical relevance of spatially resolved CA patterns. Discordant CA profiles at tumour margins are 38 

linked to local aggressiveness and stromal remodelling. To support broad adoption and 39 

reproducibility, CenSegNet is released as an open-source Python library. Together, our findings 40 

establish CenSegNet as a scalable, generalisable platform for spatially resolved centrosome 41 

phenotyping in intact tissues, enabling systematic dissection of the biology of this organelle and its 42 

dysregulation in cancer and other epithelial diseases. 43

Introduction 45 

Centrosomes, composed of a pair of orthogonally arranged centrioles surrounded by pericentriolar 46 

material (PCM), function as the principal microtubule-organising centres (MTOCs) in animal cells. 47 

They play essential roles in diverse cellular processes, including vesicular trafficking, cell polarity, 48 

motility, ciliogenesis, and the assembly of a bipolar mitotic spindle during cell division 1, 2 . Centrosome 49 

number and size are tightly regulated during the cell cycle, with duplication occurring once per cell 50 

cycle during S phase, ensuring the formation of the mitotic spindle and equal inheritance of 51 

chromosomes by daughter cells 3-5 .52 

Centrosome abnormalities (CA) can lead to multipolar spindle formation, chromosomal 53 

missegregation, and aneuploidy 3, 6, 7 —a hallmark of cancer 3, 4, 7 . The hypothesis that CA-induced 54 

aneuploidy contributes to tumorigenesis was first proposed by Theodor Boveri over a century ago 8.55 

In recent years, CA have been documented in several solid tumours including breast, prostate, colon, 56 

ovarian, and pancreatic cancers 3, 7, 9-13 , as well as haematological malignancies such as multiple 57 

myeloma, lymphomas, and leukaemias 14, 15 . While its role in tumour initiation remains debated 6, 9, 16-

, CA are consistently associated with aggressive disease features, including high-grade histology, 59 

poor prognosis, recurrence, and metastasis 3, 7, 9, 19 . Despite their clinical relevance, CA remain poorly 60 

characterised at scale due to the lack of robust, high-throughput tools capable of resolving 61 

centrosome phenotypes in complex tissue architecture. 62 

Mechanistically, CA can arise from both numerical and structural centrosome defects. Numerical 63 

amplification results from centriole overduplication, de novo centriole assembly, cytokinesis failure, 64 

mitotic slippage and cell–cell fusion 20-29 . Disruption of cell-cycle progression, such as prolonged G2 65 

arrest, can trigger premature centriole disengagement and reduplication via PLK1 activation 30, 31 .66 

Fragmentation of the PCM, driven by dysregulation of proteins such as pericentrin, γ-tubulin, PLK4, 67 

PLK1, and Aurora-A, also contributes to numerical CA 7, 32-34 . Structural CA, on the other hand, involve 68 

aberrant accumulation of PCM 4, 7, 9, 35, 36 , centrosome clustering 3, 9, or defects in centriole 69

Manual centrosome annotation remains the standard but is time-consuming, low-throughput, and 74

pipeline integrating both structural and numerical CA 7, 43 . Similarly, a 3D imaging-based pipeline 82 

quantified structural centriole abnormalities across cancer types 37 . However, these approaches 83 

require manual curation and offer only moderate throughput. A recent high-throughput platform using 84

tissues 44 , yet lacked single-cell resolution and subtype discrimination. Moreover, most existing tools 86

Development of CenSegNet for robust centrosome segmentation across imaging modalities 121 

We generated tissue microarrays (TMAs) comprising 911 breast tissue cores from normal breast 122 

tissue, breast tumours, and adjacent areas, sampled from 127 patients enrolled in the ethically 123 

approved and clinically well-characterised BeGIN cohort (Investigating Outcomes from Breast 124 

Cancer: Correlating Genetic, Immunological and Nutritional Predictors), from University Hospital 125 

Southampton (UHS) ( Fig. 1a ; see Methods). Immunohistochemistry was performed using pericentrin 126 

antibody to label centrosomes’ PCM, with haematoxylin counterstaining for nuclei ( Fig. 1a ). For 127 

training dataset construction, we manually annotated 14,679 centrosomes within 2,486 epithelial 128 

and 108 stromal compartments across 108 selected images ( Fig. 1a, see Methods). Despite 129 

extensive optimisation, co-labelling of TMAs for the centriolar protein GT335 failed to produce 130 

reliable or specific signal. This limitation highlights well-recognised challenges associated with 131 

detecting centriolar markers in paraffin-embedded tissue sections using chromogenic 132 

immunohistochemistry. To address this constraint, we complemented the TMA-based 133 

immunohistochemistry workflow with immunofluorescence coupled to high -resolution imaging. 134 

These analyses were performed on human MECs using confocal and Lightning -based computational 135 

super -resolution imaging on the Leica STELLARIS platform (see Methods), and on mouse mammary 136 

epithelium using confocal imaging, with pericentrin and GT335 used to label centrosomes and 137 

centrioles, respectively, and DAPI employed as a nuclear counterstain. From this, an 138 

immunofluorescence training dataset was assembled comprising 1,285 annotated centrosomes 139 

from 143 cells from mouse tissue and 841 human MECs, revealing strong segmentation 140 

concordance between pericentrin and GT335 labelling [human MECs: R 2 = 0.9666 (MCF10A), 141 

0.9085 (MCF10A-PLK4), 0.9995 (MCF10DCIS); mouse tissue: R2 = 0.9954] (Fig. 1b, 142 

Supplementary Fig. 1a–h ). We did not detect acentriolar MOTCs that are only labelled for 143 

pericentrin. Importantly, as detailed in the Methods section, were further independently validated by 144 

two annotators with complementary expertise in cell biology (annotator 1) and computational 145

Using these datasets, we initially benchmarked established segmentation models. To ensure the 153 

models receive appropriately normalised inputs for immunohistochemistry and immunofluorescence 154 

imaging modalities—thereby improving generalisability and minimising modality -driven bias—we 155 

adopted a modality-specific calibration strategy (see Methods). U-Net, an encoder–decoder 156 

convolutional neural network optimised for pixel-wise segmentation 46 , was selected for its extensive 157 

use in biomedical imaging. In our datasets, U-Net detected 82.98% of centrosomes in 158 

immunohistochemistry images and 97.6% in immunofluorescence, but despite achieving an overall 159 

F1 score of 0.85 in immunofluorescence, often the model either under-predicted or over-predicted 160 

centrosomes ( Supplementary Fig. 3a, b ). We next evaluated SegNet ( Fig. 1c ), another encoder-161 

decoder model leveraging max-pooling indices for efficient upsampling 48 . It achieved 72.73% and 162 

85% detection in immunohistochemistry and immunofluorescence, respectively, with a precision of 163 

0.90 but reduced recall (0.75) in immunofluorescence and a suboptimal overall F1 score of 0.68 in 164 

immunohistochemistry (0.82 in immunofluorescence) (Supplementary Fig. 3c, d). We also 165 

evaluated DeepLabv3+, a semantic segmentation model utilising atrous convolutions and atrous 166 

spatial pyramid pooling for multi-scale contextual information 49 . Despite its proven success in 167 

complex image domains, DeepLabv3+ achieved only 63.25% and 75.1% detection rates in 168 

immunohistochemistry and immunofluorescence, respectively ( Supplementary Fig. 3e, f ). These 169 

findings highlight the limitations of conventional segmentation pipelines when applied to structurally 170 

heterogeneous tissues. 171

8To overcome these constraints, we developed CenSegNet, a novel modular framework that 172 

integrates object robust detection and segmentation ( Fig. 1c ). Rather than relying on whole-slide 173 

segmentation, CenSegNet focuses on single-cell regions while modelling the entire ROI, explicitly 174 

integrating object detection with region-based segmentation in a three-step architecture ( Fig. 1c ). 175 

This design enables a more comprehensive capture of centrosome spatial distributions and 176 

morphological features, addressing key challenges such as occlusion, small object detection, and 177 

the disambiguation of overlapping structures. The framework, first employs YOLOv11 45 , a state-of-178 

the-art object detection model, to identify potential centrosome candidates. YOLOv11 comprises a 179 

convolutional backbone for feature extraction, a neck for multi-scale aggregation, and a head for 180 

classification and localisation. We fine-tuned YOLOv11-seg 45, 50 , a segmentation-optimised variant 181 

to further enhance detection accuracy, and applied a range of data augmentations—including hue, 182 

saturation, and value (HSV) adjustments, as well as geometric transformations such as translation, 183 

scaling, shearing, and horizontal flipping—to enhance generalisability ( Fig. 1c ; see Methods). To 184 

refine segmentation, we integrated U-Net in the second step. For all detected centrosomes, we 185 

extracted centrosome-centred patches (256 × 256 pixels, with 40-pixel padding) as input to a U-Net 186 

skip-connected encoder-decoder architecture for precise delineation. These patches underwent 187 

similar augmentation strategies as in the detection step, ensuring consistency. To accurately quantify 188 

centrosome numbers per cell, we incorporated StarDist 47 in the third step, a deep learning based 189 

instance segmentation tool widely adopted for nuclear and cell boundary segmentation in biomedical 190 

imaging ( Fig. 1c ). In our immunohistochemistry datasets, StarDist segmented 764,354 cells 191 

(Supplementary Fig. 1i ). To validate StarDist-aided cell-level centrosome assignment in complex 192 

tissues, we analysed keratin 8 (KRT8)-labelled mouse immunofluorescence (620 cells) and human 193 

immunohistochemistry (620 cells) tissues. StarDist-based segmentation demonstrated strong 194 

concordance with KRT8 (immunofluorescence: R 2 = 0.99; immunohistochemistry: R 2 = 0.9005) (see 195 

Supplementary Fig. 1g-k ). 196

Validation of CenSegNet for scalable and high-precision centrosome segmentation 202 

To evaluate CenSegNet’s performance, we compiled independent test datasets of 25 203 

immunohistochemistry TMA cores and 17 immunofluorescence images. Predicted centrosome 204 

counts were compared to manually annotated ground truth, revealing strong correlations in both 205 

immunohistochemistry (R 2 = 0.9999) and immunofluorescence (R 2 = 0.9873) (Fig. 2a–c ). 206 

CenSegNet consistently outperformed U-Net, SegNet, and DeepLabv3+ in segmentation accuracy 207 

and boundary resolution across both modalities, with the YOLOv11 detection module significantly 208 

enhancing overall performance ( Fig. 2a–c ). To further assess precision, we used an independently 209 

annotated subset of 550 centrosomes from tumour regions in the immunohistochemistry test set. 210 

Again, CenSegNet demonstrated superior pixel-level segmentation compared to all benchmarks 211 

(Fig. 2d ). F1 score analysis across modalities yielded a mean of 0.82 for CenSegNet, outperforming 212 

U-Net (0.72), SegNet (0.68), and DeepLabv3+ (0.65) (Fig. 2a–e ). We next benchmarked 213 

performance on 6,475 expert-annotated centrosomes—921 from normal tissue, 2,694 from edge 214 

regions, and 2,860 from tumour cores ( Fig. 2f ). These annotations, stratified by size (0.5–1.0 μm 2 to 215 

>10.5 μm 2), were compared with the full cohort of 333,148 automatically segmented centrosomes 216 

(0–0.5 μm 2 to >12.0 μm 2)—10,834 from normal tissue, 91,803 from edge tissue, and 230,511 from 217 

tumour tissue ( Fig. 2g ). Both datasets revealed similar size distributions, with the majority falling 218 

within 2.0–3.0 μm 2 and a peak at 2.0–2.5 μm 2. Normal tissue showed a higher proportion of 1.0–2.0 219 

μm 2 centrosomes, while edge regions had more large centrosomes than either normal or tumour 220 

cores. Using a previously established threshold of 6.5 μm 2-size to define structural CA in human 221

We further assessed the sensitivity of segmentation performance to the choice of detection and 224 

segmentation thresholds across four representative image types: immunohistochemistry, confocal 225 

immunofluorescence, lightning-based super-resolution immunofluorescence, and haematoxylin-226 

stained images. For each image type, either the detection or segmentation threshold was varied 227 

from 0.1 to 0.8 while the other threshold was fixed at 0.15 (default value), and performance was 228 

quantified using F1 score, IoU and Cohen’s κ ( Fig 3 ). Across all modalities, CenSegNet exhibited 229 

robust performance over varying threshold values. In immunohistochemistry images, performance 230 

remained stable as the detection threshold increased from 0.1 to 0.5, with reductions observed only 231 

at higher thresholds ( Fig. 3a, b ). When the segmentation threshold was varied, F1 score, IoU, and 232 

Cohen’s κ remained stable across the tested range ( Fig. 3a, c ). A similar pattern was observed for 233 

both confocal ( Fig. 3d-f ) and lightning-based super -resolution ( Fig. 3g-i ) immunofluorescence 234 

images. F1 score and Cohen’s κ remained stable across detection thresholds from 0.1 to 0.8, with 235 

only minor fluctuations in IoU ( Fig. 3d, e, g, h ), while variation of the segmentation threshold over 236 

the same range had little effect on performance ( Fig. 3d, f, g, i ), indicating that segmentation 237 

accuracy was not sensitive to threshold selection. For epithelial segmentation in 238 

haematoxylin -stained images, performance was stable across detection thresholds from 0.1 to 0.3 239 

and decreased at higher values ( Fig. 3j, k ). Similarly, segmentation performance remained stable 240 

when the segmentation threshold was varied from 0.1 to 0.4, with decreases observed only at larger 241 

values ( Fig. 3j, l ). Notably, the default threshold of 0.15 consistently fell within the stable performance 242 

range across all evaluated image types and metrics. Together, these validation analyses establish 243 

CenSegNet as a scalable, high -precision framework for centrosome segmentation that delivers 244 

robust and consistent performance across imaging modalities, tissue compartments, and size 245 

distributions, supporting its applicability to high -throughput analysis in complex tissue environments. 246

CenSegNet reveals orthogonal and spatially organised numerical and structural CA in breast 248 

cancer 249 

Our robust orthogonal validation above demonstrating that CenSegNet segments bona fide 250 

centrosomes provides a grounded rationale for high -throughput quantitative analyses distinguishing 251 

between numerical CA (Num CA; increased number of centrosomes per cell) and structural CA (Stru 252 

CA; altered size and/or organisation of the PCM) based on pericentrin labelling in our 253 

immunohistochemistry TMA data, to dissect their relative contributions in breast cancer. Num CA 254 

and Stru CA were not correlated within either tumour edge or core regions ( Supplementary Fig. 255 

4a ), suggesting mechanistic independence. At the single-cell level, increasing centrosome number 256 

per cell was associated with a reduction in the size of individual centrosomes ( Supplementary Fig. 257 

4b ), which become smaller, more uniform in cells containing >4 centrosomes—implying a258 

compensatory constraint on total centrosome volume. These observations suggest that Stru CA and 259 

Num CA represent orthogonal axes of centrosome dysregulation. In contrast, Num CA levels 260 

correlated positively between edge and tumour compartments (R 2 = 0.4857; Supplementary Fig. 261 

4c ), indicating progressive numerical amplification from tumour margins inward. This was reflected 262 

in an increasing proportion of cells with ≥4 centrosomes from normal tissue to the edge and tumour 263 

core ( Fig. 4a ). Stratifying patients into four CA phenotypes (Stru ⁻Num ⁻, Stru ⁺Num ⁺, Stru ⁺Num ⁻,264 

Stru ⁻Num ⁺) revealed that CA was widespread, detected in 89.8% of edge and 95% of tumour regions 265 

(Supplementary Fig. 4d ). Notably, 73% of tumour regions exhibited both Stru CA and Num CA, 266 

representing a ~19.3% increase compared to edge regions ( Supplementary Fig. 4d ). While the 267 

Stru ⁺Num ⁻ group accounted for 18.4% of edge regions but only 2% of tumour regions, the opposite 268 

trend was observed for the Stru ⁻Num ⁺ group (10.2% versus 20%, respectively). These findings allow 269 

us to conclude that structural and numerical CA are mechanistically uncoupled and highlight a spatial 270 

shift from centrosome enlargement at the tumour edge to centrosome accumulation in the tumour 271 

core. 272

These findings prompted us to consider whether enlarged centrosomal structures might reflect 303 

centrosome clustering, a process essential for the survival of cancer cells by which extra 304 

centrosomes assemble to form the two poles of the mitotic spindle 3, 9. Consistent with this, 305 

centrosome clustering in our immunohistochemistry data would be expected to manifest as large, 306 

irregular pericentrin -labelled PCM regions with internal intensity heterogeneity, indicative of multiple 307 

centrosomes in close proximity 52, 53 . Instead, our morphometric analyses show that circularity and 308 

solidity were largely maintained across the range of centrosome sizes. Circularity and solidity did not 309 

correlate significantly with centrosome size ( Supplementary Fig. 6a ), and enlarged centrosomes 310 

did not differ significantly from smaller centrosomes in these parameters ( Supplementary Fig. 6b ). 311 

These findings indicate that the majority of enlarged centrosomal foci observed in our tissue samples 312

Discussion 507 

We introduce CenSegNet, a modular deep learning framework for quantification of centrosomes in 508 

epithelial tissues at spatial and single-cell resolution. Unlike previous approaches that treat 509 

morphological analysis and segmentation as separate tasks, CenSegNet integrates centrosome 510 

detection and phenotyping, nuclear localisation, and epithelial boundary inference into a unified, 511 

multichannel pipeline. This enables context-aware segmentation of both structural (Stru CA) and 512 

numerical (Num CA) abnormalities across diverse imaging modalities. CenSegNet outperforms 513 

established models—including U-Net, SegNet, and DeepLabv3+—in accuracy, generalisability, and 514 

morphological fidelity, particularly in densely packed or morphologically heterogeneous tissue 515 

regions. The framework comprises three specialised modules: a YOLOv11-based detector trained 516 

on over 15,000 annotated centrosomes for robust localisation; a U-Net model for precise 517 

segmentation of centrosome area and morphology; and a StarDist-based cell segmentation module 518 

optimised for delineating epithelial boundaries in complex tissue architectures. This modular 519 

integration, coupled with uncertainty-aware postprocessing, enables systematic and standardised 520 

phenotyping of CA subtypes at single-cell and spatial resolution. Applied to 911 sample cores from 521 

127 patients, CenSegNet-based profiling of over 330,000 centrosomes reveals previously 522 

uncharacterised spatial trajectories and clinical correlates of Stru CA and Num CA, uncovering their 523 

mechanistic uncoupling, age-dependent modulation, and associations with tumour progression, 524 

hormone receptor status, HER2 expression, and genomic alterations. While the present cohort is 525 

not powered to establish definitive prognostic associations across all CA measures, our analyses 526 

identify Stru CA within the tumour core as significantly associated with overall survival and further 527 

indicate that spatially resolved numerical and Stru CA patterns align with clinically relevant outcome 528 

trends. Together, these findings provide a first indication that specific CA subtypes—particularly Stru 529 

CA in defined spatial contexts—may have prognostic relevance and establish a foundation for future 530 

studies to rigorously evaluate the independent prognostic value of numerical and structural CA in 531 

larger cohorts with longer and more complete clinical follow -up. 532

Model architecture and training. CenSegNet employs a modular three-step architecture 971 

comprising approximately 40 million trainable parameters. The first step consists of a detection head 972 

that localises candidate ROIs, while the second stage performs fine-grained segmentation within 973 

these ROIs to achieve precise spatial delineation. This decoupled design enables task-specific 974 

optimisation and improves memory efficiency and training stability. In the detection stage, we fine-975 

tuned YOLOv11-seg model 45 (https://github.com/ultralytics/ultralytics) using our own training set, 976 

guided by a composite loss function comprising box loss, segmentation loss, classification loss, and 977 

distribution focal loss. These components respectively optimise object localisation, foreground-978 

background separation, class prediction, and robustness to complex spatial distributions. Training 979 

was performed using the AdamW optimiser (learning rate = 0.002, momentum = 0.9) with a batch 980 

size of 16 over 300 epochs. The segmentation step 2 employed a U-Net architecture 46 with three 981

using RMSprop (learning rate = 1 × 10 -4 , weight decay = 1 × 10 -8 , momentum = 0.9), which stabilised 985
