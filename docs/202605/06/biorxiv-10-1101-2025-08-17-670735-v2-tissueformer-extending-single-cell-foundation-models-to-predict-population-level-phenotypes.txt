## Abstract 8

## Background 9

Single-cell RNA sequencing technologies have enabled unprecedented insights into gene expression and 10 

opened new pathways for diagnostics and tissue annotation. At present, most computational 11 

approaches for interpreting single-cell data predict labels or properties based on isolated single-cell 12 

transcriptomic profiles. This approach overlooks the cellular composition within a sample, which is often 13 

critical for inferring tissue identity or other sample-level phenotypes. 14 

## Results 15 

To address this limitation, we introduce TissueFormer, a Transformer-based neural network that infers 16 

population-level labels from groups of single-cell RNA profiles while retaining single-cell resolution. We 17 

applied TissueFormer to two tasks: predicting COVID-19 severity from single-cell RNA sequencing of 18 

blood samples, and predicting cortical area identity from spatial transcriptomic data in mouse brains. 19 

TissueFormer outperformed single-cell foundation models and machine learning methods applied to 20 

pseudobulk and cell type composition. 21 

## Conclusions 22 

TissueFormer’s higher performance promises more accurate diagnostics and enables the automated 23 

construction of high-resolution brain region maps in individual mice directly from spatial transcriptomic 24 

data. Applied to mice with developmental perturbations to visual input, these maps revealed a 25 

significant reduction in predicted visual cortex area, illustrating how individual differences in 26 

neuroanatomy can be quantified. More broadly, TissueFormer provides a framework for predicting any 27 

population-level phenotypes which are influenced by cellular diversity and tissue-level organization. 28 29

# Background 33 

Many important features such as tissue identity, disease state, or treatment response are defined at the 34 

level of cell populations but are driven by patterns of single-cell phenotypes. Observations of population-35 

level phenotypes can thus be used for the annotation and classification of tissue samples. For example, 36 

the proportion of cell types in a population is a useful biomarker for clinical features such as inflammation 37 

(Templeton et al., 2014 ). In the brain, an important problem in this class is the annotation of brain areas 38 

which differ in cell type composition ( Yao et al., 2021b ). However, readouts depending on cell composition 39 

currently remain limited to a handful of canonical patterns identified through decades of observation 40 

rather than discovered systematically through large-scale, data-driven analyses. 41 

With the increasing availability of single-cell RNA sequencing, it is now possible to measure patterns 42 

in single-cell properties at high resolution across large numbers of cells per sample ( Svensson et al., 43 

2018 ). This rich granularity presents a tradeoff for the analysis of phenotypes across tissues, samples, 44 

or individuals, each of which contains many sequenced cells. At one extreme, many computational 45 

pipelines focus on single cells in isolation, taking a cell profile as input and outputting a label specific to 46 

that cell. This is the approach taken by most recent single-cell ‘foundation models’ of mRNA transcription 47 

data ( Lopez et al., 2018 ; Connell et al., 2022 ; Cui et al., 2023 ; Theodoris et al., 2023 ; Rosen et al., 2023 ;48 

Yuan et al., 2024 ; Schaar et al., 2024 ; Ito et al., 2025 ; Hsieh et al., 2024 ), reviewed in ( Szałata et al., 49 

2024 ). These models are fundamentally single-cell in design, and as a result they are unable to detect 50 

signals that emerge only from the composition of cells present. 51 

An opposing strategy is to first compute summary statistics about cell populations across tissues or 52 

samples, then use these for downstream analysis and classification. One such statistic is the average 53 

expression, or pseudobulk expression profile, as is frequently used in differential state analysis ( Crowell 54 

et al., 2020 ). Alternatively, one may calculate compositional features about a population, such as cell 55 

type frequencies, ratios of cell types, putative cell-cell communication networks, and cell type specific 56 

pathway scores ( Cao et al., 2022 ). While informative, both pseudobulk and cell composition measures 57 

obscure single-cell granularity and rely on the researcher to specify which compressed representation of 58 

a population of cells is likely to be informative about sample labels. 59 

In order to maximize the potential of single-cell data, it is important that methods combine the 60 

transfer-learning capabilities and end-to-end trainability of single-cell foundation models with the ability 61 

to compare cells across a population. Certain models exist which are end-to-end trainable and can 62 

compare across cells, such as CellCnn ( Arvaniti and Claassen, 2017 ), scAGG ( Verlaan et al., 2025 ), 63 

and ScRAT ( Mao et al., 2024 ), but these models are not compatible with existing foundation models. 64 

The ability to incorporate knowledge from pretrained models is especially important given the high 65 

dimensionality of the inputs when predicting sample-level phenotypes. In each independent sample, the 66 

input dimensionality is on the order of the number of cells per sample times the number of genes per cell. 67 

By first pretraining on self-supervised tasks, foundation models effectively reduce this dimensionality by 68 

establishing a learned prior over the representation of each cell. 69 

To bridge these approaches we present TissueFormer, a neural network architecture that analyzes 70 

groups of single-cell RNA profiles collectively while utilizing knowledge embedded in pre-trained single-cell 71 

foundation models. The model incorporates a module based on the Geneformer architecture for single-72 

cell analysis ( Theodoris et al., 2023 ), which can be initialized with pre-trained weights. During processing, 73 

each cell is given a learnable representation with this pretrained module. These cell representations 74 

are then processed by several further layers of self-attention, allowing the model to learn to attend 75 

to the relevant cells and their interactions. Importantly, the output of Tissueformer is invariant to the 76 

order of cells presented as input. These design choices allow TissueFormer to learn arbitrary functions 77 

from observations of many cells to sample-level phenotypes while simultaneously leveraging pretrained 78 

single-cell foundation models. 79

To validate our approach, we applied TissueFormer to two prediction tasks in which cellular diversity 80 

is likely to be crucial. In the first task, we link spatial transcriptomic data to Allen Brain Atlas labels of 81 

regions of the mouse cortex. This task is a competitive benchmark for the supervised classification 82 

of sampled tissues, though it is important to note that clustering or alignment-style methods have 83 

also been developed for tissue annotation (e.g. Biancalani et al. (2021 ); Hu et al. (2021 ); Lee et al. 84 

(2025 )). As a second task, we compiled three datasets of single-cell transcription within blood samples 85 

of patients with COVID-19 infections of varying severity, and sought to predict infection severity from 86 

gene expression ( COMBAT, 2022 ; Ren et al., 2021 ; Stephenson et al., 2021 ). On both tasks we found 87 

that TissueFormer outperforms both standard supervised methods as well as fine-tuned single-cell 88 

foundation models at predicting sample-level annotations. These results highlight the importance of 89 

sample- or tissue-level features in transcriptomic analysis and establish a framework for integrating 90 

single-cell foundation models into higher-order biological investigations. When applied to brains of mice 91 

with developmental perturbations, TissueFormer’s predicted cortical maps further revealed individual 92 

differences in neuroanatomy that are consistent with past analyses of compositional shifts in these 93 

brains ( Chen et al., 2024 ). 94 

# Methods 95 

Our primary goal is to design and evaluate a model which is generally applicable to the supervised 96 

problem of sample or tissue annotation from single-cell data. In what follows, we first describe the 97 

particular characteristics of this problem that make it challenging for standard methods. 98

Recent related work has introduced several foundation models tailored for spatial transcriptomic 129 

data. Because these models observe groups of cells, they can be seen as solutions to a subcase of the 130 

multi-cell problem in Equation 2. For example, scGPT-spatial is pretrained to predict gene expression in 131 

held-out cells in the local neighborhood ( Wang et al., 2025 ). Unlike TissueFormer, scGPT-spatial averages 132 

the embedding of several cells in the local neighborhood for the inter-cell problem, rather than employing 133 

self-attention across cells, and thus cannot compare compositional signals. HEIST ( Madhu et al., 2025 )134 

and CI-FM ( You et al., 2025 ) define graph neural networks over a hierarchical graph spanning both nearby 135 

cells and their genes, which in principle enables comparative processing across cells. As explained 136 

below, TissueFormer uses Transformer layers and not graph neural networks, making it compatible with 137 

single-cell foundation models trained on traditional scRNA-seq data. Unlike these works, the design 138 

of TissueFormer is not restricted to spatial transcriptomic data and is applicable to any sample-level 139 

phenotype prediction problem. 140 

## TissueFormer architecture 141 

TissueFormer is a neural network architecture tailored for groups of single-cell RNA transcriptomic 142 

profiles ( Figure 1 ). It is able to attend to information within each cell’s relative mRNA transcription profile, 143 

yet also look across cells to extract information from the diversity and frequency of mRNA profiles in the 144

architecture and the easy loading of pre-trained single-cell foundation models into the cell embedding 148

The building block of TissueFormer which performs these capabilities is the Transformer block 150 

implementing self-attention ( Vaswani et al., 2017 ). Compared to older neural network architectures like 151 

Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLP), Transformers are distinguished by the shape of their inputs. Whereas an 152 

MLP observes vector-shaped inputs (i.e. a vector of mRNA transcript counts), Transformers operate on 153 

lists of vectors, or equivalently, on matrix-shaped inputs. Self-attention acts to select which vectors in 154 

the list are most relevant in the current context. 155 

In order for single-cell data to be compatible with Transformers, each cell’s vector of mRNA transcript 156 

counts 𝑐 𝑖 must be expanded into a list of vectors. Multiple strategies for this conversion are possible 157 

for single-cell data, as reviewed in Szałata et al. (2024 ). Here we use a rank-ordering approach as in 158 

Geneformer ( Theodoris et al., 2023 ). Each cell’s expressed genes are ordered by their relative expression 159 

normalized by their median expression in a pretraining corpus. Each gene name in this ordered list is 160 

then mapped to a high-dimensional embedding vector via a learnable dictionary. While alternatives to 161 

rank encoding have been tested in other foundation models, rank encoding is common in bioinformatic 162 

pipelines and has generally been found to be robust to common sources of experimental variability 163 

(Ballouz et al., 2015 ). 164 

The single-cell module within TissueFormer is designed to compress each cell’s unique and relevant 165 

information into a cell embedding for later processing ( Figure 1 b). This is achieved by prefixing to each 166 

cell a special token named the <cls> token, following standard design choices ( Devlin et al., 2019 ). The 167 

<cls> token instantiates a working space for the accumulation of information from all genes. During 168 

learning, any error-corrective information passed backwards is bottlenecked through the cell embedding, 169 

ensuring it collects the necessary information from each cell. In sum, the cell embedding vector which is 170 

passed to later layers can be written as a function of the mRNA transcript count vector 𝑐 𝑖 as: 171 

CellEmb [𝑐 𝑖 ] = SingleCellModule[RankEncoding[ 𝑐 𝑖 ]] [<cls> ] (3) The single-cell module shares the Geneformer architecture, meaning that any trained single-cell 172 

model can be loaded into this module. This gives TissueFormer the ability to incorporate model weights 173 

and biological knowledge from pretrained single-cell foundation models. In our experiments we used 174 

a version of Geneformer adapted for mouse brain tissue. We first downloaded the Geneformer 12L 175 

model, which was trained to predict the identity of randomly masked genes (15% of total genes per 176 

cell) on a corpus of 30 million human cells. We then adapted this to mouse tissue by repeating this 177 

methodology on a hand-curated dataset of approximately 3.5 million mouse cells. Using the CellXGene 178 

Census explorer ( Program et al., 2025 ), we aggregated data from several publications (Table 1) ( Yao 179 

et al., 2021b ; Govek et al., 2022 ; Kozareva et al., 2021 ; Steuernagel et al., 2022 ; Yao et al., 2021a , 2023 ;180 

Zhang et al., 2021 ). After tokenizing (see below), we then trained the Geneformer architecture on this 181 

dataset with an identical masked gene prediction objective. Models were trained for 3 epochs using 182 

AdamW, a learning rate of 0.001, and a batch size of 32 cells on two Nvidia V100 cards. We tested training 183 

on the murine dataset from scratch as well as finetuning the human model. In order to finetune the 184 

human model, we gave mouse genes the same initial embedding vector as their human orthologs, where 185 

available, and gave unmatched genes random initializations. Orthologs were obtained from Ensembl 186 

(Harrison et al., 2024 ), with the first selected if multiple orthologous genes existed. This transfer from 187 

human genes significantly improved the model compared to training on mouse genes alone as measured 188 

with the cross-entropy loss on 100,000 held-out cells from the mouse brain datasets. We call this 189

## TissueFormer architecture: hyperparameters of this study 204 

TissueFormer contains two main components: a single-cell module and an aggregation module. In 205 

this sense it can be viewed as an end-to-end trainable hierarchical model above single-cell foundation 206 

models. 207 

The single-cell module is based on the BERT architecture. We used identical hyperparameter settings 208 

as used by Geneformer 12L ( Theodoris et al., 2023 ), allowing pretrained weights to be readily employed. 209 

Specifically, we used a 12-layer BERT network with ReLU activations, hidden layer sizes of 512, and a 210 

context window of up to 2048 genes. Each Transformer had multi-head attention with 8 heads, and the 211 

inputs are appended with sinusoidal positional embeddings. 212 

The aggregation model pools across single-cell embeddings. The aggregation module had 6 Trans-213 

former layers, each with a hidden size of 768 units. All training runs on the classification task used the 214 

Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.001 and a linear learning rate decay following a learning rate 215 

warmup with warmup ratio 0.1. Our implementation relies on the Hugging Face Transformers library 216 

(Wolf et al., 2020 ), based on Pytorch ( Paszke et al., 2019 ). We used Hydra for configuration management 217 

(Yadan, 2019 ). 218 

## Application to cortical annotation 219 

The mammalian brain is often partitioned into regions with distinct functional roles, connectivity, and 220 

cellular composition. In any particular brain, variations in neuroanatomy may arise due to genetic factors 221 

as well as due to the animal’s environment. These variations underlie the evolution of innate behavior 222 

and reflect the aptitude for adaptation within a lifetime. 223 

Current methods for the annotation of single brains have certain well-known limitations. One common 224 

approach is to register a brain into the average-brain template of the Common Coordinate Framework 225 

(CCF), a 3D reference coordinate system constructed from over 1,000 reference mouse brains ( Wang 226 

et al., 2020 ). Once transformed into CCF coordinates, one can visualize the area boundaries of the Allen 227 

Brain Atlas, which again reflect a canonical, average brain. The drawback of this approach is that it 228 

obscures individual variation, especially the relative size of brain areas to one another. An alternative 229 

approach which enables individual variability is to employ experimental methods that interrogate only a 230 

small brain region in each animal, for example, focal viral projection tracing ( Xu et al., 2020 ) or two-photon 231 

functional imaging for a particular functional modality like visual responses ( Kalatsky and Stryker, 2003 ). 232

While accurate, these methods’ limited spatial scope prevents a holistic assessment of anatomy across 233 

the entire cortex. 234 

An alternative strategy that balances whole-brain coverage with single-animal individuality is to 235 

perform in situ single-cell RNA sequencing across a single brain. This method modernizes a classic 236 

approach of defining neuroanatomy through differences in cell type composition, an idea that goes back 237 

at least to Brodmann’s brain atlas ( Brodmann, 1909 ; Zilles and Amunts, 2010 ). Currently, the technical 238 

efficiency of spatial transcriptomics is now high enough to allow the profiling of cells across an entire 239 

mouse brain, and furthermore cheaply enough to cover multiple brains in single studies ( Chen et al., 240 

2024 ). Given such datasets, the remaining challenge in a robust pipeline for labeling brain areas is 241 

computational in nature. 242 

To investigate whether TissueFormer could serve this purpose, we applied TissueFormer to predict 243 

the brain region of cells from their transcriptomes as characterized in a recent dataset of whole-brain 244 

spatial transcriptomics of the brains of several mice ( Chen et al., 2024 ). Over 1 million cells across 245 

the brain, largely in the left hemisphere, were profiled in each of eight mice, four of which were raised 246 

in normal rearing conditions. Each cell profile contains counts from a panel of 104 genes selected 247 

for their ability to distinguish cell type clusters in excitatory cells. With multiple separate brains and 248 

whole-cortex coverage in one hemisphere, this dataset enables a test of whether individual differences 249 

in neuroanatomy can be resolved with spatial transcriptomics. 250 

In addition to mRNA counts, this dataset also contains labels of the brain area of each cell as 251 

established through a registration into the Common Coordinate Framework (CCF). Each brain in Chen 252 

et al. (2024 ) was previously registered to the CCF using standard software that defines a non-rigid 253 

smooth deformation from raw slice coordinates into the CCF. This step normalizes differences in gross 254 

anatomy and brain size. Importantly, once transformed into CCF coordinates, one may associate the 255 

topography of a new brain with the Allen Brain Atlas to obtain brain area annotations ( Wang et al., 2020 ). 256 

These CCF annotations provide the labels we use for training. 257 

It is important to note that CCF boundaries may not reflect ground truth neuroanatomy. While CCF 258 

registration accounts for overall brain shape, it does not account for more fine-grained differences 259 

such as the relative size of brain areas or a shift in a single boundary in one animal versus another. 260 

Nevertheless, these labels are sufficiently accurate to allow a comparison of computational methods. It 261 

is possible as well that the ‘errors’ of a model trained on such data will in fact represent a more accurate 262 

annotation than the CCF, a possibility we will return in Figure 3 in which we examine the predictions of 263 

TissueFormer on held-out brains. 264

vectors. Unlike standard color maps, this ensures that perceptual saliency aligns with biological meaning, 287 

with no salient colors (e.g. red) arbitrarily assigned. Technically, this involves computing the cell type 288 

similarity matrix, embedding this matrix into 3D via multidimensional scaling (MDS) to best preserve 289 

distances, then interpreting this 3D space as the LUV axes of the perceptually uniform LUV color space. 290 

We released a Python package which computes such colormaps called ColorMyCell to accompany this 291 

manuscript ( Benjamin, 2025 ). 292 

This dataset also contains labels of the brain area of each cell. These are inherited from the CCF 293 

coordinate space. We used these annotations as training data when predicting area from cellular 294 

transcription. As downloaded, these areas were at the finest level of the hierarchical tree of brain 295 

annotations and distinguished between cortical layers. In order to obtain the cortical area without regard 296 

to layers, we ascended one level up the hierarchical ‘ancestor’ tree of annotation layers in the Allen Brain 297 

Atlas. The resulting areas, displayed in e.g. Figure 3 , comprise 42 functionally distinct cortical areas. 298

## Tokenization 318 

All cells’ mRNA transcript vectors were ‘tokenized’, or converted into a format readable by Transformer 319 

models, with the following procedure. We used an identical strategy as performed by Geneformer, 320 

but wrote custom code to accept data in anndata format ( Virshup et al., 2023 ). First, each cell was 321 

normalized by the total counts in each cell. All mRNA counts were then normalized by a fixed vector 322 

representing the median per-gene counts in a reference data corpus. When fine-tuning Geneformer, it 323 

was empirically better to use the same median count vector used to train Geneformer (i.e. on human 324 

data) rather than re-establish median counts in mouse data. These normalized counts were then rank 325 

sorted, and the indices of the sort (i.e. the output of argsort) was used as the model input. For example, 326 

a model input of [gene 5, gene 2, ... ] describes that the gene 5 had highest relative transcription, followed 327 

by gene 2, and so on. In a modification of the Geneformer pipeline, we ensured genes with zero counts 328 

were given a special token <pad> . We also modified the Geneformer pipeline to prepend a <cls> token. 329 

The ‘sentences’ seen by the model were thus of the form [<cls> , gene 5, gene 2, ..., <pad> ]. The tokens 330 

are mapped to high-dimensional embedding vector via a dictionary learned within TissueFormer. 331 

## Group construction strategy 332 

In order to train and predict with TissueFormer, it is necessary to prepare groups of cells which share 333 

a label. This requires code infrastructure to appropriately group cells according to the proper criteria. 334 

Since single-cell datasets are already quite large, we took an online strategy which loaded the single-cell 335 

dataset (after tokenization) and constructed groups online via a custom sampler and data collator within 336 

the Hugging Face code ecosystem for training TissueFormer. 337 

For the COVID-19 datasets, groups of 𝑁 cells are sampled uniformly at random from each donor. For 338 

brain annotation, our strategy was to select cells in groups which are approximately cortical columns, 339 

reflecting the columnar organization of the cerebral cortex in which neurons within a radial column share 340 

functional properties ( Mountcastle, 1997 ). In order to cover the cortical surface with a roughly equal 341 

density, our pipeline first selected a seed cell at random from the training data, which potentially covers 342 

multiple brains. We next extracted the 𝑁 cells in the same brain which were closest to that cell in the XY 343 

plane of the flatmap coordinate system. This resulted in a group of 𝑁 cells which lie within a column. 344 

The width of the column differed in each group depending on the local density of cells. Note that because 345 

each brain contains a comparable number of cells, the column size in the test brain was comparable to 346 

the column size during training despite there being 3 training brains and 3 times the overall number of 347 

cells. 348

## TissueFormer training 364 

For the brain annotation task, we used leave-one-brain-out cross-validation: in each of four folds, three 365 

brains were used for training (90% of cells) and validation (10% of cells from the same three brains), and 366 

one brain was held out entirely as the test set. Random seeds were fixed across all methods to ensure 367 

identical data splits. 368 

We trained TissueFormer on an NVIDIA H100 card with a batch size of 4096 total cells divided into 369 

𝑁 groups, each with |𝐺 | = 4096∕ 𝑁 cells. A single model trains to completion for this dataset set (1.4 370 

million cells) in 10 epochs in less than an hour. The most efficient training strategy involves saturating the 371 

GPU memory, and this is largely driven by the overall number of cells rather than the number of distinct 372 

groups. We trained all models with varying group size 𝑁 to convergence to ensure a fair comparison. 373 

However, as convergence can be difficult to verify, we attempted to further match the training resources 374 

for each model by comparing models with an equal number of training steps, which is proportional to 375 

the number of cell groups seen. 376

## Brain map visualization 401 

To create Figure 3 a, we first trained 4 models in a cross-validated scheme, with 1 brain held out each fold. 402 

To achieve a high-resolution coverage, we then predicted on the test brain using every cell in the test 403 

brain once as a center seed of group selection. The prediction on each cell was stored as its label. These 404 

are visualized in Figure 3 —figure Supplement 1 . We then constructed a method to present smooth brain 405 

maps with clear boundaries between areas. Namely, we trained a support vector classifier (SVC) with 406 

a radial basis function kernel on the cells and their predicted labels, then visualized the predictions on 407 

pixels. We used the cuML implementation, a GPU-accelerated method, and used 𝛾 = 0 .00001 and 𝐶 = 1 408 

as hyperparameters of the SVC ( Raschka et al., 2020 ). Intuitively, this method acts to color each pixel 409 

in Figure 3 a by a weighted average of the TissueFormer-predicted labels of the cells surrounding that 410 

pixel. The weights are selected due to a combination of proximity to the pixel and a factor determined by 411 

the algorithm to minimize the rate at which cells are misclassified, i.e. located on a pixel with a different 412 

color (area) than that cell’s TissueFormer-predicted area. To mask regions with low cellular density, 413 

we estimated the spatial density of cells on the flatmap using a Gaussian kernel density estimator 414 

(bandwidth 12, cuML implementation). The resulting density was normalized to [0 , 1] , and pixels with 415 

normalized density below 0.04 were overlaid with a semi-opaque white mask (95% opacity). To convert 416 

predicted area sizes from flatmap pixels to physical units (mm 2), we estimated the physical cortical 417 

surface area represented by each flatmap pixel. Because the butterfly flatmap is a distorted 2D projection 418 

of the curved cortical surface ( Wang et al., 2020 ), pixels in different parts of the flatmap correspond 419 

to different amounts of cortical surface. We computed this correction by mapping each flatmap pixel 420 

back to its corresponding coordinate on the pial surface (at 10 𝜇 m resolution) and estimating the local 421 

surface area element via finite differences between neighboring pixels. The area of a predicted region 422 

was then the sum of these per-pixel surface areas over all pixels classified as that region. 423

# Results 460 

We evaluated TissueFormer on two tasks in which cellular diversity is expected to be informative: 461 

predicting cortical area identity from spatial transcriptomic data in mouse brains, and predicting COVID-462 

19 severity from single-cell RNA sequencing of peripheral blood. For the brain annotation task, we 463 

compared TissueFormer against traditional metrics such as cell-type ratios and pseudobulk expression, 464 

as well as other deep learning methods. TissueFormer outperformed single-cell models on both tasks, 465 

confirming the practical value of attending across cells when predicting population-level phenotypes. In 466 

the brain task, we additionally found evidence that standard tissue alignment procedures do not correctly 467 

register tissue into the correct functional areas. 468

The utility of attending across cells can be made clear by comparing with the performance of machine 470

## Annotating cell groups with TissueFormer 494 

To apply TissueFormer, we first created a pipeline to group cell sharing a label. Specifically, we grouped 495 

cells into cylindrical columns which fall inwards from the surface of the cortex ( Figure 2 d-e). Note that 496 

this strategy can easily be generalized to other shapes for spatial data from other tissues, or to other 497 

sampling strategies for non-spatial data. The cerebral cortex can be viewed as a layered cake draped 498 

over the exterior of the mouse brain, with functional areas as slices ( Figure 2 a). Thus, cortical columns 499 

contain cells in the same functional area. Our pipeline programmatically selects a single cell, then grows 500 

a cylinder centered on that cell up to the minimum radius needed to contain 𝑁 total cells. The density of 501 

this dataset is such that a group of 64 cells spans on average 0.008 mm 2, equivalent to a 56 μm radius. 502 

Each columnar group of 𝑁 cells is labeled with the most common CCF annotation of cells in that group. 503 

We next trained TissueFormer to predict the group’s label from the set of transcriptomic profiles 504 

within the group. We again cross-validated by training on 90% of cells from three brains, validating on 505 

the remaining 10% of cells, and testing on one held-out brain. As we varied the number of cells in each 506 

group from 𝑁 = 1 to 𝑁 = 256 , we observed that the validation accuracy of TissueFormer increased from 507 

around 35% to nearly 80% accuracy ( Figure 2 f). On test brains, the accuracy Tissueformer also increased 508 

markedly with group size from 30% to over 60% accuracy ( Figure 2 g). Providing the cortical depth of 509 

each cell as additional input to the model did not improve results, suggesting that depth represents 510 

redundant information ( Figure 2 —figure Supplement 1 f). Note that random guessing on this 1-of-42 511 

classification task yields 8% accuracy due to imbalanced classes. Similar relative accuracies were seen 512 

when training with a class-balancing weighted objective ( Figure 2 —figure Supplement 1 c). These results 513 

demonstrate that TissueFormer is able to dramatically outperform single-cell models at predicting tissue 514 

identity. 515 

As the group size increased from 𝑁 = 1 to 𝑁 = 256 , the accuracy increased smoothly and roughly 516 

logarithmically with group size, indicating a predictable scaling law with more cells. Performance 517 

saturated at 𝑁 = 128 cells, possibly because at this scale and at this density of cells per brain the groups 518 

sometimes straddle boundaries, at which point group identity begins to lose meaning. Indeed, at 𝑁 = 256 519 

over 70% of groups contained a boundary ( Figure 2 —figure Supplement 1 g). Higher sampling densities 520 

would enable larger group sizes in smaller locales, and thus possibly an even higher saturating accuracy. 521 

Due to this problem of spatial density, it is not possible to verify the maximum group size at which cells 522 

contain no further information about area. Overall, the considerable increase of performance with group 523 

size highlights the importance of the ability to integrate and compare information across single cells. 524

## Predicted cortical maps 585 

TissueFormer can be used as an automated pipeline to create maps of cortical anatomy from single-cell 586 

transcription after being trained on reference brains. Note that annotating from reference brains is a 587 

supervised task, not to be confused with the unsupervised task of spatially clustering single-cell data 588 

(see Discussion). In Figure 3 a, we demonstrate this capability and display the predicted cortical maps for 589 

each of 4 mice treated as a held-out test set. Pixels in these maps were colored by a weighted average 590 

of nearby cells’ predictions (see Methods). For visualization we used the predictions of TissueFormer 591 

trained with a class-size-balancing objective ensuring that the prior probability was uniform across 592 

all areas regardless of area size. We found that all four maps were coarsely similar to the reference 593 

annotation (see Figure 2 a), supporting the finding in Figure 2 that TissueFormer is able to predict brain 594 

area more accurately than other approaches. 595 

A close inspection of these cortical maps revealed several notable differences in the predicted 596 

anatomy of each mouse. For example, the boundary between the somatosensory cortex and the motor 597 

cortex is consistently shifted in the posterior-lateral direction in brain 1 relative to the Common Coordinate 598 

Framework (CCF) annotations, yet is consistently shifted in the opposite (anterior-medial) direction in 599 

brain 2 ( Figure 3 b). Additionally, the primary visual area is shifted medially in brain 2, yet laterally in brains 600 

3 and 4. Likewise, the primary auditory area is shifted medially in brain 2 yet is expanded and shifted 601 

laterally in brain 4. These shifts are a predominant cause of why test brain ‘accuracy’ in Figure 2 g is lower 602 

than validation accuracy. 603 

While it is possible that the discrepancies between the CCF annotation and the predicted annotation 604 

are ‘errors’ of the model, it is also possible that this reflects true differences in anatomy between mice or 605 

errors in registration with the CCF. The CCF represents the average anatomy of over 1,000 mouse brains, 606 

obscuring potential individual variability between mice. To investigate this possibility, we examined the 607 

somatosensory-motor boundary in more detail. This boundary can be canonically identified by differences 608 

in cell type composition. In particular, motor cortex is classically considered to lack a Layer 4 ( Brodmann, 609 

1909 ), and thus should show a large decrease in the density of Layer 4/5 IT excitatory cells relative to 610 

Layer 5 IT cells. This easily verifiable boundary was qualitatively visible in coronal slices colored by cell 611 

type ( Figure 3 c). To confirm its location, we examined the density of these two cell types along slices 612 

taken from brain 1 and brain 2 which intersect the sensory/motor boundary ( Figure 3 d). We found that 613 

the CCF boundary was inconsistent across brains relative to the ratio of cell types, but TissueFormer’s 614 

predicted maps showed a consistent 1:1. Furthermore, in the CCF boundaries, we observed a large shift 615 

between mice in the ratio for large distances on either side ( Figure 3 e). In contrast, the density ratios 616 

were consistent in their alignment to the predicted boundaries. Thus, a closer inspection of cell type 617

three independent cohorts totaling over 2.6 million cells from 376 donors: COMBAT ( COMBAT, 2022 ), 649

# Discussion 684 

We developed TissueFormer, a neural network architecture that attends to groups of single-cell tran-685 

scriptomic profiles in order to predict a label or annotation common to that group. On a brain area 686 

classification task using multi-brain spatial transcriptomics, TissueFormer achieved 60% accuracy with 687 

groups of 64 cells on held-out test brains, compared to 30% for a matched single-cell model, and outper-688 

formed pseudobulk and cell type composition baselines. On a COVID-19 severity prediction task using 689 

non-spatial blood scRNA-seq from three independent cohorts, TissueFormer likewise outperformed 690 

classical baselines and matched or exceeded other deep learning approaches. In both settings, accu-691 

racy increased with group size, highlighting the importance of attending across cells when predicting 692 

tissue-level properties. 693

anatomical contiguity (as in spatial transcriptomics), by patient sample (e.g., blood draws, biopsies), or by 695

TissueFormer provides a promising tool for automated brain mapping from spatial transcriptomics. 701 

Compared to the labels due to Common Coordinate Framework (CCF) registration, TissueFormer’s 702 

predicted labels displayed individual variability that better correlated with inter-animal differences in local 703 

gene expression and cell type distributions, despite being trained to predict CCF labels. This tool could 704 

thus expedite the study of individual differences in the size and location of transcriptomically-defined 705 

regions in the brain. As a concrete example, TissueFormer’s predicted maps revealed a significant 706 

reduction in the area of primary visual cortex in mice deprived of visual input during development, 707 

consistent with past analyses of cell-type compositional shifts in these brains ( Chen et al., 2024 ). 708 

Our approach to brain mapping is a supervised approach in which reference brains supply ground 709 

truth labels. Note that this is distinct from the approach of aligning tissue without regard to area labels, as 710 

in Tangram ( Biancalani et al., 2021 ) and CAST ( Tang et al., 2024 ). An important caveat of the supervised 711 

approach is the lack of perfect training data. No datasets of spatial transcriptomics are yet available 712 

which have animal-by-animal annotations of neuroanatomy verified by additional modalities such as 713 

projection tracing or functional imaging. Without access to such datasets, we instead trained to predict 714 

the imperfect CCF labels. In general, there is no formal guarantee that the predictions on held-out brains 715 

should reflect a better assessment than the CCF itself. While we are led by our empirical analysis to trust 716 

the model, this must be done with caution until a model can be trained on a dataset with multiple brains 717 

individually annotated with complementing experimental modalities. 718 

In the current study, we did not take up the question of whether different boundaries than the Allen Brain 719 

Atlas would be in any sense better, as in recent spatial clustering methods. Current boundaries are widely 720 

used and important for shared nomenclature. However, the automated clustering and identification of 721 

spatially homogeneous regions within spatial transcriptomic dataset is an interesting and open research 722 

question. Several algorithms have already been developed for this purpose ( Dries et al., 2021 ; Zhao et al., 723 

2021 ; Chitra et al., 2025 ; Hu et al., 2021 ; Dong and Zhang, 2022 ; Singhal et al., 2024 ; ?; Jackson et al., 724 

2024 ). Because these unsupervised methods discover spatial domains without reference labels, their 725 

outputs are not directly comparable to those of supervised methods like TissueFormer, which predicts 726 

known annotations from labeled training data. When algorithms from this family are applied to the 727 

mouse cortex, the resulting regions correspond more to cortical layers than to functional regions ( Ortiz 728 

et al., 2020 ; Partel et al., 2020 ; Lee et al., 2025 ). This is consistent with there being larger differences 729 

in gene expression across layers than across cortical areas. In contrast, the functional specialization 730 

of neurons in cortex varies more across area than across layers within an area; neurons within a 731 

cortical column typically mediate the same cognitive tasks and furthermore respond to similar features 732 

within that task ( Mountcastle, 1997 ; Callaway, 1998 ). Amid this dichotomy between transcriptomic and 733 

functional contiguity in space, we chose in this manuscript to classify functional areas for practical utility 734 

and for the reason of it being a benchmark task that leverages transcriptomic diversity within cellular 735 

ensembles. Nevertheless, it would be interesting in future work to train TissueFormer with self-supervised 736 

or contrastive objectives so as to discover brain areas de novo .737

Figure 3—figure supplement 2. Normalized spatial density of select genes in each animal. Arrows highlight the animal-specific shifts of regional anatomy relative to the CCF (white boundaries), and how these are consistent between genes and with the predictions of Tissueformer (top row, reproduced from 

Figure 3 . The top three rows (Tshz2, Coro6, and Zfmp2) recapitulate the medial shift in the boundary to the retrospenial areas in Brain 1 and the lateral shift in this boundary in Brain 4. The bottom 5 rows recapitulate the shift in the somatosensory/motor boundary, the same as analyzed in cell type distributions in Figure 3. To construct normalized gene densities, we first normalized mRNA transcription levels within each cell. We then plot the average normalized transcription within each hex. Normalization was necessary to reduce confounds of unequal spatial sampling.
