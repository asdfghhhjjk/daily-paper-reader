# Abstract 

Whole-slide images (WSIs) present a fundamental challenge for computational pathology due to their extreme resolution, multi-scale heterogeneity, and the requirement for clinically reliable interpretation. Although recent pathology foundation models have enabled fluent report generation, they often lack clinical grounding, failing to accurately represent key diagnostic concepts and relationships observed by pathologists. This limitation arises from the difficulty of integrating heterogeneous visual evidence spanning fine-grained cellular patterns, slide-level tissue architecture, and high-level diagnostic concepts, while maintaining interpretability and clinical coherence. Here we present SCOUT: Semantic Context-aware mOdality fUsion Transformer, a context-aware concept-grounded multimodal framework for pathology report generation that enables progressive conditioning of image representations by global slide information and explicit diagnostic concepts. The method integrates local histological patterns, whole-slide context, and expert-curated semantic descriptors within a unified learning paradigm, allowing visual features to be dynamically refined throughout the encoding process. By combining depth-aware contextual modulation with adaptive multimodal fusion during text generation, the framework produces clinically coherent reports while preserving complementarity across representational scales. We demonstrate that this approach improves generalization across datasets and enhances interpretabil-ity by explicitly linking visual evidence to diagnostic concepts and explanatory text. We evaluate SCOUT against three state-of-the-art baselines—WSI-Caption, HistGen, and BiGen—using iden-tical CONCH1.5 visual features on TCGA-BRCA, MICCAI REG, and HistAI. Across these three benchmarks, SCOUT achieves the best BLEU-1 to BLEU-4 and METEOR scores on all datasets, and the best ROUGE-L on TCGA-BRCA and MICCAI REG while remaining competitive on HistAI. In particular, SCOUT reaches 0.436/0.303/0.202/0.156 BLEU-1/2/3/4, 0.204 METEOR, and 0.312 ROUGE-L on TCGA-BRCA, and 0.865/0.834/0.805/0.780 BLEU-1/2/3/4, 0.568 METEOR, and 0.874 ROUGE-L on MICCAI REG. Our results suggest that progressive contextual conditioning is a powerful and general strategy for multimodal medical report generation, providing a flexible foundation for integrating domain expertise into vision–language models for computational pathology. 

# 1 Introduction 

Automated generation of structured reports from medical images has emerged as a promising application of multimodal machine learning, with the potential to reduce documentation burden and improve consistency in clinical workflows[ 10 ,4 , 31 , 22 , 11 , 13 , 9]. In computational pathology, this task is particularly challenging[ 8, 12 , 20 ]: pathology reports synthesize fine-grained cellular observations, global tissue architecture, and standardized diagnostic concepts into coherent narratives that are both descriptive and explanatory. Report generation in this setting therefore requires not only visual understanding but also the integration of multi-scale evidence with structured medical knowledge. Recent advances in multimodal learning and pathology vision-language models have enabled increasingly fluent report generation from whole-slide images (WSIs). Such designs implicitly assume that these representations are universally optimal for all downstream linguistic and reasoning tasks. This assumption is particularly limiting in pathology, where diagnostically relevant features vary substantially across disease types, magnification levels, and reporting conventions. A central limitation of current pathology report generation methods is the lack of contextual adaptability during visual feature extraction. Local patch-level features often capture fine cellular detail but lack global context, while slide-level representations summarize tissue architecture at the cost of spatial specificity. Moreover, emerging approaches like HistGen[ 10 ] and BiGen[ 31 ] incorporate additional information, but as auxiliary signals, rather than as drivers of how visual features themselves are formed and refined. This separation between feature extraction and contextual reasoning limits both generalization and interpretability. Models may generate fluent reports while failing to accurately ground statements in relevant visual evidence, and often provide limited insight into how diagnostic concepts influence predictions. In contrast, human pathology practice involves iterative reasoning, where visual impressions are continuously refined based on global context and domain knowledge. Capturing this process computationally requires models in which visual representations are dynamically modulated by contextual and semantic signals throughout the learning pipeline. For instance, WSI-Caption[ 4] and related multiple instance learning approaches model interactions across large sets of patch tokens using Transformer-based aggregation, but rely on fixed pre-extracted visual features, which can dilute diagnostically relevant signals due to the overwhelming number of redundant regions. HistGen[ 10 ] attempts to address this through a local–global hierarchical encoder and a cross-modal context module that aligns visual and textual representations across granularities. However, its visual representations are still computed prior to language generation and refined largely through hierarchical aggregation and external memory, rather than being dynamically modulated by explicit semantic signals during encoding. In this work, we introduce a context-aware multimodal framework for pathology report generation that explicitly models the interaction between visual evidence and diagnostic concepts throughout the representation hierarchy. Unlike prior approaches that rely on fixed visual features, our method enables progressive conditioning of image representations via global slide context and structured semantic signals during encoding. Specifically, we introduce a depth-aware modulation mechanism that refines patch-level features at each encoder layer using global contextual embeddings and concept representations, allowing the model to dynamically emphasize diagnostically relevant patterns across spatial scales. In contrast to retrieval-based or token-level alignment strategies, this formulation treats diagnostic concepts as continuous conditioning signals that guide feature learning rather than auxiliary inputs. Further, to support multimodal reasoning during report generation, we design a dual-path cross-attention decoder with independent attention streams over visual and concept representations, followed by a gated fusion mechanism that adaptively integrates complementary information at each decoding step. By explicitly linking visual features to concept-level representations and natural language outputs, the framework produces reports that are not only fluent but also clinically grounded and interpretable, providing insight into the basis of its predictions. # 3 Methods 

3.1 Overview 

We propose a concept-grounded multimodal framework for pathology report generation that models diagnostic reasoning as a progressive and context-dependent process. Rather than treating visual understanding and language generation as sequential stages, the proposed approach enables continuous interaction between visual evidence, global context, and semantic concepts throughout the model. The framework integrates three complementary sources of information: (i) patch-level representations capturing fine-grained histological morphology, (ii) slide-level embeddings encoding global tissue architecture, and (iii) concept-level representations corresponding to clinically meaningful diagnostic attributes. At a high level, these modalities are jointly leveraged within a unified architecture consisting of three stages: (1) multi-scale feature extraction and adaptation, (2) context-aware encoding with progressive concept conditioning, and (3) adaptive multimodal decoding via contextual multi-head gating. Multi-scale and concept-aware visual representation: Histopathology images contain diagnostically relevant information across multiple spatial scales, from cellular morphology to global tissue organization. To capture this, we represent each slide using three complementary components: (i) patch-level visual features encoding local morphology, (ii) slide-level embeddings summarizing global context, and (iii) concept-level representations corresponding to clinically meaningful diagnostic attributes which are explicitly extracted and curated with domain expertise. Crucially, these slide and concept representations are not treated as auxiliary labels or post-hoc explanations. Instead, they serve as conditioning signals that influence how visual features are refined within the encoder. 

Progressive contextual modulation of visual features Rather than using fixed visual features, the encoder progres-sively refines patch representations across layers while conditioning on slide-level context and concept representations. This layer-wise modulation enables local features to be iteratively updated based on global and semantic information, allowing different depths of the network to capture complementary interactions between morphology, context, and concepts. 

Adaptive multimodal integration during decoding The decoder integrates patch-level, slide-level, and concept-level representations through modality-specific attention pathways. At each decoding step, these signals are combined using an adaptive gating mechanism, allowing the model to dynamically adjust the contribution of each modality based on the current linguistic context. This enables flexible transitions between visually grounded descriptions and concept-driven reasoning during report generation. 

Interpretability and clinical grounding The proposed framework is explicitly designed to support interpretability at multiple levels. Contextual modulation links global slide characteristics and diagnostic concepts to specific visual features, while adaptive multimodal integration exposes how different sources of information contribute to individual textual outputs. In addition, concept-level representations enable the generation of explanatory statements that articulate the rationale behind model predictions, bridging the gap between automated inference and clinical reasoning. Together, these design choices provide a principled approach for integrating domain knowledge into vision–language models for computational pathology, emphasizing methodological transparency, adaptability, and clinical relevance. 63.2 Architecture 

The proposed framework comprises four conceptual components [Figure 1]: • Modality-specific feature extractors that capture local histological detail, global slide context, and explicit diagnostic concepts. • Adaptive projection layers that map heterogeneous representations into a shared latent space. • A context-aware encoder that progressively refines visual features under the influence of global context and semantic knowledge. • An adaptive multimodal decoder that integrates complementary information sources to generate coherent and clinically grounded reports. Each component is designed to reflect a distinct stage of diagnostic reasoning, from evidence extraction to contextual interpretation and narrative synthesis. 

3.2.1 Multi-scale visual feature extraction Patch-level visual features. To capture fine-grained histological detail, we extract patch-level features from whole-slide images using CONCH[ 21 ], a foundation model trained on large-scale histopathology data. WSIs are tiled into non-overlapping or weakly overlapping 512 × 512 patches at 20 × magnification, and each patch is embedded into a fixed-dimensional representation that encodes cellular morphology and local tissue structure. Formally, given a whole-slide image I, we obtain a set of patch embeddings: 

P = {x1, x2, . . . , xL}, xi ∈ Rdp , (1) where L denotes the number of patches and dp is the embedding dimension. These representations serve as the primary source of localized visual evidence throughout the model. They are particularly effective at capturing morphological features like nuclear atypia, glandular structure, and cellular organization, but lack explicit global context. Addressing this limitation motivates the incorporation of complementary slide-level representations. 

Slide-level global context. To encode global tissue architecture and long-range spatial organization, we extract slide-level embeddings using TITAN, a whole-slide representation model designed to summarize large-scale histological patterns. Unlike patch-level features, these embeddings capture contextual information such as tissue composition, spatial heterogeneity, and global morphological trends. Each slide is represented as a single vector: s ∈ Rds

This summarizes slide-wide characteristics such as architectural patterns and staining distributions, etc. While such representations are insufficient for fine-grained description on their own, they provide crucial contextual signals that guide the interpretation of local visual evidence. 

Concept-level semantic features. In addition to purely visual representations, we incorporate explicit diagnostic concepts derived through a semi-automated pipeline combining large language model (LLM)–assisted extraction and expert curation using GECKO [ 15 ]. Candidate concepts are first identified from pathology reports and domain-specific corpora using an LLM (e.g., GPT-5), which extracts clinically relevant attributes such as architectural patterns, cytological abnormalities, and stromal features. These candidates are subsequently refined through expert review to ensure clinical validity, remove redundancies, and standardize terminology. The resulting concept set therefore reflects both data-driven discovery and domain expertise, providing a structured semantic representation aligned with routine diagnostic practice. Each case is associated with a concept embedding: C = {c1, c2, . . . , cK }, ck ∈ Rdc , where K denotes the number of concepts. These embeddings encode clinically meaningful attributes such as architectural patterns and cytological abnormalities. This design reflects the diagnostic workflow of human pathologists, where known disease entities and morphological patterns shape visual interpretation rather than being inferred only after observation.

3.2.3 Context-Aware Encoder with Progressive Modulation 

WSIs exhibit extreme heterogeneity: diagnostically relevant regions are sparse, context-dependent, and often ambiguous when viewed in isolation. While patch-level representations capture local morphology, their interpretation depends critically on global slide context (e.g., tumor burden, architectural patterns) and explicit diagnostic concepts (e.g., Gleason patterns, necrosis). To model the iterative nature of diagnostic reasoning, we design a context-aware encoder that progressively refines patch representations by conditioning them on both slide-level and concept-level context at every depth of the encoder, rather than only at the input or output. This enables early layers to suppress irrelevant visual features, while deeper layers emphasize clinically meaningful structures. 

Patch-level encoding. The encoder consists of L stacked Transformer layers operating on patch-level tokens. At each layer, patch representations are updated via self-attention and feed-forward transformations, followed by contextual modulation using slide-level and concept-level information. P(l) = EncoderLayer( P(l−1) ), (4) followed by a spatial aggregation module PAM( ·) that enhances local contextual interactions: ˆP(l) = PAM( P(l)).

This operation captures spatial and morphological relationships among image patches.

3.2.4 Multimodal Decoder with Adaptive 3-Way Gated Fusion 

To generate clinically coherent reports, the decoder must integrate heterogeneous sources of information, including fine-grained patch features, global slide context, and diagnostic concept representations. Rather than relying on early fusion or simple concatenation, we design a decoder that explicitly models interactions between these modalities through parallel cross-attention pathways and adaptive gated fusion. 

Autoregressive decoding. Given a sequence of previously generated tokens y<t , the decoder produces hidden representations using causal self-attention: Ht = SelfAttn( y<t )

ensuring that predictions depend only on past tokens. 

Parallel cross-attention. At each decoding layer, the token representations attend independently to three modality-specific feature sets obtained from the encoder: 

Hm = CrossAttn( Ht, F(m) ), m ∈ { patch , slide , concept } (10) This design allows each modality to contribute complementary information: patch features capture local morphology, slide features encode global tissue context, and concept features represent high-level diagnostic semantics. 

Limitations of naive fusion. A straightforward combination of these representations (e.g., summation or concatena-tion) assumes equal importance across modalities and tokens, which is unrealistic in pathology. The relevance of each modality varies dynamically depending on the linguistic context (e.g., cellular descriptions vs. diagnostic summaries), necessitating an adaptive fusion mechanism.

Interpretability and practical advantages. The learned gating weights α provide an explicit measure of modality importance at each token and attention head, enabling fine-grained analysis of how visual evidence and diagnostic concepts contribute to generated text. This design allows the model to dynamically shift between modalities, supporting both descriptive (visual grounding) and explanatory (concept-driven reasoning) components of pathology reports. Decoding proceeds autoregressively until an end-of-sequence token is generated. This decoder design provides several advantages. First, the adaptive gating mechanism enables dynamic modulation of modality contributions, allowing the model to shift between fine-grained visual grounding and high-level semantic reasoning depending on the linguistic context. Second, by separating modality-specific cross-attention pathways and combining them through learned fusion, the model avoids over-reliance on any single information source, improving robustness. Third, the learned gating weights offer interpretable signals that reflect the relative influence of visual evidence and diagnostic concepts at each decoding step, enabling analysis of reasoning patterns. Finally, the integration of concept representations as primary conditioning signals—rather than auxiliary supervision—encourages alignment between generated text and clinically meaningful semantic structures.

4.1 Datasets and Splits 

All experiments were conducted on curated whole-slide image datasets paired with pathology reports. Slides were partitioned into training, validation, and test sets at the patient level to prevent information leakage across splits. Unless otherwise stated, validation data were used exclusively for model selection and hyperparameter tuning, while the test set was held out until final evaluation. We evaluate the proposed framework on three datasets—TCGA-BRCA (from PathText), HistAI, and MICCAI REG—selected to cover complementary levels of disease specificity, institutional diversity, report homogeneity, and annotation noise. Table 2 summarizes the key differences among these datasets and provides context for the quantitative results in Section 4.1. In particular, TCGA-BRCA represents a disease-specific setting with relatively lower diversity but noisier reports, HistAI reflects a more heterogeneous multi-institutional setting with less standardized reporting, and MICCAI REG provides a comparatively cleaner and more homogeneous benchmark. Evaluating across these three settings allows us to assess not only absolute report-generation quality, but also the robustness of the proposed method under varying reporting conventions and data characteristics. 

PathText (TCGA-BRCA subset). We use the breast cancer subset of PathText, a publicly available pathology report generation dataset derived from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA), consisting of whole-slide histopathology images paired with corresponding diagnostic reports [ 4]. Relative to the other datasets, this subset is more disease-specific and exhibits higher annotation noise, making it useful for evaluating whether the model can capture specialized diagnostic terminology and morphology despite less standardized reports. WSIs and reports were split at the patient level following the protocol described in the original dataset publication [4]. 

HistAI. The HistAI dataset provides high-resolution histopathology slides with associated structured and free-text annotations curated for clinical AI research [ 2]. As summarized in Table 2, HistAI is more institutionally diverse and less homogeneous in reporting structure than MICCAI REG, making it a useful test bed for evaluating robustness to inter-site variability and differences in reporting style. We therefore use HistAI to assess how well the model generalizes when both the visual distribution and linguistic form of reports become more heterogeneous. 

MICCAI REG Challenge Dataset. The MICCAI Report Generation (REG) challenge dataset comprises histopathol-ogy images paired with expert-authored diagnostic reports, designed specifically for benchmarking automated report generation methods [ 24 ]. Compared with TCGA-BRCA and HistAI, this dataset is comparatively cleaner and more homogeneous in report style, as indicated in Table 2. We include it to measure performance in a standardized evaluation setting and to determine whether the proposed method remains effective even when reporting conventions are more uniform. 

4.2 Image Preprocessing and Patch Sampling 

WSIs were tessellated into 512 ×512 non-overlapping patches at 20 × magnification. Background regions were removed using standard tissue detection heuristics based on thresholding and tissue-region filtering [ 16 ]. For each slide, a fixed number of patches was sampled uniformly to ensure computational tractability and consistent batch statistics. Patch-level visual features were extracted using the CONCH [ 21 ] foundation model, producing fixed-dimensional embeddings for each patch. These features were frozen during training to isolate the contribution of the proposed architecture. Global slide representations were obtained using TITAN [ 7 ], capturing coarse-grained tissue context complementary to patch-level features. 

4.3 Concept Extraction and Representation 

Diagnostic concepts were derived through a hybrid pipeline combining large language model-based extraction and expert validation, following the setup in [ 15 ]. This process ensured both scalability and clinical relevance. Concept representations were obtained using GECKO [ 15 ], producing embeddings aligned with the language modeling space. These embeddings were treated as structured semantic priors and used as conditioning signals during both encoding and decoding. 

4.4 Training Details 

Models were trained using the AdamW optimizer with weight decay applied to all non-normalization parameters [ 19 ]. Learning rates were scheduled using cosine annealing with warm restarts [ 18 ]. To mitigate overfitting, we applied 11 dropout across attention and feed-forward layers, as well as within FiLM modulation networks. Training was conducted for a fixed number of epochs, with model selection based on validation performance. At inference time, reports were generated autoregressively using beam search with fixed decoding parameters across validation and test sets. All models were evaluated using identical input features to ensure that performance differences reflect architectural improvements rather than feature extraction. Unless otherwise specified, decoding parameters were fixed across validation and test sets to ensure comparability. 

4.5 Evaluation Metrics 

Generated reports were evaluated using standard natural language generation metrics, including: • BLEU (1–4) [ 26 ], which measures n-gram overlap between generated and reference reports and is reported from unigram to four-gram precision. • ROUGE-L [ 17 ], which captures the longest common subsequence between the generated and reference text and reflects sentence-level content preservation. • METEOR [ 1], which evaluates unigram alignment while accounting for stemming and synonymy, providing a complementary measure of semantic similarity. 

# 5 Related Work 

Automated Medical Report Generation: 

Automated generation of clinical reports from medical images has been studied extensively in radiology, where the task is commonly framed as translating imaging evidence into structured or semi-structured diagnostic narratives[ 13 ,9, 3, 30 , 23 ]. Early approaches focused on template-based or retrieval-based systems that mapped detected visual findings to predefined report sections. While effective in constrained settings, these systems lacked flexibility and did not generalize well to diverse pathologies or reporting styles. The advent of deep learning shifted the field toward end-to-end image-to-text generation, inspired by image captioning in natural images. Encoder–decoder architectures combining convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with recurrent neural networks (RNNs)[ 29 ] were among the first to demonstrate feasibility for radiology report generation. Subsequent works introduced attention mechanisms to align image regions with textual tokens[ 28 ], significantly improving descriptive accuracy and coherence. More recently, transformer-based architectures have become the dominant paradigm. Models such as Show-Attend-and-Tell variants, hierarchical LSTM–Transformer hybrids, and fully transformer-based frameworks have been proposed for chest X-ray and CT report generation[ 5, 6, 25 ]. Several studies emphasized hierarchical decoding, where sentence-level and word-level generators reflect the structured nature of radiology reports. Others incorporated graph-based disease dependencies, reinforcement learning with clinical reward functions, or contrastive learning objectives to improve factual correctness and reduce hallucinations. Despite these advances, radiology report generation typically benefits from relatively low-resolution images, standardized acquisition protocols, and well-defined diagnostic vocabularies, assumptions that do not hold in histopathology. 

Vision–Language Models in Medical Imaging: Parallel to domain-specific advances, large-scale vision–language models (VLMs) such as CLIP and ALIGN demonstrated that joint embedding of images and text at scale enables strong cross-modal generalization. These models inspired a wave of multimodal architectures for medical applications, including contrastive pretraining on image–text pairs and prompt-based adaptation. However, most generic VLMs operate at a global image level and lack mechanisms for fine-grained reasoning over multiple spatial instances, which is essential for whole-slide pathology images. Moreover, while VLMs learn semantic alignment, they do not inherently encode domain-specific diagnostic concepts or provide structured interpretability aligned with clinical reasoning. 

Concept-guided and interpretable learning in pathology: Concept-based learning approaches aim to improve interpretability by introducing intermediate semantic representations. In medical imaging, such methods often rely on predefined concepts or post hoc explanations to link predictions to human-interpretable features. Recent works have explored the use of concept-based representations to improve interpretability in computational pathology. Approaches such as SI-MIL [ 14 ] and GECKO [ 15 ] leverage semantic concepts derived from either expert annotations or large-scale language models to explain model predictions or guide representation learning. These methods typically associate learned visual features with clinically meaningful concepts, enabling post hoc interpretation or weakly supervised concept discovery. While effective for interpretability, such approaches are primarily designed for classification or representation analysis and do not directly integrate concept signals into generative modeling pipelines. In particular, 12 concept information is often used as an auxiliary objective or explanatory layer rather than as an active conditioning signal during inference. 

Computational Pathology and Report Generation: Computational pathology introduces additional complexity due to the gigapixel resolution of WSIs and the hierarchical organization of tissue morphology. Early deep learning approaches in pathology focused on patch-level classification or slide-level prediction using multiple instance learning (MIL). These methods aggregate patch features to produce slide-level labels but do not generate explanatory text. The extension from classification to free-text report generation is non-trivial. Diagnostic pathology reports encode not only visual findings but also conceptual abstractions (e.g., architectural patterns, cytologic features, grading criteria) and clinical reasoning that integrates observations across spatial scales. WSI-Caption[4] was among the first to formalize WSI-to-report generation as a multiple-instance generative problem. By aggregating patch-level representations and generating reports at the slide level, MI-Gen demonstrated that joint vision–language learning is feasible at WSI scale. Additionally, MI-Gen showed that report supervision can improve downstream diagnostic tasks. Nevertheless, the model largely treats the report as a monolithic output sequence and does not explicitly model diagnostic concepts or multimodal reasoning pathways. HistGen [ 10 ] addressed the mismatch between dense visual information and concise diagnostic text by introducing a local–global hierarchical encoder and cross-modal context interaction. Its hierarchical design improves alignment between visual granularity and linguistic abstraction and represents a significant step forward in structured WSI representation learning. However, HistGen primarily emphasizes feature aggregation and alignment, rather than adaptive conditioning or explicit concept-level reasoning during generation. Bi-Gen [ 31 ] further explored semantic enrichment by retrieving historical diagnostic knowledge associated with visually salient regions. By jointly learning from WSIs and retrieved textual knowledge, Bi-Gen improves semantic consistency and reduces redundancy. While effective, this approach relies on external knowledge retrieval and bi-modal fusion, and it does not explicitly disentangle representation refinement from reasoning or provide transparent control over how different modalities influence individual report statements. These approaches demonstrate strong fluency and improved alignment with ground truth reports. However, they typically rely on fixed visual representations extracted prior to decoding or incorporate auxiliary features derived from the same patch embeddings. As a result, they lack mechanisms for dynamically integrating multi-scale contextual information and may produce text that is only weakly grounded in diagnostically relevant visual evidence. In contrast to prior approaches, our framework integrates concept representations as first-class conditioning signals and enables progressive interaction between local and global features through depth-wise modulation. Furthermore, our adaptive gated fusion mechanism allows dynamic, token-level control over modality contributions during report generation, providing both improved performance and enhanced interpretability. 

# 6 Discussions and Conclusion 

In this work, we present a multimodal framework for pathology report generation that explicitly integrates local visual evidence, global slide context, and structured diagnostic concepts through progressive conditioning and adaptive fusion. Our approach advances beyond prior report generation models that rely primarily on hierarchical aggregation or static fusion mechanisms by combining multi-scale visual representations with structured diagnostic concepts and adaptive multimodal reasoning. Our results demonstrate that this design improves both quantitative performance and qualitative coherence across diverse datasets, suggesting that incorporating structured semantic priors and iterative context refinement can address key limitations of existing approaches. A central contribution of this work is the introduction of progressive context-aware encoding, in which patch-level representations are repeatedly modulated by evolving slide-level and concept-level features. This design departs from conventional pipelines that treat visual representations as fixed inputs to language models. Instead, it enables a bidirectional interaction between local observations and global context, more closely resembling the iterative reasoning process used in clinical pathology. The observed improvements in higher-order language metrics and cross-dataset generalization indicate that such dynamic conditioning is beneficial for capturing complex multi-scale dependencies and enables the model to extract features that are both discriminative and diagnostically meaningful. The proposed multimodal decoder further extends this paradigm by employing adaptive multi-way gated fusion, allowing patch-level morphology, slide-level summaries, and explicit concepts to contribute dynamically to report generation. This contrasts with existing methods that either fuse modalities at a single stage or rely on implicit cross-attention without explicit control over modality contributions. The resulting reports demonstrate improved coherence and reduced redundancy, and the learned fusion weights provide a degree of transparency into how different sources of evidence 13 influence individual report statements. This property is particularly important for clinical applications, where trust and interpretability are essential for adoption. The incorporation of explicit diagnostic concepts as conditioning signals represents another key aspect of this work. Unlike prior approaches that use concepts as auxiliary supervision or post hoc explanations, our framework integrates them throughout both encoding and decoding. This design encourages alignment between generated text and clinically meaningful semantic structures, which may contribute to improved robustness and interpretability. More broadly, it highlights the potential of combining foundation model representations with structured domain knowledge in medical vision–language tasks. Our evaluation across TCGA-BRCA, HistAI, and the MICCAI REG datasets highlights another important aspect of the proposed framework: generalization. While many pathology report generation models perform well on in-distribution test sets, performance often degrades substantially under domain shift. The consistent gains observed on the MICCAI REG dataset suggest that explicitly separating representation refinement from multimodal reasoning, and grounding both in structured concepts, yields representations that are more stable across institutions and acquisition protocols. This finding has implications beyond report generation, indicating that concept-aware modulation may serve as a general strategy for robust multimodal learning in pathology. The disease-specific analysis on the TCGA-BRCA also illustrates the benefits of explicit concept integration. Breast cancer diagnosis relies on well-defined histomorphological criteria, and the improved performance observed in this setting suggests that the proposed approach is particularly effective when domain concepts are strongly codified. This opens opportunities for extending the framework to other cancer types and subspecialties where diagnostic criteria are similarly structured, such as prostate or gastrointestinal pathology. Despite these advances, this work has several limitations. First, the quality of concept-level supervision plays a critical role in model performance. Although we leverage LLM-assisted concept extraction validated by expert pathologists, errors or omissions in concept annotations may propagate through the model. Second, while we demonstrate improved interpretability via fusion weights and concept coverage, further work is needed to develop standardized evaluation protocols for explainability in pathology report generation. Finally, the computational cost associated with multi-level modulation and gated fusion may pose challenges for large-scale deployment, motivating future research into more efficient approximations. The proposed framework also offers several promising directions for extension. Future work could explore joint optimization of visual encoders and report generation models, as well as more robust methods for automated concept extraction and validation. Incorporating longitudinal patient data or molecular features could further enrich report generation and support integrative diagnostics. Additionally, coupling the model with interactive or human-in-the-loop systems may enable pathologists to guide or refine generated reports, fostering collaboration between human expertise and automated reasoning. Beyond pathology, the principles introduced here, such as the depth-aware contextual modulation and adaptive multimodal fusion, may generalize to other medical domains where complex visual evidence must be translated into structured clinical narratives. In conclusion, our results suggest that progressive contextual conditioning and adaptive multimodal fusion provide a flexible and effective framework for pathology report generation that aligns closely with clinical reasoning processes. By unifying multi-scale visual features, explicit diagnostic concepts, and adaptive reasoning mechanisms, the proposed framework establishes a foundation for more interpretable, robust, and clinically meaningful automated reporting systems. We believe this represents an important step toward trustworthy multimodal AI tools for computational pathology and beyond.
