Abstract 28 

Prostate cancer displays substantial clinical and histopathological heterogeneity which is 29 

not fully captured by conventional Gleason grading. To resolve the spatial and phenotypic 30 

complex ity of the prostate tumor microenvironment , we performed imaging mass cytometry 31 

using a prostate -tailored 3 4-plex antibody panel on a clinically annotated tissue microarray 32 

cohort of 19 5 patients of primary stage disease after radical prostatectomy (5 23 regions of 33 

interest; 2 .19 million cells) . We identified 34 distinct cell types spanning epithelial, 34 

endothelial, stromal and immune compartments, and further organiz ed into 18 epithelial -35 

dominated, cancer associated fibroblast -dominated, and immune -rich spatial niches .36 

Within the epithelial compartment, we detect ed an ERG ⁺p53 ⁺ luminal population whose 37 

abundance is independently associated with poor overall and progression -free survival. In 38 

the stroma, we define d extracellular matrix remodeling -related cancer associated fibroblast 39 

and smooth muscle cell lineages, including a periglandular CD105 high niche with strong 40 

stromal -immune connectivity that is selectively associated with worse clinical outcome. 41 

Finally, cumulative immune niche burden correlate d with histological inflammation and 42 

stratifies for worse patient survival . Together, these data provide a spatially resolved single -43 

cell atlas of primary PCa and reveal stromal -immune -epithelial niches with prognostic 44 

relevance beyond Gleason grade. 45

Introduction 47 

Prostate cancer (PCa) displays extensive intra -tumoral and inter -patient heterogeneity, 48 

ranging from indolent to clinically progressive tumors despite definitive local therapy 1–6.49 

Current clinical risk stratification relies heavily on histopathological Gleason grading; 50 

however, intermediate Gleason grade categories frequently fail to capture the biological 51 

diversity and prognostic variability observed among patients 7,8 . This limitation contributes to 52 

both over - and undertreatment and underscores the need for improved biomarkers that 53 

more accurately reflect tumor biology and predict clinical outcome s.54 

Increasing evidence suggests that this shortcoming arises partly from the complex and 55 

spatially organized architecture of the tumor microenvironment (TME), not fully captured on 56 

standard diagnostic slides 9–11 . At the epithelial level, PCa typically arises from luminal 57 

epithelial cells within glands that canonically harbor basal and luminal lineages 58 

interspersed with rare neuroendocrine (NE) cells 12,13 . Single cell atlases of the prostate and 59 

proximal urethra have expanded the epithelial repertoire to include club -like and hillock -like 60 

cells, which are linked to epithelial homeostasis and injury responsive programs and can 61 

emerge under inflammatory conditions 14 –16 . These noncanonical states illustrate the 62 

marked epithelial plasticity of the prostate, enabling transitions toward androgen receptor 63 

(AR ) independent phenotypes, including treatment emergent neuroendocrine prostate 64

Although traditionally considered “immune cold,” localized PCa contains a structured and 67 

functionally constrained immune microenvironment. Spatial and single cell analyses show 68 

that immune evasion reflects not only limited infiltration but also spatially restricted 69 

suppression, including exhausted T cells juxtaposed with suppressive myeloid populations 70 

and stromal programs that bias cytokine signaling 4,16,19 . Moreover, immune -epithelial 71 

interactions involving club -like epithelial cells, immunosuppressive myeloid populations, 72 

and androgen receptor (AR) -positive stromal cells define coordinated multicellular hubs 73 

within the tumor microenvironment that are increasingly recognized as clinically 74 

relevant 16,20 –26 .75 

The stromal compartment, comprising smooth muscle cells (SMCs), cancer associated 76 

fibroblasts (CAFs), pericytes, and extracellular matrix (ECM), represents a major yet 77 

incompletely characterized component of the PCa TME. Single -cell transcriptomics has 78 

outlined diverse CAF states with myofibroblastic, inflammatory, and antigen -presenting 79 

features, and has linked stromal programs to angiogenesis, immune modulation, bone 80 

metastasis and androgen deprivation therapy ( ADT ) resistance 27 –31 . However, the spatial 81 

organization, lineage relationships, and clinical relevance of these stromal phenotypes, and 82 

their interactions with immune and epithelial compartments, remain poorly resolved. 83 

Spatially resolved single -cell proteomic technologies, such as imaging mass cytometry 84 

(IMC) 32 , now enable multiplexed in situ mapping of the TME at cellular resolution. While IMC 85 

studies have provided insights into CAF biology in other tumor types 33,34 , a comparable 86 

spatial proteomic atlas of primary PCa is lacking. 87 

Here, we construct a spatially resolved single -cell atlas of primary PCa using a prostate 88 

tissue -tailored 34 -plex IMC panel applied to a large, clinically annotated radical 89 

prostatectomy tissue microarray (195 patients, 523 regions, 2.19 million cells). We resolve 90 

epithelial, stromal, endothelial, and immune cellular populations and their organization into 91 

compartments, define CAF/SMC heterogeneity , and identify 18 recurrent spatial niches ,92 

that form hierarchical epithelial -stromal -immune ecosystems. We further evaluate the 93 

clinical relevance of these arch itectures, including a CD105 ⁺ periglandular CAF niche, that 94 

extend risk stratification beyond conventional histopathology .95

Spatial proteomics defines the complex and heterogeneous architecture of primary 98 

PCa TME 99 

We performed a comprehensive spatial proteomic analysis using imaging mass cytometry 100 

(IMC) on a tissue microarray (TMA) cohort of 19 5 primary PCa specimens . Quality control 101 

filtering removed regions with staining artifacts and segmentation errors, resulting in a final 102 

dataset of 5 23 high -quality ROIs and 2.1 9 million single cells for downstream analysis. This 103 

approach enabled high -dimensional, spatially resolved single -cell analysis of epithelial, 104 

stromal, immune, and endothelial compartments within intact tissue architecture ,105 

including quantitative compositional analysis of cell populations, and spatial organization 106 

of niches (Fig. 1a) . The cohort includes treatment -naive primary tumors collected at radical 107 

prostatectomy (RP) as part of the European Multicenter Prostate Cancer Clinical and 108 

Translational research group (EMPaCT) 35,36 and is supported by comprehensive clinical and 109 

pathological annotations includ ing patient -level Gleason grade group, and longitudinal 110 

clinical endpoints such as recurrence , progression , and overall survival (Methods, 111 

Supplementary Fig. 1a). The TMA includes up to four cores per patient , capturing the 112 

multifocal tissue architecture and intra -patient heterogeneity of PCa. Histopathological 113 

evaluation for core -level Gleason group, presence of reactive stroma (stromogenic features), 114 

and inflammation status was performed by an expert pathologist on serial sections stained 115 

with hematoxylin and eosin (H&E). 116 

Histopathological review of matched H&E sections confirmed the expected multifocal 117 

architecture of PCa and revealed marked differences with respect to Gleason grade across 118 

cores from the same patient (Supplementary Fig. 1b) . In 72.9% of patients, at least one of all 119 

core s displayed a Gleason pattern that differed from the patient -level Gleason grade group, 120 

including cases where high -grade foci occurred in tumors clinically classified as low grade 121 

(Supplementary Fig. 1b). Th is finding underscores the epiphenomenon of intratumoral 122 

heterogeneity of primary PCa and further highlights the limitations of conventional Gleason 123 

grading in capturing clinically relevant disease biology , thereby motivat ing the need for 124 

spatially resolved molecular characterization of the PCa TME 37 .125 

Using a 34 -plex IMC antibody panel, we resolved the major cellular compartments of the 126 

PCa TME, including epithelial, stromal, immune , and endothelial populations (Fig. 1 b). The 127 

IMC panel was designed to capture both canonical cell lineages and functional states, with 128 

particular emphasis on resolving stromal heterogeneity (SMCs, pericytes, and diverse CAF 129 

states, including inflammatory and matrix -remodeling programs ). CAF and SMC marker 130 

selection was informed by r ecent single -cell transcriptomic analysis of primary PCa 38 , with 131 

carboxylesterase 1 (CES1) and CD105 selected as inflammatory ( iCAF ) and myofibroblast 132 

CAF (myCAF ) marker s, respectively . YAP1 phosphorylated at tyrosine 357 (pYAP) was 133

Single -cell phenotyping reveals diverse epithelial, immune and stromal 154 

subpopulations .155 

To systematically define cellular populations within the PCa TME , we performed single -156 

nuclei segment ation and quality control followed by unsupervised cluster ing and marker -157 

based cell annotat ion (Fig. 2 a). This yielded a total of 2 ’191 ’967 high -quality single -cell 158 

profiles across all patients , classified into 34 cell types . T he epithelial compartment 159 

represented the largest fraction (48%), followed by stromal (30.3%) , immune (15.1% ), and 160 

endothelial compartment s (3.7%) , while a small fraction of cells (3%) remained un classified 161 

and was excluded from the analysis . UMAP visualization demonstrated a clear separation 162 

between epithelial and stromal compartments (Fig. 2 b) , with cells from different patients 163 

largely intermixing across clusters. Only a small number of rare subclusters appeared to be 164 

patient -specific, consistent with biological heterogeneity rather than batch effects. 165 

Within the epithelial compartment, 9 phenotypically distinct populations were identified ,166 

including luminal (KRT8/18 +, AR +) and basal ( KRT5 +, p63 +) epithelial cells , and a rare mixed 167 

cluster with both luminal and basal markers (KRT8/18 + KRT5 +), suggestive of intermediate or 168 

transient states . Additiona l subpopulations captured distinct proliferative and oncogenic 169 

programs , including luminal Ki67 + proliferative cells, luminal ERG + tumor cells consistent 170 

with TMPRSS2 -ERG fusion, and luminal p53 -expressing cells ( Fig. 2a; Supplementary Fig. 171 

2a). Notably, a small subset of ERG + cells (6.1% ) also expressed p53, potentially reflecting 172 

tumor cells under genotoxic stress or enriched for TP53 mutations. Neuroendocrine (N E)173 

cells identified by synaptophysin express ion constituted one of the smallest populations, 174 

consistent with recent studies implicating NE plasticity during disease progression and 175 

therapeutic resistance 18,44 .176

Identification of distinct CAF populations with ECM -remodeling and SMC -like 202 

properties 203 

We next focused on resolving fibroblast heterogeneity within the non -immune stroma. 204 

Unsupervised clustering of stromal cells identified 10 distinct CAF populations, reflecting 205 

diverse phenotypic and functional states (Fig. 2a, 3a -b). Most stromal cells separated into 206

Cell type composition and association with clinical outcome 273 

To investigate whether variation in cell -type composition relates to clinical outcome, we 274 

aggregated cell -type frequencies at a patient -level and performed hierarchical clustering ,275 

revealing six patient groups ( P1-P6) with distinct cell type composition s (Fig. 4 a-b) . The two 276 

largest clusters ( P3 and P5) were characterized by relative enrichment for luminal epithelial 277 

and tumor epithelial cells , respectively ; P2 and P4 were enriched for diverse CAF and SMC 278 

populations , whereas P6 was predominantly enriched for immune populations (Fig. 4a -b). 279 

To assess whether the intra -patient heterogeneity previously observed across Gleason 280 

grade groups is also reflected at the level of cellular compositions, we independently 281 

clustered core -level cell type distributions , identifying 6 core -level groups (C1 -C6) 282 

(Supplementary Fig. 3a) . Consistent with the patient -level analysis, C2 and C5 were 283 

dominated by luminal epithelial and tumor epithelial cells, respectively. We next evaluated 284 

th e correspondence between core -level and patient -level clusters . We observe d285 

pronounced intra -patient heterogeneity , with cores from the same patient frequently 286 

assigned to different compositional clusters (Supplementary Fig. 3 b). However, C2 and C5 287 

appeared largely mutually exclusive : patient s with at least one core in C2 did not tend to 288 

ha rbor core s in C5 , and vice versa, suggesting the presence of distinct, patient -specific 289 

epithelial dominant programs rather than co -existing compositional states. 290

Discussion 508 

While single -cell and spatial transcriptomics have started to untangle the PCa 509 

microenvironment, its spatial organization at the protein level remains unclear 4,14,62 . Here, 510 

we present a large -scale, spatially resolved single -cell proteomic atlas of the primary PCa, 511 

integrating epithelial, stromal, endothelial, and immune phenotypes across 1 95 patients .512 

We identified 18 recurrent spatial niches that reveal how localized PCa organizes into 513 

multicellular local ecosystems. Importantly, we demonstrate that spatial context provides 514 

prognostically meaningful information that cannot be inferred from cell -type abundance or 515 

Gleason grade alone. 516 

Within the epithelial compartment, we identif y an ERG +p53 + luminal cell state that robustly 517 

predicts both poor overall and progression -free survival . Strong nuclear p53 accumulation 518 

is consistent with TP53 missense mutations with adverse pathology 63 and recent work in 519 

mouse models indicates that ERG and p53 alterations cooperate in driving aggressive 520 

disease 64 . The prognostic relevance of this co -expressing population, absent for ERG or p53 521 

alone, supports a synergistic biological process, potentially reflecting a sequential 522 

evolutionary trajectory in which TMPRSS2 -ERG fusion precedes TP53 disruption. Notably, 523 

ERG +p53 + cells were present even in low -grade regions and frequently co -occurred with 524 

immune -rich niches, suggesting that epithelial genomic instability may arise within , and be 525 

sustained by , specific inflammatory microenvironments . Recent work in mouse prostate 526 

organoids support this model, show ing that ERG ‑overexpressing epithelial cells actively 527

Together, these observations point to a co -evolution of ERG +, p 53 -defective epithelial states 532

evasion. 534 

Our spatial analysis further points to substantial heterogeneity within the stromal 535 

compartment . We distinguish CAF populations with typical myCAF features, from 536 

contractile, SMC population s, and further stratify these based on CD105, CD146, CES1, 537 

EGR1 and AR expression , with the latter co -expressed in SMCs with YAP and CES1. Within 538 

the stromal compartment, CD105 (endoglin) is a notable feature: a TGFβ coreceptor 539 

enriched on angiogenic endothelium and prominently expressed by activated CAF 540 

populations. Its expression has been repeatedly linked to angiogenesis and stromal 541 

remodelin g, and mechanistic studies indicate that CD105 + CAFs potentiate neuroendocrine 542 

differentiation and ADT resistance 39,69,70 . We have also previously shown that CD105 + CAFs 543 

have low AR expression and exhibit in sensitivity to AR blockade , whereby disrupti on of the 544 

NFκB -TGFβ1 -YAP1 signaling axis could curb this specific myCAF activity 39 . Importantly, 545 

findings herein demonstrate that the biological and clinical relevance of myCAF is highly 546 

dependent on spatial context : myCAFs cells preferentially organize into a small, 547 

periglandular niche enriched in histologically stromogenic regions and show the strongest 548 

association with adverse outcome . This niche exhibits extensive connectivity between 549 

my CAFs and multiple immune populations, including macrophages, T helper cells, and 550 

fibrocytes, as well as other CAF subsets, pointing to a highly interconnected stromal -551 

immune hub coordinating multicellular interactions across compartments. Therapeutically, 552 

targeting CD105 has been proposed as a means to reduce CAF -mediated signaling and 553 

promote vascular normalization 71 . Such interventions could reprogram the stroma from a 554 

driver of immune exclusion into a microenvironment that supports effective antitumor 555 

immune responses. Our data further refine this concept by suggesting that targeting 556 

my CAFs may require consideration of their spatial organization and interacting partners ,557 

and position CD105 not simply as a marker, but as a mechanistic node at the stromal -558 

vascular interface . As myofibroblast -like cells, CD105 + CAFs contribute to ECM deposition, 559

biomechanical effects, a stiffened ECM also acts as an immunological barrier, limiting 562 

lymphocyte infiltration and impairing anti -tumor immunity 33,74 . Immunosuppressive 563 

properties have been linked with myCAF populations implicating macrophages and 564 

perturbed T -helper cell function 75 . In this context, the adverse prognostic impact of the 565 

myCAF -rich niche (which includes direct interactions with macrophages and T -helper cells) 566

Beyond stromal organization, our analysis also identifies structured immune niches with 569

Taken together, our findings show that stromogenic histology alone cannot capture the 580 

clinically relevant complexity of the PCa microenvironment. Instead, spatially resolved 581 

analyses reveal that distinct multicellular architectures, ranging from the periglandular 582 

my CAF rich niche to broader epithelial, stromal, and immune ecosystems, encode 583 

prognostic information that is not evident from cellular composition alone. These results 584 

underscore the importance of integrating spatially defined CAF phenotypes and higher -585 

order tissue organization to identify biologically meaningful tumor –stroma interfaces and 586 

improve patient risk stratification. 587 

Several limitations should be considered. First, TLS -like structures were inferred based on 588 

spatial organization without formal assessment of maturation or localization, which may 589 

influence their clinical significance 59 . Similarly, stromogenic status was assessed using 590 

non -standardized histopathological criteria, potentially limiting its prognostic resolution 60 .591 

Second, while IMC enables high -dimensional protein profiling, it remains restricted to a 592 

predefined marker panel and does not capture the full spectrum of cellular states, 593 

particularly within the myeloid and T cell compartments. Finally, the slow progression and 594 

relatively low number of clinical events, typical for localized PCa cohorts 77 , limits statistical 595 

power for survival analyses and underscores the need for independent external validation. 596 

Future studies integrating IMC with spatial transcriptomics and genomic profiling will be 597 

critical to link protein -defined niches to transcriptional programs and mutational 598 

landscapes. In particular, dissecting the functional interplay between ERG ⁺p53 ⁺ epithelial 599 

states and myCAF stromal niches may reveal actionable dependencies. Experimental 600 

models, including organoid -stroma co -culture systems, and stroma isolation 38 will be 601 

essential to establish causal relationships and test whether genetic or pharmacologic 602

Cell Phenotyping 752 

Cell -type annotation was performed using an iterative clustering and marker -based 753 

annotation strategy. First, all cells were embedded and clustered in an unsupervised 754 

manner: a k-nearest neighbors (k -NN) graph of all cells was computed using the 755 

rapids_singlecell (version 0.4.0) implementation of Parametric UMAP 86 , followed by 756 

community detection using the Leiden algorithm 87 , as implemented in the igraph package 757 

(https://igraph.org ). 758 

Clusters were subsequently annotated based on canonical marker expression profiles. To 759 

increase phenotypic resolution, we applied a hierarchical refinement strategy in which 760 

major cellular compartments were first identified and then re -clustered independently. At 761 

the top level, clusters were grouped into immune and non -immune populations based on 762 

the expression of canonical immune markers (e.g., CD3, CD4, CD8a, CD20, CD68, FoxP3, 763 

CD11b, CD66b). The immune compartment was then re -clustered using all immune -764 

specific markers, yielding 11 distinct immune cell populations (Fig. 2a). 765 

The non -immune compartment was further subdivided into epithelial, endothelial, and 766 

stromal lineages based on established markers. Epithelial cells were identified using pan -767 

keratin, E -cadherin, KRT8/18, KRT5, and p63, and further resolved into nine phenotypic 768 

groups using lineage and functional markers (e.g., AR, PSA, Ki -67, p53, YAP1). A focused re -769 

clustering of basal -like populations improved separation into basal, luminal, and 770 

transitional states. Endothelial cells were defined by CD31 and ERG expression and further 771 

subdivided into three phenotypes. Remaining non -immune, non -endothelial cells were 772 

assigned to the stromal compartment and re -clustered using mesenchymal and fibroblast -773 

associated markers (e.g., α -SMA, collagen I, vimentin, CD146, CD105, PDPN), while 774 

excluding CD45+ and CD31+ populations. This approach identified multiple stromal 775 

subpopulations, forming the basis for downstream analysis of fibroblast heterogeneity. 776 

Cells that could not be confidently assigned at intermediate stages were labeled as 777 

undefined and re -clustered using the full marker panel. Due to the small size of immune 778 

cells and the imaging resolution (~1 μm), a minority of cells exhibited apparent double 779 

positivity (e.g., CD4 ⁺CD20 ⁺), likely reflecting segmentation limitations in densely packed 780 

regions rather than true co -expression. 781 

Overall, this iterative framework combines unsupervised clustering with marker -based 782 

annotation to achieve robust and biologically consistent cell -type classification while 783

Spatial Niche Identification 819 

Tissue niches were identified using a neighborhood -based approach adapted from previous 820 

study 57 . For each IMC core, a cell -cell proximity graph was generated: each cell  𝑖  was 821 

connected to all neighboring cells within a 32 -pixel radius ( ≈32  𝜇 m). The local neighborhood 822 

composition of cell  𝑖  was quantified as the vector  𝑓 𝑖 = (𝑓 𝑖 1, 𝑓 𝑖 2, … , 𝑓 𝑖𝑇 ), where T denotes the 823

the PCa TME. These local representations were subsequently clustered to identify recurrent 827 

multi -cell interaction patterns corresponding to putative TME niches. Clustering was 828 

performed using k -means (scikit -learn implementation) with a Euclidean distance metric 829 

and k -means++ initialization, testing k = 24 clusters . To avoid spurious niche definitions, a830 

post -clustering filtering step retained only clusters present in at least 5 patients and 831 

containing at least 15 cells per sample occurrence. 832 

To evaluate clustering robustness, k -means was repeated 50 times with different random 833 

seeds, and clustering partitions were compared using the adjusted Rand index (ARI) 88 , as 834 

implemented in scikit -learn. The ARI quantifies the probability that a pair of cells is assigned 835 

to the same niche in both runs, adjusting for chance. An ARI of 1 indicates identical niche 836 

assignments, whereas values close to zero indicate random assignments. The identified 837 

niches showed high agreement between runs (Supplementary Fig.7a) . The final niche 838 

assignment was selected as the run with maximal overall agreement to all other runs 839 

(highlighted in Supplementary Fig .7a) . One niche was excluded after filtering, and the 840 

remaining 23 niches were manually validated based on cell -type composition and spatial 841 

co -localization, and further examined for biological plausibility, leading to the merging of five 842 

niches, and yielding a final set of 18 distinct niches .843

Kaplan -Meier Survival Analysis 873 

Kaplan -Meier survival curves were used to assess associations between clinical outcomes 874 

(overall survival and progression -free survival) and features derived from cell -type 875 

composition, spatial niche organization, composite niche scores, and histopathological 876 

annotations. Survival time was defined as the interval from diagnosis to event (death or 877 

disease progression) or last follow -up. For each feature of interest, patients were stratified 878 

into groups based on predefined criteria. 879 

For features quantified at the core level (e.g., cell -type or niche abundance), patients were 880 

stratified based on aggregated core -level measurements. Specifically , each core was 881 

classified as high or low for the cell type or niche of interest based on the cohort -wide 882 

median proportion of the respective cell type /niche , computed across cores with non -zero 883 

cell type /niche abundance. Patient -level groups were then defined by aggregating core -level 884 

labels: a patient was assigned to the high group if at least one core was classified as high, 885 

and to the low group otherwise. 886
