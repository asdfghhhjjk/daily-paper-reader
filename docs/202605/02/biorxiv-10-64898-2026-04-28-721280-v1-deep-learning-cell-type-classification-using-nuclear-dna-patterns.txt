Multicellular organisms comprise various types of cells, which are characterized by gene 10 

expression through interactions between chromosomal DNA and nuclear proteins. Many cutting -11 

edge methods have been  developed to reveal the three -dimensional organization of chromosomes. 12 

The detailed analyses of whole chromosomes have begun to uncover structural features specific to 13 

several cell types. Here, we show that cell types are instantly and highly accurately classified using 14 

conventional DNA staining and a convolutional neural network (CNN). A high -resolution single 15 

slice image of the nucleus is sufficient for the accurate classification of both live and fixed cells, 16 

including neurons and non -neural cells. These findings suggest that there may be cell -type -specific 17 

features decipherable by deep learning in a thin two -dimensional slice of the nucleus. 18

INTRODUCTION 19 

Many cell types have been found since the microscope was developed. Initially , cell types were 20 

classified based on their morphologie s. 1 Then , molecular markers such as proteins and RNAs that 21 

are specifically expressed in a cell type of interest have allowed for its precise classification. Their 22 

expression is regulated by many nuclear proteins, including transcription factors and various types 23 

of histones, the binding sites of which are mapped  by  chromatin immunoprecipitation. 24 

Chromosomal loci in spatial proximity to each other are analy zed by chromatin conformation 25 

capture techniques, such as high -throughput chromosome conformation captur e.  2,3  The positions 26 

of chromosomal loci, RNAs and proteins are directly visuali zed by in situ hybridization such as 27 

DNA seqFISH+ and immunostainin g.  4 Each chromosome occupies a territory, albeit partially 28 

overlapping with other chromosomes, and is separated into structures, such as the A and B 29 

compartments, which are enriched for transcription ally  active and inactive genes, respectively, and 30 

further subdivided  into topologically associating domains and  chromatin  loops. Whereas bulk 31 

analyses using a population of cells suggest the existence of cell -type -specific structural features 32 

in chromosomes of some cell types, single -cell analyses have revealed cell -to -cell heterogeneity 33 

even  within the same cell typ e.  5

34 

On the other hand , CNNs, a  type of  deep -learning  algorithm , have been widely used for 35 

image recognition and classificatio n,  6-9 and  serv e as diagnostic tool s in the clini c.  10  Moreover, 36 

CNNs have been applied to predict primary origins for cancers using tissue  section s 11  and to 37 

analyze cellular states, such as cell cycle phases, virus infection and senescence, using DNA -38 

stained images of nucle i.  12 -14  Here, we present a simple yet powerful appr oach combining 39 

conventional DNA staining with CNNs  for  the classification  of cell types . After staining nuclei 40 

with a fluorescent dye,  Hoechst 33342 (Hoechst) or 4’,6 -diamidino -2-phenylindole (DAPI), we 41

RESULTS 52 

Classification of  live cultured cells 53 

We sought to determine whether live cultured cells might be classified based on the features of 54 

their own nuclei. Four  well -characterized  mouse cells were cultured: P19 embryonal carcinoma 55 

(P19)  cell s,  20  neurons, astrocytes and NIH3T3 fibrob last s (Figure 1 A). 21  The n eurons were 56 

generated from P19 cells  in vitr o.  22  Cell nuclei were stained with Hoechst, which is commonly 57 

used for live cells . 23  To  capture high -resolution images of the nucleus,  we used the 160 × objective 58 

lens ( Figure  S1 ) and cropped interphase nuclei ( Figure  1B).  The nuclear area of neurons  was 59 

significantly smaller than those of other cell types ( Figure  1C). We applied  a random forest (RF) 60 

algorith m,  24,25  which has been effectively used as a classifier. We trained an RF based on twelve 61 

features of the nucleus, including nuclear area and circularity etc., with stratified five -fold cross -62 

validation  to ensure stable performance , using nuclear images from the four cell types (P19 cells, 63 

n =  1001; neurons,  n =  1001; astrocytes,  n =  947; NIH3T3 fibroblasts,  n =  1007) ( Figure  S2 ). The 64 

nuclear area seems to be  the  most important for prediction. The evaluation using independent 65 

external test set ( n = 200 for each cell type) suggests that the model might be able to predict neurons 66 

with high accuracy but is not effective at classifying other cell types ( Figure  1D). 67 

Then we employed a CNN, which includes several convolution, max pooling and dropout 68 

layers ( Figure  S3A ).  Fully connected  (FC)  layers are applied  after flattening feature maps.  During 69 

training, the cropped images were  augmented by random rotation, resizing and brightness 70 

adjustment.  We trained eight separate CNN models on these images using stratified eight -fold 71 

cross -validation (P19 cells,  n =  15015; neurons,  n =  15015; astrocytes,  n =  14205; NIH3T3 72 

fibroblasts,  n =  15105). Across the eight models, key performance metrics for classifying each cell 73 

type, including precision, recall, F1 -score and the area under the curve (AUC) of the receiver 74

In this study, w e have demonstrated  that  high -resolution  two -dimensional single -plane images of 189 

Hoechst - or DAPI -stained nuclei  serve  as cell -type -specific identifiers for live cultured cells and 190 

fixed tissue cells. Our findings suggest that the deep -learning -decipherable distribution patterns of 191 

Hoechst and DAPI that bind to DNA are conserved among the same cell type and maintained 192 

throughout the nucleus. Despite huge varieties of subtypes of neurons including morphologies and 193 

functional properties, unified grouping of various subtypes of neurons by  the  UFN models might 194 

be linked with pan -neuronal features, possibly because neurons share expression of neuron -195 

specific genes for their functions. 196 

Different models were required for live cultured cells and fixed tissue cells. The nuclear 197 

organization of fixed cells may have been affected by sample preparation, in which a harsh antigen 198 

retrieval step is required to unmask epitopes for immunostainin g.  39 

199 

Defining the precise nuclear patterns that enable cell type classification remains a challenge. 200 

To identify cell -type -specific patterns, we applied integrated gradients (IG) , 40  which visualizes 201 

feature attributions by showing important features for each model prediction as a saliency map 202 

(Figure s S8 -S10 ), but no consistent and interpretable patterns were observed for  both live and fixed 203 

cells. This may suggest that our CNN models do not classify cell types simply relying on easily 204 

discernible features. The optic al resolution of our confocal microscope (~200 nm in a single plane) 205 

is insufficient to resolve  the  positions  of individual dye molecules bound to DNA , implying  that 206 

the CNN models  may  extract some larger features of  nuclear DNA staining . Future efforts focusing 207 

on the interpretability of the models will be essential for deciphering the fundamental principles 208 

of nuclear organization underlying cell types. 209

RF classification 351 

For building RF models, we quantified  twelve  nuclear morphological features using ImageJ: Area, 352 

Perimeter, Width, Height, Major, Minor, Circularity, Solidity, Aspect ratio, Feret, MinFeret and 353 

Feret  angle. We buil t RF models  using RandomForestClassifier  from  the Scikit -learn Python 354 

package  and  performed  stratified  k-fold cross -validation  47,48 . The  nuclear images  were resized to 355 

150 × 150 pixels  before feature extraction . The parameters of the RF model (max features, 356 

n_estimator and  k-fold) were tuned to improve the prediction performance. The optimal parameter 357 

values were determined to be max features = 0.8, n_estimator = 300 and  k = 5. 358

CNN model architecture and training 360 

We  constructed  CNN  model s using multiple  conv olution  layers , max  pooling layers  and  dropout 361 

layer s (Figure  S3A). After flattening, FC layers were employed. Each of convolution and FC layers, 362 

except for the output layer, was followed by the  rectified linear unit activation function. The output 363 

layer utilized the softmax activ ation function.  We adopted batch normalization twic e. Model 364 

parameters were optimized using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 1×10 -3 and decay of 365 

1×10 -3. Categorical cross -entropy was used as the loss function.  Models were trained for 100 366 

epochs. 367
