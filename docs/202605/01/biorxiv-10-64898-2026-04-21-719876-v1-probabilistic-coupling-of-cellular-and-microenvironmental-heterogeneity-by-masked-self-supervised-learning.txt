## ABSTRACT 

Spatial omics technologies have advanced to single-cell resolution, enabling systematic analysis of 1

tissue microenvironments alongside cellular-state heterogeneity. However, computationally defining 2

microenvironmental states at single-cell resolution and identifying representations most informative 3

for biological discovery remain major challenges. Here we present Mievformer, a Transformer-4

based masked self-supervised framework that learns microenvironmental embeddings by encod-5

ing neighboring cellular states and relative spatial configurations to parameterize the conditional 6

distribution of continuous cell states at central spatial positions. Through InfoNCE optimization, 7

Mievformer learns representations that capture the relative enrichment of cell states across microen-8

vironments, formalized as a conditional density ratio, thereby enabling probabilistic inference of the 9

coupling between microenvironmental and cellular heterogeneity. Mievformer outperformed exist-10 

ing methods in niche clustering on simulated spatial transcriptomics data and achieved the highest 11 

average performance across five real datasets spanning three spatial transcriptomics platforms when 12 

evaluated using DREC, a ground-truth-free metric that most strongly correlated with ground-truth 13 

performance in simulations. Beyond conventional clustering, Mievformer enables identification 14 

of cellular subpopulations based on their microenvironmental distribution and detection of gene-15 

expression signatures associated with colocalization of specific cell populations. Together, these 16 

results establish Mievformer as a quantitatively robust and biologically informative framework for 17 

learning microenvironment representations in spatial omics. 18 

## 1 Introduction 19 

Single-cell transcriptomics has revealed prevalent cellular heterogeneity across diverse biological systems, including 20 

disease states[1]. These data have enabled the reconstruction of developmental lineage hierarchies and their mas-21 

ter regulators, as well as the identification of disease-associated cell populations that clarify cell-level pathomecha-22 

nisms. However, the progression and diversification of multicellular systems, such as tumors, are driven not only 23 

by autonomous cell-state transitions but also from sequential, context-dependent cell-cell interactions that define the 24 

evolution of cellular communities[2, 3]. Thus, elucidating tissue development and disease through the framework 25

of cellular heterogeneity requires characterising the heterogeneity of microenvironments wherein multiple cell types 26 

interact. This perspective drives omics analyses of the cell states that produce microenvironmental heterogeneity and 27 

the identification of cell populations that are enriched in specific microenvironments. 28 

Spatial omics, conducted by measuring gene expression in its native spatial context, offers unprecedented power to dis-29 

sect microenvironmental heterogeneity[4], since it enables the identification of disease-specific microenvironments [5] 30 

and improves data-driven inference of cell-cell interactions by providing spatial proximity constraints [6]. However, 31 

the notion of "microenvironmental state" remains conceptually ambiguous, hampering its computational definition and 32 

estimation. A prevalent strategy is to learn representations that directly reconstruct spot-level expression from spatial 33 

transcriptomics. For example, BayesSpace[7] assigns discrete microenvironmental states under Markov random-field 34 

constraints to enforce spatial coherence and optimise state-specific expression profiles and assignments via Markov 35 

Chain Monte Carlo. STAGATE[8] and GraphST[9] construct spatial proximity graphs and learn latent microenviron-36 

mental states using graph-based encoders. However, these approaches equate the microenvironmental state with the 37 

expression observed at each spatial location, which can be limiting for modern single-cell-resolution platforms where 38 

cells with distinctively different expression profiles such as tumor and immune cells can be adjacent to each other in a 39 

shared microenvironment. Recently, several methods have been proposed to better leverage the increasing resolution 40 

of spatial omics. NicheCompass[10] models microenvironments with a graph attention network[11] by jointly regress-41 

ing the central cell expression profile and the neighbourhood-averaged expression, while regularising the latent space 42 

so that microenvironmental similarities align with the spatial proximity graph. Banksy[12] derives microenviron-43 

mental representations by augmenting neighbourhood-averaged expression with spatial expression gradients, thereby 44 

capturing features that mean profiles alone miss. CellCharter[13] constructs a spatial neighbor graph via Delaunay 45 

triangulation and aggregates neighborhood features across multiple graph layers, enabling the identification of spatial 46 

cell niches at multiple scales. Nevertheless, because these approaches rely on expression aggregated over user-defined 47 

neighbourhoods, their outputs are sensitive to the neighbourhood definition and, in practice, attenuate the single-cell 48 

resolution of the measurements. 49 

Here, we present Mievformer, a Transformer-based masked self-supervised framework that learns microenvironmental 50 

embeddings by encoding neighboring cellular states and relative spatial configurations. Rather than regressing cen-51 

tral expression profiles or neighborhood-aggregated features, Mievformer parameterizes the conditional distribution 52 

of continuous cell states at central spatial positions. Through InfoNCE [14] optimization, it learns representations 53 

that capture the relative enrichment of cell states across microenvironments, formalized as a conditional density ra-54 

tio, thereby enabling probabilistic inference of the coupling between microenvironmental and cellular heterogeneity. 55 

This framework supports analyses beyond conventional clustering, including single-cell-resolution characterization 56 

of microenvironmental preferences and identification of gene-expression signatures associated with colocalization of 57 

specific cell populations. Together, these capabilities establish Mievformer as a quantitatively robust and biologically 58 

informative framework for spatial omics analysis. 59 

## 2 Results 60 

2.1 Overview of Mievformer 61 

Mievformer learns microenvironmental representations by encoding the cellular states and spatial configurations of 62 

neighboring cells using a Transformer-based architecture with masked self-supervised learning. Specifically, it masks 63 

the cell state at a central cell position and employs InfoNCE loss to maximize the mutual information between the 64 

inferred microenvironmental embedding and the observed central cell state. This contrastive learning objective yields 65 

embeddings that parameterize the conditional density ratio of cell states in local tissue contexts (see the Methods 66 

section for details). 67 

Formally, given spatial transcriptomics data comprising gene expression y, low-dimensional expression embeddings 

z obtained via Principal component analysis (PCA), and spatial coordinates x, Mievformer operates as follows: For each cell i with spatial neighborhood N (i), the microenvironmental embedding is computed as: 

ei = Transformer ([MASK] + PE (0) , {h(zj ) + PE (xj − xi) | j ∈ N (i)}),

where h is a learned projection of cell embeddings and PE (·) denotes the sinusoidal positional encoding of relative 68 

spatial coordinates. The model defines a score function s(ei, z j ) and computes the probability Pij that the cell state 69 

zj would be observed at the masked position via softmax normalization across all cells in the mini-batch. Training 70 

maximizes Pii , the likelihood of the actual observed cell state zi, which is equivalent to minimizing the InfoNCE 71 

loss. This optimization learns embeddings such that the score function approximates the conditional density ratio 72 

p(z|e)/p (z). Once trained, Mievformer can compute microenvironmental embeddings for any spatial position and 73 

derive the stochastic association between cell states and microenvironments from the learned density ratio. 74

Beyond conventional clustering-based characterization of microenvironmental heterogeneity, the density ratio learned 75 

by Mievformer enables several downstream analyses through probabilistic inference. First, by applying density-ratio-76 

based Bayesian inversion to the learned ratio p(z|e)/p (z), p(e|z)/p (e) is obtained, which represents the posterior 77 

density ratio of microenvironments given a cell state. Aggregating these ratios over microenvironmental clusters yields 78 

single-cell-resolution microenvironmental membership profiles, which can be used to identify cell subpopulations with 79 

specific microenvironmental preferences. Second, calculating the expectation of the density ratio p(z|e)/p (z) across 80 

all cell states belonging to a specific cell population by Monte-Carlo approximation, the density of that population in 81 

microenvironment e is obtained. Correlation analysis with this density reveals the genes up- or down-regulated given 82 

the colocalization with the target cell popution. Together, these capabilities establish Mievformer as a quantitative 83 

framework for clarifying the joint structure of cellular and microenvironmental heterogeneity in spatial omics data 84

2.2 Performance superiority in deriving representations of microenvironment in simulated data 86 

To quantitatively assess the performance of Mievformer, we applied it to simulation data configured with multiple 87 

patterns, each comprising three ground-truth microenvironmental regions (inner, boundary, and outer), and compared 88 

its performance against existing methods (Figure 2). The simulation consisted of multiple concentric annular patterns, 89 

each composed of these three region types and assigned a distinct, pattern-specific cell-cluster composition. These 90 

repetitive lumen-like patterns were designed to mimic tissue architectures observed in biological structures such as 91 

thyroid follicles, renal tubules, and seminiferous tubules. For each spatial spot, a cluster label was first determined 92 

according to the composition of its assigned region (inner, boundary, or outer), and then an expression profile was 93 

randomly sampled from cells belonging to that cluster. 94

2.3 Benchmarking microenvironmental representations on real datasets 107 

Next, we compared the performance of microenvironmental representation methods on five real spatial transcriptomics 108 

datasets: Xenium datasets from pancreatic, lung, and glioblastoma cancers, a Stereo-seq mouse brain dataset[15], 109 

and a Visium HD mouse brain dataset. We applied k-means clustering to the microenvironmental representations 110 

obtained by each method and qualitatively compared their spatial distributions. Relative to clusters derived from 111 

PCA coordinates, all methods exhibited more spatially coherent patterns; however, qualitative assessment alone was 112 

insufficient to determine superiority among methods (Figure 3a). 113 

Although multiple quantitative metrics have been proposed and employed in prior studies of microenvironmental rep-114 

resentation, their validity has not been rigorously established. To address this, we evaluated the correlation between 115

several metrics and ground-truth performance (Niche ARI) in simulation data. Specifically, we examined one existing 116 

metric (CAS), three novel metrics we propose (SCAS, NEA, and DREC), and four metrics from the NicheCompass 117 

benchmarking module[10] (NCB-GCS, NCB-CAS, NCB-NASW, NCB-MLAMI). Our implemented metrics are sum-118 

marized as follows: 119 

CAS (Cell Type Affinity Similarity): Measures the correlation between cell-type co-localization patterns in mi-120 

croenvironmental embedding neighborhoods and in spatial neighborhoods, thereby quantifying the preservation of 121 

spatial co-localization. However, this metric can assign high scores to representations that closely approximate spatial 122 

coordinates themselves. 123 

SCAS (Shuffled Cell Type Affinity Similarity): After clustering microenvironmental states, spatial coordinates are 124 

randomly permuted within each cluster, and the correlation of cell-type co-localization patterns with the original 125 

coordinates is computed. This metric tests whether co-localization patterns are retained under shuffling, mitigating the 126 

bias favoring spatial-coordinate-based embeddings. 127 

NEA (Neighbourhood Enrichment Accuracy): Evaluates consistency between cell-type composition ratios in mi-128 

croenvironmental clusters and spatial co-localization. Defined as the Pearson correlation between an enrichment 129 

matrix Esp computed from spatial nearest neighbours (2-nearest neighbours) and an expected enrichment matrix 130 

Eniche = P · P T derived from the cell-type composition matrix P within microenvironmental clusters. 131 

DREC (Dual Recovery): Quantifies the proportion of expression-similar cells whose nearby spatial neighbors belong 132 

to the same microenvironmental cluster as the original cell. Specifically, for each cell, we select the k-th nearest cell in 133 

expression space (based on the top 20 principal components, with k = 10 ) and evaluate whether the k-nearest spatial 134 

neighbor ( k = 10 ) of that selected cell belongs to the same microenvironmental cluster as the original cell. 135 

The comparison of these metrics with ground-truth Niche ARI in simulation data revealed that DREC exhibited the 136 

strongest correlation across varying simulation parameters, including duct thickness and number of duct categories 137 

(Figure 3b). Conversely, CAS showed the second-lowest correlation. Notably, the baseline representation using 138 

spatial coordinates alone achieved the highest CAS value, confirming our concern that CAS favors proximity-based 139 

features. Although SCAS exhibited moderate correlation, performance of multiple methods converged near ceiling 140 

values, precluding Mievformer’s superiority observed in ARI and qualitative analyses. Both SCAS and NEA, similar 141 

to CAS, evaluate coarse-grained inter-cell-type co-localization rather than single-cell-resolution microenvironmental 142 

consistency, which likely explains DREC’s superior discriminative power. The four NCB metrics generally exhibited 143 

weak or negative correlations with Niche ARI, with NCB-MLAMI showing a strongly negative correlation ( −0.34 144 

to −0.61 ). Based on these findings, we adopted DREC as the primary performance metric for subsequent real-data 145 

analyses. 146 

Finally, we benchmarked all methods on five real datasets using DREC (Figure 3c). Mievformer achieved the highest 147 

DREC score in four of the five datasets, with only the Xenium GBM dataset being an exception. In the Visium 148 

HD Mouse Brain dataset, Mievformer tied with GraphST for the top position. Across all five datasets, Mievformer 149 

attained the highest mean DREC score. Notably, the PCA baseline, which is simple expression-based clustering, 150 

also exhibited high DREC scores, with only Mievformer, NicheCompass, Banksy, and CellCharter exceeding it. This 151 

likely reflects the biological tendency for expression-similar cells to co-localize spatially. Nevertheless, these results 152 

demonstrate that Mievformer captures microenvironmental structure more faithfully than existing methods, including 153 

expression-based baselines, suggesting its representations better reflect latent microenvironmental organization. 154 

2.4 Elucidating microenvironmental diversity in lung cancer 155 

To qualitatively assess whether Mievformer’s microenvironmental embeddings capture biologically meaningful mi-156 

croenvironmental diversity, we performed detailed interpretation of Mievformer outputs on lung cancer Xenium data 157 

(Figure 4). Clustering of the microenvironmental embeddings identified twelve clusters with specific cell-type com-158 

positions (Figure 4a,b). Five clusters (Niche 4, 5, 6, 7, and 10) were dominated by tumor cells, whereas Niche 1 159 

predominantly was occupied by monocytes/macrophages, Niche 0 was T cell-enriched, and Niches 2, 3, and 11 were 160 

fibroblast-dominant niches. Notably, even among the tumor-dominant clusters, Mievformer resolved fine-grained mi-161 

croenvironmental differences: Niche 4 comprised >80% tumor cells, Niche 5 contained 20% fibroblasts alongside 162 

tumor cells, and Niche 7 resembled Niche 4 but included 10% endothelial cells. 163 

Spatial analysis revealed that tumor-dominant niche clusters (4, 5, 6, 7, and 10) were localized to the tumor core, 164 

fibroblast-enriched clusters (2 and 3) occupied stromal regions, and immune-enriched clusters were concentrated at 165 

tumor-stroma boundaries (Figure 4d). Interestingly, macrophage-dominated Niche 1 was distributed throughout the 166 

tumor interior. Direct comparison of the spatial patterns of Niches 4 (tumor-enriched) and 1 (macrophage-enriched) 167 

confirmed that Mievformer successfully distinguished these spatially intermingled microenvironments (Figure 4c). 168

2.5 Identification of cell subpopulations based on microenvironmental distribution 171 

Mievformer enables the characterization of cell-type subpopulations based on their microenvironmental distributions 172 

by computing membership probabilities to microenvironmental clusters for each individual cell. To quantitatively 173 

validate the membership probabilities assigned by Mievformer, we aggregated the estimated probabilities for each 174 

combination of microenvironmental clusters and cell types (Figure 5b). For all the cell types, the membership proba-175 

bility to the corresponding niche cluster was consistently the highest among microenvironmental clusters containing 176 

≥5% of cells from the given cell type (Figure 5b). While fibroblasts exhibited highly specific cluster assignments, we 177 

observed the some combinations of microenvironmental clusters and cell types exhibited relatively low specificity to 178 

corresponding microenvironmental clusters. For instance, tumor cells in Niche 5 showed substantial assignment not 179 

only to Niche 5 but also to Niche 7, and macrophages in Niche 10 similarly exhibited increased assignment to Niche 180 

7. 181 

To investigate whether these differences in mean assignment probability reflected overall differences in cluster speci-182 

ficity or intra-cluster variation, we examined the distribution of membership probabilities within each microenvi-183 

ronmental cluster for each cell type. Clusters with specific assignments displayed sharp peaks near probability 1.0, 184 

whereas clusters with non-specific assignments, although containing cells with high assignment probabilities to the 185 

corresponding cluster, exhibited broad distributions with substantial variance (Supplementary Figure 2). These results 186 

indicate that Mievformer’s membership probabilities to microenvironmental clusters correctly capture the relationship 187 

between expression variation within cell types and distribution to microenvironmental clusters, suggesting that for cer-188 

tain cell types and microenvironmental clusters, there exist cells specifically enriched in particular microenvironments 189 

and those that are not. To leverage this distribution across microenvironmental clusters as a novel cellular feature 190 

encoding niche specificity, we performed hierarchical clustering based on microenvironmental membership profiles of 191

2.6 Single-cell resolution analysis of microenvironmental colocalization 210 

Mievformer enables the estimation of the conditional probability that any cell state would be observed in each mi-211 

croenvironment. This capability allows quantitative assessment of whether cells from specific populations of interest 212 

could be distributed to particular spatial locations. By computing the probability of target cell population existence 213 

at each single-cell location, we can quantify the likelihood of colocalization with the target population, which is the 214 

probability of residing in the same microenvironment. Using these single-cell colocalization scores, we can identify 215 

genes whose expression is up-regulated given the colocalization with target populations. 216 

We applied this framework to Xenium data of pancreatic cancer, computing microenvironmental states and cell-type-217 

specific existence probabilities (Figure 6b–d). The estimated existence probability for each cell type was consistent 218 

with its observed spatial distribution. Quantitatively, for all cell types, the averages of the estimated density in mi-219 

croenvironments where that cell type was present exceeded twice the density averaged across all microenvironments 220 

(Figure 6e). These results demonstrate that Mievformer’s density estimates accurately reflect the spatial distribution of 221 

actual cell populations. Examining the histograms of cell-type densities (Figure 6f) revealed distinct colocalization be-222 

haviors: while microenvironments with estimated cancer cell density exceeding 0.99 represented a substantial fraction 223 

of cancer-cell-containing microenvironments, such highly exclusive microenvironments were nearly absent for pan-224 

creatic stellate cells. This indicates that while cancer cells frequently occupy isolated microenvironments, pancreatic 225 

stellate cells almost invariably colocalize with other cell types at close range. 226 

Finally, we used estimated cell densities to identify colocalization signatures with specific cell populations. For en-227 

dothelial cells, we computed correlations between tumor cell density and gene expression (Figure 6g). Genes ex-228 

hibiting the top10 positive correlations included CXCR4, which is specifically expressed in tip cells extending into 229 

tumor regions during angiogenesis[16], and APOLD1, which contributes to the angiogenic process[17]. These re-230 

sults suggest the successful capture of the expression signature of tip cells expected in peritumoral angiogenesis. For 231 

the analysis of colocalization between tumor cells and pancreatic stellate cells, we analyzed correlations between 232 

stellate cell density and gene expression in tumor cells (Figure 6h). Among genes exhibiting the top10 positive cor-233 

relations, MYC has been reported to be associated with EMT in pancreatic cancer[18]. Furthermore, we validated 234 

this finding by quantifying c-Myc expression in fibroblast-proximal and tumor-core regions of PDAC sections using 235 

immunohistochemistry (Figure 6i,j). Nuclear-level quantification revealed significantly higher c-Myc expression in 236 

the fibroblast-proximal region compared with the tumor core (median DAB-positive fraction 0.45 vs. 0.11; Mann– 237 

Whitney U test, p = 8 .14 × 10 −10 ), consistent with a role for fibroblast-derived signals in modulating tumor cell 238 

proliferative capacity[19]. Together, these results demonstrate that Mievformer’s density-based framework enables 239 

the systematic characterization of how colocalization with specific cell populations influences expression profiles. 240 

## 3 Discussion 241 

In this study, we present Mievformer, a novel framework for estimating microenvironmental states as parameterizations 242 

of the conditional distribution of continuous cell states, and demonstrate its utility through simulation and real-data 243 

analyses. First, we quantitatively evaluated Mievformer’s performance relative to existing methods using simulated 244 

and real datasets. In simulations with known ground truth, Mievformer consistently achieved superior performance 245 

across a wide range of parameter settings. When applied to five real spatial transcriptomics datasets across Xenium, 246 

Stereo-seq, and Visium HD platforms, Mievformer achieved the highest average performance on our proposed eval-247 

uation metric, ranking first in four of five datasets. Beyond performance benchmarking, we demonstrated that the 248 

probabilistic coupling between cell and microenvironmental states learned by Mievformer enables biologically mean-249 

ingful discovery and hypothesis generation. We established two downstream analytical frameworks: (1) cell clustering 250 

based on microenvironmental distribution profiles, and (2) identification of colocalization signatures through corre-251 

lation with cell-population density estimates. Both approaches successfully recapitulated established knowledge of 252 

tumor microenvironments, validating their effectiveness for dissecting microenvironmental cell communities from 253 

single-cell spatial transcriptome data. 254 

Since the emergence of spatial transcriptomics, numerous methodologies have characterized microenvironmental 255 

states from spatial omics data. Early spatial transcriptomics platforms lacked single-cell resolution, prompting meth-256 

ods that primarily sought faithful representations of spot-level expression profiles while incorporating spatial con-257 

straints to enforce consistency across neighboring spots[20]. However, this paradigm becomes problematic at single-258 

cell resolution, where microenvironments often comprise local mixtures of transcriptionally distinct cell types, re-259 

quiring representations that capture context beyond individual expression profiles. To address these limitations, re-260 

cent approaches have aimed to derive low-dimensional representations from spatially aggregated features, such as 261 

neighborhood-averaged expression[10, 12]. While this strategy enables spatially coherent microenvironmental esti-262 

mation even at single-cell resolution, it effectively reduces the resolution of the data to a user-defined neighborhood 263

Self-supervised learning approaches for microenvironmental state estimation have been previously proposed [10]; 271 

however, these methods fundamentally differ from Mievformer in their learning objectives and resulting representa-272 

tions. Some approaches reduce to regression of central cell expression profiles[21], while others frame the problem 273 

as probabilistic deconvolution of cell type proportions at each spatial location[22]. In contrast, Mievformer parame-274 

terizes the conditional probability distribution of continuous cell states at the central position, enabling probabilistic 275 

inference of the coupling between microenvironmental and cellular heterogeneity at single cell and single spot res-276 

olution. This probabilistic framework yields bidirectional inference capabilities: given a microenvironmental state, 277 

Mievformer estimates the distribution of cell states at the corresponding microenvironment; conversely, given a cell 278 

state, it computes the posterior distribution over microenvironments. These capabilities enable downstream analyses 279 

that are not readily accessible with existing methods, including estimating cell-population densities in arbitrary mi-280 

croenvironments and single-cell-resolution clustering based on microenvironmental membership profiles. Herein, we 281 

demonstrated the biological utility of these analyses through multiple case studies. In lung cancer data, clustering anal-282 

ysis based on niche distribution revealed increased PPR1B expression in tumor cells residing in fibroblast-enriched 283 

microenvironments. In pancreatic cancer data, correlation analysis between endothelial cell expression and tumor 284 

cell density identified CXCR4 upregulation in endothelial cells colocalizing with tumors, recapitulating the known 285 

expression signature of angiogenic tip cells. Together, these results establish that Mievformer’s probabilistic coupling 286 

of cell states and microenvironments provides a distinct utility for dissecting cellular heterogeneity which correlated 287 

with microenvironmental context. 288 

Although the estimation of microenvironmental states offers significant biological utility, quantitative performance 289 

evaluation remains challenging, similar to unsupervised representation learning in other domains. NicheCompass 290 

addressed this challenge by aggregating multiple evaluation metrics to ensure robust performance assessment[10]. 291 

However, the validation of individual metrics primarily relied on their theoretical foundations rather than empirical 292 

verification. In this study, we explicitly assessed the validity of the metrics using simulation data with known ground 293 

truth. We found that some of the metrics assigned higher scores to naive baselines such as spatial coordinates or 294 

raw expression profiles instead of the methods specialized for microenvironmental representation learning, including 295 

Mievformer. Notably, these same metrics exhibited weak correlations with ground-truth performance measures. In 296 

contrast, this study introduced DREC, which demonstrated consistently strong correlation with ground-truth metrics 297 

across diverse simulation conditions. We attribute DREC’s superior discriminative power to its ability to evaluate 298 

microenvironmental consistency at single-cell resolution by directly operating on cell embeddings, thereby avoiding 299 

the information loss inherent in cluster-aggregated evaluations of cell-type composition. This finer-grained approach 300 

enables DREC to more sensitively capture microenvironmental structure than metrics that assess only coarse cell-type 301 

colocalization patterns. 302 

Although Mievformer demonstrates superior performance in single-cell-resolution microenvironmental state estima-303 

tion, certain limitations must be acknowledged. First, the current framework is optimized for characterizing spatially 304 

contiguous microenvironments and may not fully capture higher-order community structures, such as hierarchically 305 

nested or spatially interleaved microenvironmental patterns. For instance, CellCharter[13] captures multi-scale neigh-306 

borhood structure through hierarchical graph-layer aggregation. Extending Mievformer to model such hierarchical or-307 

ganization would require architectural modifications to explicitly represent multi-scale spatial dependencies. Another 308 

important consideration is the extension to multi-sample spatial transcriptomics analysis. Mievformer is primarily 309 

designed to characterize microenvironmental heterogeneity within individual tissue sections, but recent studies in-310 

creasingly require integrative analysis across multiple samples, patients, or experimental conditions. In such settings, 311 

batch effects arising from technical variability between samples must be appropriately accounted for. NicheCompass 312 

incorporates a batch correction mechanism that enables joint embedding of cells from multiple samples into a shared 313 

latent space[10], and Nicheformer leverages a foundation model approach pretrained on large-scale single-cell and 314 

spatial atlases to achieve cross-dataset generalization[23]. Integrating similar multi-sample harmonization strategies 315 

into Mievformer would broaden its applicability to cohort-level studies and facilitate the identification of microenvi-316 

ronmental changes associated with disease progression or treatment response. Addressing these limitations represents 317 

promising directions for improving spatial omics analysis methods. 318

4.1 In situ gene expression profiling 320 

In situ RNA expression analysis was performed using Xenium (10x Genomics, CG000601). FFPE sections (10 μm321 

thick) were prepared on Xenium slides. Fixation and tissue permeabilization were performed according to the protocol 322 

provided by 10x Genomics. The probe hybridization mixture was prepared using a Xenium Human Multi-Tissue and 323 

Cancer Panel. The prepared probes were hybridized, and post-hybridization washing, ligation, and amplification were 324 

performed according to the user guide. Autofluorescence quenching and nuclear staining were conducted in the dark. 325 

Using the prepared slides, fluorescent probe hybridization and imaging were conducted via a Xenium Analyzer (10x 326 

Genomics). After the Xenium run, H&E staining was performed on the Xenium slide. 327 

4.2 Data preprocessing 328 

In this study, we analyzed spatial transcriptome data generated by three platforms: Xenium (10x Genomics), Stereo-329 

seq (BGI Genomics), and Visium HD (10x Genomics). Each dataset underwent the following preprocessing pipeline 330 

using rapids-singlcell [24], a GPU-accelerated implementation of scanpy[25]. First, quality control (QC) metrics were 331 

calculated for each cell, and cells were filtered based on the number of detected genes. Specifically, cells with fewer 332 

than 20 detected genes were excluded. Next, gene filtering was performed to remove genes detected in fewer than 333 

0.5% of cells to eliminate uninformative genes. Expression values were then normalized using total-count normal-334 

ization, where each cell’s counts were scaled so that the total count per cell equals the median total count across all 335 

cells, followed by natural logarithm transformation via log( x + 1) (log1p ). PCA was applied to the preprocessed 336 

expression matrix, retaining the first 30 principal components. A k-nearest neighbor graph was constructed in the 337 

PCA space, followed by UMAP[26] dimensionality reduction for visualization and Leiden clustering[27] for cell-338 

type annotation. These clustering results were used to define cell populations for downstream microenvironmental 339 

analyses. The annotation was performed based on known marker genes for each cell type, which were derived from 340 

PanglaoDB[28]. Spatial coordinates were normalized by the mean distance to k-nearest neighbors (default k = 100 )341 

used in Mievformer, ensuring that relative positional coordinates were within an appropriate range for model input. 342 

4.3 Datasets 343 

We used five spatial transcriptomics datasets in this study. The Xenium Lung and Xenium Pancreas datasets 344 

were generated in this study using the Xenium In Situ platform (see “In situ gene expression profiling” for ex-345 

perimental details). These data will be deposited in the Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) database upon ac-346 

ceptance. The Xenium GBM (glioblastoma) data were obtained from the 10x Genomics public dataset reposi-347 

tory (https://www.10xgenomics.com/datasets). The Stereo-seq Mouse Brain data were obtained from the MOSTA 348

were obtained from the 10x Genomics public dataset repository (https://www.10xgenomics.com/datasets). 350

4.4.2 Mievformer algorithm 360 

Mievformer employs a masked self-supervised learning framework to compute the probability of observing a central 361 

cell state given its surrounding cellular context, and maximizes this probability during training. The architecture 362 

comprises NicheEncoder and Distributor as main components. 363 

NicheEncoder constructs the input sequence by placing a learnable mask token at the central cell position: [mask +364 

δi,i , {h(zj ) + δij | j ∈ N (i)}]. Each neighboring cell state zj is linearly projected to dmodel dimensions (default: 64) 365 

via transformation g and combined with its positional encoding δij .366 

This sequence is processed through multiple Transformer encoder layers (default: 3), implemented using 367 

PyTorch’s[31] nn.TransformerEncoderLayer , which integrates multi-head self-attention and feedforward net-368 

works. The final hidden state of the mask token is transformed into a microenvironmental state ei using a feedforward 369 

neural network. 370 

Distributor computes the probability that each cell embedding zj (j = 1 , . . . , M ) in the mini-batch could be observed at the central position given microenvironment ei. The model defines a score function: 

sθ (ei, z j ) = we(ei)⊤wz (zj ) + bz (zj ),

where we and wz are neural networks mapping microenvironmental and cell-state embeddings to a shared hidden dimension h, and bz provides a cell-state-dependent bias. We did not include bias term for a microenvironmental state ei, since the calculated scores are normalized for each ei. The conditional probability is obtained via softmax normalization: 

Pij = exp (sθ (ei, z j ))∑Mj′=1 exp (sθ (ei, z j′ )) .

Model parameters are optimized using the AdamW optimizer[32] with a learning rate of 1 × 10 −3, implemented via 375

coherence. Mini-batch size is set to 512 cells by default. The model checkpoint with the lowest validation loss is 381 

retained for downstream analysis. All neural networks were implemented in PyTorch[31]. 382

4.5 Evaluation metrics 410 

For quantitative evaluation of microenvironmental representations, the following metrics were employed: 411 

Niche Adjusted Rand Index (Niche ARI): Evaluates the concordance between ground-truth clusters and estimated 412 

microenvironmental clusters in simulation data. ARI ranges from 0 to 1, with higher values indicating greater agree-413 

ment. For clustering microenvironmental representations, k-means was applied with the number of clusters set to 414 

match the true number of microenvironmental clusters configured in the simulation. 415 

Cell Type Affinity Similarity (CAS): Assesses the concordance between cell-type colocalization patterns in mi-416 

croenvironmental embedding neighborhoods and those in spatial neighborhoods. Specifically, k-nearest neigh-417 

bor graphs (default k = 5) were constructed for both microenvironmental embeddings and spatial coordi-418 

nates using rapids_singlecell[24], and neighborhood enrichment matrices were computed using squidpy’s[34] 419 

nhood_enrichment function. The resulting matrices were flattened, and Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient 420

4.7 Simulation data generation with multiple microenvironmental patterns 472 

To enable quantitative evaluation and benchmarking of Mievformer against competing methods, we generated syn-473 

thetic spatial transcriptomics data with known ground-truth microenvironmental structure. The simulation framework 474 

supports multiple distinct microenvironmental patterns while preserving realistic gene expression characteristics by 475 

directly sampling expression profiles from observed cells in pancreatic cancer Xenium data. 476 

4.7.1 Spatial structure and microenvironmental pattern definition 477 

The simulation domain was defined as a square region of dimensions 1000 × 1000 arbitrary units. We configured D478 

distinct microenvironmental patterns ( D = duct_category_num ), each representing a spatially contiguous region 479 

with defined cellular composition. 480 

For each pattern k ∈ { 0, . . . , D − 1}, we generated nduct annular structures (ducts) as follows: 481 

• Duct centers were sampled uniformly within the simulation domain, and each was randomly assigned a 482 

pattern label k uniformly. 483 

• To introduce spatial heterogeneity, the radius r and boundary thickness t of each duct were perturbed from 484 

baseline values ( radius_base and thick_base , respectively) according to: 485 

r = radius_base × (1 + 2 a(U − 0.5) ),t = thick_base × (1 + 2 a(U − 0.5) ),

where U ∼ Uniform [0 , 1] and a = 0 .2 controls the magnitude of variation. 486 

• The inner and outer radii were computed as rin = max(0 , r − t) and rout = r + t, defining three concentric 487 

microenvironmental zones per duct: inner, boundary, and outer. 488 

Each spatial location was assigned one of 2D + 1 ground-truth microenvironmental labels: outside (background), 489 

inner_ k, or boundary_ k for k = 0 , . . . , D − 1.490 

4.7.2 Cell placement and microenvironmental assignment 491 

A total of ncell cells were uniformly distributed across the simulation domain. Each cell’s microenvironmental label 492 

was determined via nearest-neighbor search as follows: 493 

• For each pattern k, we constructed a k-nearest neighbors index ( k = 10 ) using only duct centers of that 494 

pattern and computed the distance from each cell to its nearest duct of pattern k.495 

• If the minimum distance was less than rout , the cell was provisionally labeled as boundary_ k; if less than rin ,496 

it was relabeled as inner_ k.497 

• Labels were assigned in hierarchical order (boundary → inner) across all patterns, with later patterns poten-498 

tially overwriting earlier assignments. 499 

• Cells whose distances to all ducts exceeded rout retained the default label Outside. 500

4.7.3 Cell-type composition and expression profile sampling 501 

For each microenvironmental label (outside, inner_ k, and boundary_ k), we defined distinct cell-type compositions 502 

and sampled corresponding expression profiles from the reference dataset. 503 

• We first computed the empirical cell-type frequency vector π from the reference Xenium data using a prede-504 

fined cell-type annotation key ( cluster_key ). 505 

• For each microenvironment n, a cell-type composition vector p(n) was sampled from a Dirichlet distribution 506 

with concentration parameter α = alpha_base × π, where alpha_base controls the degree of variation 507 

around the reference composition. 508 

• For each cell assigned to microenvironment n, a cell type was drawn according to the multinomial distribution 509 

Multinomial (1 , p (n)).510 

• The gene expression profile for that cell was obtained by randomly selecting one observed cell of the assigned 511 

type from the reference dataset and copying its preprocessed expression vector. 512 

This sampling strategy ensures that simulated expression profiles reflect the biological variability present in real data 513 

while maintaining controlled microenvironmental structure. 514

4.8 Immunohistochemistry 521 

Paraffin-embedded tissue sections (4 μm) were deparaffinized, rehydrated, and subjected to antigen retrieval using 522 

Target Retrieval Solution, Citrate pH 6 (Dako). Endogenous peroxidase activity was blocked with 3% hydrogen 523 

peroxide in methanol for 5 min, followed by blocking with 3% BSA in PBS for 30 min. Sections were incubated 524 

overnight at 4 °C with an anti–c-Myc antibody (Y59, Abcam), then with EnVision+ Dual Link System-HRP (Dako) for 525 

30 min at room temperature. Signals were visualized using the Liquid DAB+ Substrate Chromogen System (Dako), 526 

and sections were counterstained with Mayer’s hematoxylin, dehydrated, cleared, and mounted. Hematoxylin and 527 

eosin staining was performed for background evaluation. 528 

Quantitative analysis of c-Myc immunohistochemistry was performed to compare expression levels between 529 

fibroblast-proximal and tumor-core regions. Color deconvolution was applied to separate hematoxylin and DAB chan-530 

nels using the HED color space. Nuclear segmentation was performed on the hematoxylin channel using Otsu’s 531 

thresholding (multiplier = 0.6) followed by the watershed algorithm. The DAB positivity threshold was determined by 532 

fitting a two-component Gaussian Mixture Model to pixel values from all nuclear regions, with the threshold set at the 533 

midpoint between component means. For each nucleus, the DAB-positive pixel fraction was calculated as the propor-534 

tion of pixels exceeding this threshold. Two regions of interest ( 130 × 130 pixels) were defined in fibroblast-proximal 535 

and tumor-core regions. Statistical comparison was performed using the Mann–Whitney U test. 536
