Abstract 39 

The human liver depends on multiscale structural organization from vascula-40 

ture to cells to organelles to perform its diverse metabolic functions. A unified 41 

three-dimensional view linking these hierarchical scales in intact human tissue 42 

would be useful for better understanding these levels and how they relate to 43 

each other. We present a high-resolution volume electron microscopy reconstruc-44 

tion of human periportal liver tissue (152 × 140 × 33 μm3 at 8 nm pixel 45 

size) acquired by serial block-face scanning electron microscopy. Using a mul-46 

tiscale deep learning approach, we performed automated segmentation across 47 

the entire volume, enabling comprehensive annotation of vasculature, cells, and 48 

organelles. Quantitative analysis of bile duct architecture revealed coordinated 49 

scaling between lumen geometry and cholangiocyte number and size. Sinusoidal 50 

capillary branches exhibited distinct structural profiles with differential endothe-51 

lial coverage. Analysis of 35,790 complete mitochondria identified substantial 52 

morphological heterogeneity, with elongated mitochondria displaying preferen-53 

tial endoplasmic reticulum (ER) contacts at narrowing sites, a pattern consistent 54 

with ER-mediated fission or fusion. This multiscale reconstruction establishes an 55 

ultrastructural reference for the healthy human liver and provides a quantitative 56 

framework for investigating hepatic physiology and disease. 57

# 1 Introduction 61 

The human liver performs essential metabolic functions including detoxification, nutri-62 

ent processing, and bile production [1]. These diverse processes depend on intricate 63 

multiscale organization, where structural hierarchies—from vasculature to cells to 64 

organelles—coordinate to maintain tissue homeostasis [2–4]. At the vascular and cellu-65 

lar scale, bile ducts and sinusoidal capillaries establish the architectural framework for 66 

hepatic function [2, 5]. Bile ducts, lined by cholangiocytes, collect and transport bile 67 

produced by hepatocytes, while sinusoidal capillaries facilitate bidirectional exchange 68

scale, organelles exhibit morphological and spatial organization linked to functional 70 

specialization. For example, mitochondria–endoplasmic reticulum (ER) contacts regu-71

central to liver physiology and implicated in metabolic disease [4, 6–8]. 73 

Despite the importance of these multiscale relationships, a unified three-74 

dimensional and dynamic view of human liver tissue ultrastructure is not available. 75 

Previous studies of liver vasculature using light microscopy, optical clearing, or compu-76 

tational modeling have characterized associations between vascular architecture and 77 

the surrounding cellular environment [2, 9, 10], but lack the resolution to visualize 78 

vascular lumens, individual cells, and their organelles simultaneously. At the subcel-79 

lular level, studies in mouse liver and cultured cells have established ER involvement 80 

in mitochondrial morphology and dynamics [4, 6, 11], yet quantitative analysis of 81 

mitochondria–ER contacts in intact human liver tissue has not been performed. 82 

Here we present a high-resolution three-dimensional reconstruction of human 83 

periportal liver tissue using serial block-face scanning electron microscopy. The recon-84 

structed volume (152 × 140 × 33 μm3 at 8 nm pixel size) encompasses hierarchical 85 

structures spanning tissue, vasculature, cells, and organelles. We developed a deep 86 

learning framework for automated multiscale segmentation, enabling comprehensive 87 

annotation across the entire volume. Quantitative analysis revealed structural hetero-88 

geneity in bile duct lumen–cholangiocyte contacts and sinusoidal capillary branching 89 

organization. Analysis of 35,790 mitochondria identified substantial morphological het-90 

erogeneity, with elongated mitochondria displaying preferential endoplasmic reticulum 91 

contacts at narrowing sites—a pattern consistent with dynamics of ER-mediated fis-92 

sion or fusion. Together, this work establishes a multiscale ultrastructural reference 93 

for the human liver periportal region, providing both a morphological foundation and 94 

an analytical framework for investigating hepatic physiology and disease. 95 

# 2 Results 96 

## 2.1 3D reconstruction of human liver tissue 97 

The human liver displays intricate spatial organization, particularly within the peri-98 

portal region (Fig. 1a), which harbors portal tracts and diverse cell types fundamental 99 

to hepatic function and disease susceptibility [12]. To investigate it at nanoscale res-100 

olution, we performed volume electron microscopy (vEM) on a healthy human liver 101 

tissue sample encompassing the periportal region. To identify the appropriate region 102 

of interest (ROI), we first performed X-ray micro-computed tomography on a resin-103 

embedded tissue block (Fig. 1b). The 3D X-ray image provided orthogonal views of 104 

the block (xy, xz, and yz) (Supplementary Fig. 1a-c), enabling assessment of overall 105 

tissue integrity, staining uniformity, and localization of the periportal area. To further 106 

identify the periportal zone, immunohistochemistry was performed using CYP2A6, a 107 

periportal marker enzyme, which revealed the expected periportal-to-pericentral gra-108 

dient (Fig. 1c). The periportal ROI was then manually defined based on anatomical 109 

landmarks, including the portal vein, bile duct, and hepatic artery. Following ROI 110 

identification, the tissue block was trimmed to expose the selected periportal region 111

## 2.2 Automated segmentation enables construction of a labeled 132 

## human liver volume map 133 

To interpret the biological architecture of the reconstructed volume, we segmented the 134 

vasculature, cellular, and organelle structures across multiple spatial scales. Because 135 

these structures differ markedly in size, morphology, and density, we employed separate 136 

deep learning models optimized for each scale. For vascular and cellular structures, 137 

whose spatial coordinates were annotated in CATMAID, we used the Segment Any-138 

thing Model 2 (SAM2) [15], a foundation model for prompt-based visual segmentation 139 

in images and videos (Fig. 2a). The coordinates from the annotated structure mark-140 

ers of each cellular structure (defined based on the middle of each structure instance) 141 

were used as point prompts for SAM2’s image mode, which generated pixel-wise labels 142 

corresponding to each structure, which were manually proofread. Using SAM2’s video 143 

mode, which uses a memory-attention mechanism that propagates object identity 144 

across frames, the initial label for each instance was extended through the volume 145 

toward both the beginning and end slices. This pipeline was applied to all annotated 146 

vascular and cell structures. Segmentation performance was evaluated against manual 147 

annotations using standard metrics including F1 score, precision, recall, intersection-148 

over-union (IoU), and mean false distance [4, 8] (Table 2). The labels were then 149 

proofread to correct prediction error. The reconstructed labels were rendered in 3D 150 

to visualize the distribution of vascular and cellular structures within the periportal 151 

region (Fig. 2b, Supplementary Video 2). 152 

Unlike vascular and cellular structures that could be annotated with sparse spatial 153 

coordinates, subcellular organelles exhibited more regular morphologies but occurred 154

## 2.3 Cellular-scale analysis of the human liver map reveals 176 

## lumen–cell relationships in bile ducts and sinusoidal 177 

## capillaries 178 

Bile ducts within the periportal zone transport bile and are lined by cholangiocytes, 179 

whose structural organization influences bile flow and composition [21]. Despite their 180 

importance in liver physiology and disease, detailed 3D analysis of bile duct archi-181 

tecture in human tissue remains limited. The bile duct comprises a central lumen 182 

surrounded by cholangiocytes (Fig. 3a). Across the serial EM slices, cholangiocytes 183 

were consistently observed encircling the lumen. Both the lumen diameter and the 184 

number of surrounding cholangiocytes increased progressively along the duct length 185 

(Fig. 3b). Quantitative analysis across the entire volume shows a positive correla-186 

tion between lumen cross-sectional area and cholangiocyte number (Fig. 3c). To study 187 

lumen-cell contacts, we compared lumen cross-sectional area with average cholangio-188 

cyte cross-sectional area in individual slices (2D), and lumen cross-sectional area with 189 

average volume of nearby cholangiocytes across the duct (3D) (Fig. 3d, Supplemen-190 

tary Fig. 3a). Both approaches reveal a strong positive relationship between lumen 191 

size and cholangiocyte number and size, indicating coordination between the ductal 192 

lumen and its epithelial lining. 193 

Beyond the bile duct system, sinusoidal capillaries constitute the primary microvas-194 

culature mediating blood-hepatocyte exchange. Sinusoidal branching patterns deter-195 

mine local blood flow dynamics and influence regional hepatocyte function [2]. We 196 

used the cellular-scale map capturing sinusoidal capillaries extending through the vol-197 

ume to investigate the change around a prominent branching point near the middle of 198

## 2.4 Subcellular-scale analysis reveals mitochondrial 220 

## morphological distribution 221 

Beyond cellular and vascular structures, the subcellular-scale labeling of the multi-222 

scale volume revealed 35,790 complete mitochondria exhibiting diverse morphologies 223 

(Fig. 1f, Fig. 2b). To systematically characterize this morphological heterogeneity, 224 

we performed quantitative feature extraction and dimensionality reduction analysis. 225 

Shape-related morphological features of each mitochondrion were computed using 226 

PyRadiomics [22], which computes quantitative descriptors from 3D images (Table 6). 227 

Each mitochondrial instance was processed to generate a feature vector, which was 228 

subjected to principal component analysis (PCA) (Fig. 4a). PC1 accounts for the 229 

largest proportion of total variance (60.3%, Supplementary Fig. 4a). Mapping the 230 

extracted morphological features onto PCA space reveals that PC1 predominantly cap-231 

tures mitochondrial size, with higher PC1 values corresponding to increased surface 232 

area, mesh volume, and voxel volume (Fig. 4b, Supplementary Fig. 5). This trend was 233 

accompanied by concurrent changes in elongation (Fig. 4a-b, Supplementary Fig. 5). 234 

Visual inspection of representative mitochondria across the PC1 spectrum confirms 235 

that increasing PC1 values correspond to larger and more elongated morphologies 236 

(Fig. 4c). To examine the spatial distribution of morphologically distinct mitochondria 237 

within the volume, we selected representative instances from opposite tails of the PC1 238 

distribution: low-PC1 instances from the left of the distribution peak and high-PC1 239 

instances from the right (Supplementary Fig. 4b). Visualization of these examples in 240 

both two-dimensional cross-sections and three-dimensional subvolumes validated that 241 

PC1 reflects mitochondrial size and elongation (Fig. 4d-e). To characterize the spatial 242

## 2.5 Mitochondria-ER contacts reveal mitochondrial dynamics 267 

Given that mitochondria undergo continuous fission and fusion cycles [23], the 268 

observed morphological heterogeneity prompted us to investigate whether distinct 269 

morphological states reflect underlying mitochondrial dynamics. Because the ER 270 

has been implicated in regulating mitochondrial division and fusion events [23, 24], 271 

we analyzed the spatial relationships between mitochondria and ER in the human 272 

liver volume, beginning with comparisons across mitochondria of different size and 273 

elongation (PC1). To quantify mitochondria–ER contacts, we adopted a previously 274 

established distance-based method [25]. The ER surface was iteratively expanded at 275 

fixed distance intervals, and its intersection with the mitochondrial surface was mea-276 

sured at each step to quantify contact as a function of distance (Fig. 5a). Cryo-electron 277 

and transmission electron microscopy studies have shown that smooth mitochondria-278 

ER contacts typically occur at 8-25 nm, whereas rough ER contacts can extend to 279 

50-60 nm [25, 26]. Accordingly, we expanded the ER label at 32 nm (4 voxels) intervals 280 

up to 128 nm (16 voxels), at which distance most mitochondrial surfaces were covered 281 

by ER with minimal additional increase in contact beyond 96 nm (Fig. 5a). Two-282 

dimensional and three-dimensional visualizations of representative high- and low-PC1 283 

mitochondria reveal extensive ER surrounding both morphological classes (Fig. 5a-b). 284 

To determine whether mitochondria with distinct PC1 morphologies exhibit differen-285 

tial ER contacts, we compared the high- and low-PC1 populations defined in our PCA 286

narrowed mitochondria exhibit no apparent spatial preference for ER contact at either 321 

distance. 322

# 3 Discussion 340 

We present a multiscale, multicellular vEM image of a human liver periportal region, 341 

establishing a nanoscale reference atlas spanning tissue, vascular, cellular, and subcel-342 

lular organization. To achieve this, we developed an integrated imaging pipeline that 343 

accommodates multiscale region selection and acquisition across spatial scales. Using 344 

deep learning-based segmentation, we generated a comprehensive three-dimensional 345 

annotation encompassing vasculature, cells, and organelles. This multilevel recon-346 

struction enables quantitative analyses across spatial scales, revealing structural 347 

heterogeneity at multiple levels of organization: vascular architecture including bile 348 

duct and sinusoidal branching patterns, cellular-scale features such as endothelial 349 

coverage and cholangiocyte distribution, and organelle-level characteristics including 350 

mitochondrial morphological diversity and spatially enriched mitochondria-ER con-351 

tacts at sites of mitochondrial narrowing, consistent with signatures of mitochondrial 352 

dynamics. 353 

The observed correlation between bile duct lumen size and cholangiocyte num-354 

ber and size aligns with mechanobiological models of ductal morphogenesis. Previous 355 

studies have shown that bile duct lumen formation depends on coordinated cholan-356 

giocyte proliferation, osmotic forces from biliary secretions, and timed differentiation, 357 

with luminal expansion emerging from these processes [21]. Our finding that lumen 358 

enlargement is accompanied by an increase in surrounding cholangiocytes is consistent 359 

with these mechanisms. This relationship also provides a framework for understand-360 

ing pathologies such as vanishing bile duct syndrome, where ductopenia (the loss or 361 

absence of bile ducts per portal tract) reflects progressive cholangiocyte depletion 362 

and precedes cholestatic disease progression [27, 28]. The strong structural correlation 363 

between lumen size and cholangiocyte abundance in our dataset thus mirrors clinical 364 

observations of bile duct loss in cholangiopathies, suggesting a potential morphological 365 

basis for early diagnostic indicators. 366 

The asymmetric sinusoidal branching observed in the labeled volume, where a thin-367 

ner branch with extensive endothelial-lumen contact converges with a wider branch 368 

exhibiting reduced endothelial coverage, suggests localized adaptation to mechani-369 

cal stress. Liver sinusoidal endothelial cells are highly mechanosensitive, modulating 370 

fenestration size and number in response to flow dynamics to regulate material 371 

exchange between sinusoidal blood and hepatocytes [29]. Our ultrastructural observa-372 

tions captured fenestrated endothelium lining the sinusoidal capillary (Supplementary 373

However, validation of this hypothesis requires comparative analysis with diseased 389 

tissue. 390 

Quantitative analysis of 35,790 mitochondria and their ER contacts provides ultra-391 

structural evidence consistent with ER-mediated regulation of mitochondrial dynamics 392 

in human liver tissue. Previous studies in cell culture and animal models have 393 

demonstrated that ER tubules mark mitochondrial division sites and remain closely 394 

associated during fission and fusion events, with smooth ER specifically implicated at 395 

these sites [6, 7, 23, 25]. Although not all elongated mitochondria in our dataset nec-396 

essarily represent active fission or fusion intermediates captured in a static snapshot, 397 

statistical analysis across this large population revealed consistent enrichment of ER 398 

contacts at narrowing sites, aligning with these prior observations. This correlation 399 

suggests that a subset of the morphological heterogeneity observed here reflects ongo-400 

ing mitochondrial dynamics preserved as ultrastructural signatures in human liver 401 

tissue. 402 

The imaging resolution and contrast in our dataset precluded reliable identification 403 

of ribosomes to distinguish smooth from rough ER. Nevertheless, the preferential ER 404 

enrichment at mitochondrial narrowing sites falls within the distance range reported 405 

for smooth ER contacts [25, 26]. Future studies employing higher-resolution imaging 406 

or correlative approaches will be necessary to definitively characterize ER subtype 407 

involvement in human tissue. Additionally, we did not detect zonation-dependent 408 

differences in mitochondrial morphology across hepatocytes, potentially because our 409 

dataset was limited to the periportal region. Extending this approach to liver tis-410 

sue volumes spanning the porto-central axis will determine whether mitochondrial 411 

morphological gradients exist in human liver. 412 

Collectively, this work provides an unprecedented multiscale reconstruction of 413 

human liver architecture, establishing both a structural reference for the healthy peri-414 

portal region and a computational framework for quantitative organelle analysis. The 415 

imaging and analysis pipeline developed here will facilitate comparative studies across 416 

hepatic zones and disease states, enabling investigation of how tissue, vascular, cel-417 

lular, and organelle organization becomes altered in liver pathology. Such multiscale 418

## 4.1 Human liver tissue acquisition 423 

Human liver tissue was obtained from a neurologically deceased donor deemed accept-424 

able for liver transplantation. Sample collection was performed under institutional 425 

ethics approval (University Health Network REB# 14-7425-AE) with de-identified 426 

donor metadata recorded and stored securely, as previously described [34]. 427 

Donor livers were flushed in situ with cold histidine-tryptophan-ketoglutarate 428 

(HTK) preservation solution. The caudate lobe was resected and maintained in HTK 429 

solution on ice. In the laboratory, the lobe was cannulated using 1.2–2.0 mm irrigation 430 

cannulae inserted into exposed vessels on the cut surface and gently perfused at 4 °C431 

with HEPES-buffered saline (HBS) containing EGTA (approximately 10 mL min −1

> 432

per cannula) to clear residual blood. 433 

For fixation, tissue was transferred to a chilled Petri dish, trimmed into approxi-434 

mately 1 mm 3 cubes using a sterile single-edge razor blade, and immediately immersed 435 

in aldehyde fixative (4% formaldehyde and 2.5% glutaraldehyde in 0.1 M cacodylate 436 

buffer, pH 7.4) for 12–24 h at 4 °C. After fixation, samples were rinsed thoroughly in 437 

0.1 M cacodylate buffer (3 × 10 min). 438 

## 4.2 Tissue processing for volume electron microscopy 439 

Aldehyde-fixed tissues were processed using a modified OTO (osmium-440 

thiocarbohydrazide-osmium) protocol. Samples were first post-fixed in 2% osmium 441 

tetroxide (OsO 4) with 1% potassium ferrocyanide (K 4[Fe(CN) 6]) in 0.1 M cacodylate 442 

buffer for 90 min at room temperature. After washing with distilled water, samples 443 

were stained with 1% thiocarbohydrazide (TCH) for 30 min, rinsed, and stained 444 

with 1% osmium tetroxide for 1 h. Tissue was then washed and dehydrated through 445 

a graded ethanol series (50%, 70%, 90%, 100%; 20 min each step) and equilibrated 446 

in propylene oxide. Infiltration proceeded in a 1:1 mixture of propylene oxide and 447 

Spurr resin for 2 h, followed by 100% Spurr resin overnight at room temperature. 448 

The following day, samples were transferred to fresh Spurr resin for an additional 2 h, 449 

embedded in silicone molds, and polymerized at 70 °C for 24 h. 450 

## 4.3 X-ray micro-computed tomography 451 

X-ray micro-computed tomography was performed on a Zeiss Xradia Versa 510 X-ray 452 

microscope to assess sample integrity and identify regions of interest prior to electron 453 

microscopy. The resin-embedded specimen was imaged at 80 kV with 360 ° sample 454 

rotation to acquire projection images for three-dimensional reconstruction. 455

## 4.5 Serial block-face scanning electron microscopy 469 

Imaging was performed on a Zeiss Gemini 2 SEM equipped with a Gatan 3View 470 

2XP ultramicrotome. Serial sectioning and image acquisition were automated using 471 

3View control software. Images were acquired at an accelerating voltage of 2.5 kV 472 

with a 30 μm objective aperture, the electron gun in analytical mode, and high-473 

current beam settings. Backscattered electron signal was detected using the in-column 474 

detector to enhance heavy-metal contrast. Focal charge compensation with nitrogen 475 

gas was applied during imaging to mitigate charging artifacts. For each section, the 476 

dwell time was 3 μs per pixel with 8 nm pixel size. The sectioning thickness (z-step) 477 

was 55 nm throughout the acquisition. Autofocus and stigmator correction routines 478 

were engaged at regular intervals, and drift correction was applied between cycles as 479 

needed. The resulting dataset comprised a contiguous three-dimensional volume with 480 

8 nm in-plane pixel size and 55 nm axial spacing. 481 

## 4.6 EM image stitching and alignment 482 

Stitching and alignment were performed in Python using Google’s SOFIMA algorithm 483 

(https://github.com/google-research/sofima) [13]. For stitching, we processed 5 ×5 tile 484 

arrays for each section of the serial block-face scanning electron microscopy dataset. 485 

Coarse tile position optimization was first applied to roughly define relative tile posi-486 

tions by solving a constrained optimization problem. The obtained relative positions 487 

from the coarse optimization step were then used to compute flow fields between pairs 488 

of neighboring tiles to achieve fine tile position optimization. For mesh solving, each 489 

tile was modeled as a spring mesh, and the equilibrium state was calculated to bal-490 

ance two competing forces: the spring elastic force, which preserves the original tile 491 

geometry, and a force associated with the calculated flow field, which ensures connec-492 

tivity with neighboring tiles. Based on the solved mesh, the optimally positioned tiles 493 

were rendered to generate the final stitched section. 494 

For alignment, flow fields were computed between each stitched section and its 495 

adjacent sections. Both full-resolution and 2 × downsampled flow fields were calculated, 496 

as the lower-resolution flow field can compensate for regions where the full-resolution 497

To facilitate visualization and annotation, the vEM dataset was imported into a CAT-504

## 4.8 Manual segmentation of subcellular structures 516 

Following pathologist validation, manual segmentation was performed on selected 517 

sections. Target sections were imported into VAST [20], a tool for labeling large image 518 

stacks. We used the pen tool to manually annotate nuclei, cell boundaries, mitochon-519 

dria, and ER within hepatocyte regions. The segmentation labels were then exported 520 

from VAST for downstream deep learning model training. 521 

## 4.9 Vascular and cellular scale segmentation 522 

Vascular structures (sinusoidal capillary lumen, bile duct lumen, and portal vein 523 

lumen) and cellular structures (endothelial cells, cholangiocytes, and hepatocytes) 524 

were segmented using SAM2 [15], a foundation model for promptable segmentation 525 

of images and videos. Annotated point prompts were placed in CATMAID on each 526 

target structure, positioned at the middle cross-section of objects spanning the z-axis. 527 

In image mode, the model predicted the label of the current object, which was then 528 

proofread. Based on this label, a bounding box was defined by recording the left-529 

most, topmost, rightmost, and bottommost coordinates. These bounding boxes were 530 

input to the model in video mode to propagate the predicted labels from the initial 531 

section to both the start and end of the instance across the volume, achieving complete 532 

three-dimensional segmentation of each object. To evaluate segmentation performance, 533 

three randomly selected cross-sections of each object were manually annotated and 534 

compared with the corresponding model predictions. All labels underwent final proof-535 

reading. Certain cellular structures presented segmentation challenges due to their 536 

inherent morphology. Sinusoidal endothelial cells possess extremely thin cytoplasmic 537 

extensions and fenestrated regions where cell boundaries are not clearly delineated at 538

## 4.10 Subcellular scale segmentation 545 

Nuclei, mitochondria, ER, and hepatocyte boundaries were automatically segmented 546 

using nnU-Net [16], a self-configuring U-Net model. The model processes individual 2D 547 

image slices as input and predicts pixel-wise segmentation. All hyperparameters and 548 

model configurations follow the nnU-Net self-configured settings. Each segmentation 549 

class was trained with a separate model. Models of nucleus, mitochondrion, and ER 550 

were pre-trained on five mouse liver datasets from OpenOrganelle [19] (dataset IDs: 551 

jrc mus-liver, jrc mus-liver-4, jrc mus-liver-5, jrc mus-liver-6, and jrc mus-liver-7). 552 

Model of cell boundary was pre-trained on one mouse liver dataset from OpenOr-553 

ganelle (dataset ID: jrc mus-liver) After pre-training, the neural network encoder 554 

was frozen and the model was further trained on our manually annotated human 555 

liver images. Human-in-the-loop refinement was performed by manually correcting 556 

the predicted segmentation on images not included in the current training set. 557 

These revised segmentations and their corresponding images were then added to the 558 

training data for subsequent iterations. This process was repeated for two loops. 559 

Finally, the predicted segmentations were post-processed using a series of mor-560 

phological operations Supplementary (Table 4). The segmentations were smoothed 561 

using a Gaussian filter (scipy.ndimage.gaussian filter) to reduce boundary irregu-562 

larities. Connected component analysis (skimage.measure.label) was performed to 563 

identify individual objects. Size filtering was used to remove small spurious frag-564 

ments (skimage.morphology.remove small objects). Internal holes within segmented 565 

structures were filled (scipy.ndimage.binary fill holes) to ensure topological consis-566 

tency. And watershed-based agglomeration was applied to merge over-segmented 567 

regions (skimage.segmentation.watershed). For segmentation evaluation, we randomly 568 

selected three fully manually segmented sections that were excluded from training. All 569 

classes were evaluated by comparing manual and automated segmentation on these 570 

test images. Model performance was assessed at each stage (pre-trained models, each 571 

loop training, and final segmentation) using F1 score (Dice coefficient), intersection-572 

over-union (IoU), precision, recall, and mean false distance based on the Hausdorff 573 

distance. 574

## 4.16 Mitochondria-ER contact characterization 630 

Mitochondria-ER contacts were characterized by measuring the distance between 631 

mitochondrial surfaces and ER. Following a previously established method [25], the 632 

mitochondrial outer surface was generated by eroding the mitochondrial volume by 633 

one voxel and subtracting the eroded volume from the original volume. ER labels were 634 

then iteratively dilated based on the desired distance intervals. The number of over-635 

lapping voxels between the dilated ER and mitochondrial surface was counted as the 636 

interacting surface. The percentage of mitochondrial surface covered by ER was calcu-637 

lated by dividing the interacting surface area by the total mitochondrial surface area. 638 

To avoid multiple counting, voxels counted in a given dilation step were excluded from 639 

subsequent larger dilation steps. 640 

## 4.17 Mitochondrial dynamics in relation to narrowing sites 641 

Mitochondria were filtered based on their morphological characteristics as captured 642 

by principal component analysis (PCA). Only mitochondria with PC1 values greater 643 

than 1 were included in the analysis. These were further subdivided into narrowing 644 

mitochondria (PC2 > 1) and non-narrowing mitochondria (PC2 < -1). To identify 645 

narrowing sites, the major axis of each mitochondrion was determined by performing 646 

PCA on the 3D coordinates of its constituent voxels. Cross-sectional areas were cal-647 

culated perpendicular to this axis at regular intervals. A narrowing site was defined 648 

as a position exhibiting a local minimum in cross-sectional area, excluding the termi-649 

nal regions where cross-sectional area naturally decreases toward the mitochondrial 650 

endpoints. The local minimum was further validated by requiring it to be less than 651 

80% of the median cross-sectional area along the entire axis. When multiple local 652 

minima were present, the position with the smallest cross-sectional area was selected 653 

as the narrowing site. For narrowing mitochondria, the peri-narrowing region was 654 

defined as the 50% of the mitochondrial surface centered on the narrowing site. For 655 

non-narrowing mitochondria lacking an identifiable local minimum in cross-sectional 656 

area, the peri-narrowing region was defined as the middle 50% of the surface (cor-657 

responding to 25–75% along the major axis). In both cases, the remaining surface 658 

constituted the non-peri-narrowing region. Contact enrichment was calculated as the 659 

ratio of mitochondria–ER contact density in the peri-narrowing region to that in the 660
