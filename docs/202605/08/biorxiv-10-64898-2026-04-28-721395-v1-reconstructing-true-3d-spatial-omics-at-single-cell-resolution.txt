## ABSTRACT 

Capturing the three-dimensional (3D) organization of cells is essential for deciphering complex biological processes, yet comprehensive 3D spatial omics is severely hindered by the destructive nature of physical sectioning and the depth limitations of intact tissue imaging. Current computational methods rely on 2.5D stacking of discrete slices, which inherently disrupts tissue topology and fails to resolve continuous depth-dependent molecular gradients. To bridge this gap, we introduce D EEP SPATIAL ,an Optimal Transport flow matching framework that models tissue evolution as a continuous dynamic vector field. By solving the underlying probability flow ODEs, D EEP SPATIAL enables the direct extraction of uninterrupted, infinitely resolvable tissue states at arbitrary spatial depths. Using Deep STAR/RIBOmap 3D technologies, we demonstrate that D EEP SPATIAL achieves improved 3D reconstruction fidelity relative to 2.5D approaches, yielding structures that more closely recapitulate native tissue microenvironments in real-world datasets. Across diverse spatial omics modalities, including spatial proteomics using imaging mass cytometry in human breast cancer and spatial transcriptomics using openST in head and neck squamous cell carcinoma metastatic lymph nodes, D EEP SPATIAL produces biologically interpretable and high-fidelity reconstructions across datasets. We evaluated the scalability and robustness of D EEP SPATIAL on a large-scale mouse brain dataset, reconstructing a continuous 3D cellular atlas comprising 39 million cells within 41.6 hours. Systematic downstream characterization validated its ability to recapitulate consistent spatial architectures, cell-type distributions, transcriptomic patterns, and microenvironmental structures across brain regions. Collectively, these results demonstrate D EEP SPATIAL as a generalizable and efficient solution for true 3D spatial reconstruction across scales and modalities. 

## 1 Introduction 1

Spatial omics has profoundly advanced our understanding of tissue architecture by enabling the mapping of gene expression 2

patterns within their native spatial context 1–4 . Because the three-dimensional (3D) organization of cells is tightly coupled to 3

their biological function, comprehensive 3D spatial profiling is essential for resolving complex biological systems 5, 6 . Such 4

approaches enable the reconstruction of continuous cellular and structural transitions, the delineation of spatial heterogeneity 5

and molecular gradients, and the preservation of intact tissue architecture necessary to uncover localized transcriptional 6

dysregulation 7–9 .7

Current experimental approaches to capture 3D spatial omics generally fall into two categories: invasive serial sectioning 8

and non-invasive intact thick-tissue profiling. Invasive techniques involve physically sectioning the tissue into sequential 2D 9

slices, a process that inherently destroys the continuous 3D structure and introduces physical artifacts such as tears, folds, 10 

and geometric deformations 8, 10, 11 . Conversely, intact 3D imaging technologies attempt to profile thick tissue blocks without 11 

sectioning. However, these methods face severe physical and practical limitations: they are typically restricted to relatively thin 12 

tissue volumes (up to 200 μm), rendering organ-level 3D mapping impractical 12, 13 . Additionally, these approaches are heavily 13 

constrained by resource-intensive, highly complex experimental protocols and prohibitive costs. 14 

To overcome the experimental bottlenecks associated with physical sectioning and imaging depth, researchers have developed 15 

various computational frameworks to achieve 3D spatial analysis using existing 2D multi-slice datasets. Early computational 16

Despite these computational advancements, current frameworks fundamentally construct 2.5D models that rely on the spatial 24 

stacking of discrete 2D planes 18 . Even with the aid of dense virtual interpolation, the underlying representation remains 25 

inherently discontinuous and resolution-limited 21, 22 . Excessive interpolation also introduces spurious spatial artifacts and 26 

redundant computations. This dimensional fragmentation between adjacent layers forcibly disrupts the continuous spatial 27 

connectivity of the tissue microenvironment, often obscuring subtle depth-dependent molecular gradients and blurring fine 28 

anatomical boundaries 23 . Such discrete frameworks struggle to accurately reconstruct the complex 3D cellular neighborhoods 29 

and topological structures that span multiple layers. To truly capture the uninterrupted topology and fluid transitions within 30 

biological microenvironments, transitioning from discrete 2.5D stacking to continuous, real 3D modeling is strictly necessary. 31 

To address this fundamental limitation, we propose D EEP SPATIAL , a generative framework based on flow matching 24, 25 that 32 

seamlessly reconstructs the continuous 3D tissue microenvironment from discrete measured slices. D EEP SPATIAL reframes the 33 

multi-modal transitions between physical slices as a mixed continuous-discrete probability density evolution. Methodologically, 34 

we first establish biologically rigorous cell-to-cell correspondences via Unbalanced Optimal Transport (UOT) 26 , explicitly 35 

constrained by spatial, transcriptomic, and categorical cell-type affinities. To model the underlying continuous dynamics, we 36 

introduce a scalable Transformer 27, 28 architecture that employs a unified tokenization strategy to process heterogeneous cell 37 

states within a shared high-dimensional vector field. By solving the learned Probability Flow Ordinary Differential Equation 38 

(ODE) 29, 30 with a density-preserving bidirectional sampling strategy, D EEP SPATIAL enables the direct and exact recovery 39 

of uninterrupted tissue topology at any arbitrary physical depth. Consequently, D EEP SPATIAL completely overcomes the 40 

structural fragmentation of physical sectioning, achieving a paradigm shift in spatial omics from slice-level 2.5D stacking to 41 

biologically faithful, volume-level 3D continuous modeling. 42 

Comprehensive experimental validation across diverse biological systems, including tumor microenvironments, metastatic 43 

lymph nodes, and a 39-million-cell mouse whole-brain atlas, demonstrates that D EEP SPATIAL achieves a transformative 44 

shift from discrete 2.5D stacking to biologically faithful 3D continuous modeling. To facilitate broad adoption and further 45 

development within the community, the DeepSpatial framework, along with its interactive documentation, full source code, and 46 

reproducibility tutorials, is publicly accessible at our Project Homepage and GitHub Repository. 47

2.1 Overview of D EEP SPATIAL 49 

DEEP SPATIAL is a generative deep-learning framework designed to construct a continuous, true 3D tissue atlas from sparsely 50 

sampled 2D spatial omics sections (Fig. 1). By reframing 3D reconstruction as a continuous probability density evolution, 51 

the model seamlessly bridges physical z-axis gaps. As illustrated in Fig. 1a, the framework processes multi-layer 2D spatial 52 

data by integrating spatial coordinates, high-dimensional omics features (genes and proteins), and physical slice metadata 53 

(thickness and interval). Through this continuous generative process, D EEP SPATIAL systematically transforms these discrete 54 

inputs into a spatially continuous 3D virtual tissue microenvironment (Fig. 1b), wherein every synthesized cell is assigned exact 55 

3D coordinates, omics expressions and cell-type identities. 56 

The computational core of D EEP SPATIAL models the multi-modal transitions between physical slices as a mixed continuous-57 

discrete probability density evolution (Fig. 1c). First, to establish biologically rigorous alignments across physical gaps, the 58 

model computes cell-to-cell correspondences via UOT. This is governed by a cost matrix that balances spatial proximity 59 

and transcriptomic similarity while accounting for cellular proliferation or apoptosis. Second, we employ a scalable multi-60 

modal Transformer trained via flow matching to learn a shared, continuous vector field representing the dynamic evolution of 61 

heterogeneous cell states. Finally, by solving the underlying Probability Flow ODE guided by an interpolated macroscopic cell 62 

density field, D EEP SPATIAL enables the direct and exact synthesis of tissue states at any arbitrary spatial depth. 63 

This paradigm shift from discrete 2.5D stacking to continuous 3D modeling unlocks comprehensive downstream analytical 64 

capabilities (Fig. 1d). The uninterrupted 3D microenvironment allows for the extraction of highly resolved virtual slices at 65 

arbitrary depths and the precise delineation of topologically complex 3D spatial domains. Furthermore, the framework’s 66 

scalability supports the reconstruction of large, organ-level spatial architectures, such as a 39-million-cell mouse whole brain 67

2.2 Benchmarking D EEP SPATIAL against real 3D spatial data 69 

To further evaluate the spatial reconstruction fidelity of D EEP SPATIAL , we tested it on three real 3D datasets: Deep-STARmap 70 

(cSCC and mouse brain) and Deep-RIBOmap (mouse brain), each derived from 150 μm continuous tissue volumes 13 . We 71 

first downsampled the ground-truth 3D data into sparse pseudo-2D sections (Fig. 2b), then reconstructed full volumes using 72 

DEEP SPATIAL (true 3D) and SpatialZ (2.5D stacking), respectively. Finally, we quantitatively compared the two reconstructions 73 

against the ground truth at both spatial and transcriptomic levels (Fig. 2a). 74 

We first confirmed that cell-type proportions were highly consistent among ground-truth, 3D-reconstructed, and 2.5D-imputed 75 

data. We then divided each 3D volume into local patches ( 50 × 50 × 30 for cSCC; 50 × 50 × 50 for mouse brain) and computed 76 

the cosine similarity of cell-type distributions within each patch. D EEP SPATIAL -reconstructed tissues showed significantly 77 

higher similarity to ground truth: 0.37 (cSCC), 0.48 (STARmap), 0.43 (RIBOmap), whereas 2.5D imputation only achieved 78 

0.19, 0.27, and 0.26 (Figs. 2c–e), which is attributed to severe discontinuity along the z-axis. We further validated that virtual 79 

cross-sections from our 3D reconstruction closely matched the real data (Figs. S1b, S2b, S3b). 80 

We next evaluated transcriptomic spatial structure by computing Moran’s I for the top 100 highly variable genes. Both 81 

reconstructions correlated well with ground truth, but D EEP SPATIAL 3D consistently achieved higher accuracy: cSCC 82 

(R = 0.897 vs 0.766 ), mouse brain STARmap ( R = 0.902 vs 0.884 ), RIBOmap ( R = 0.875 vs 0.816 ) (Figs. 2c–e). Cell-type-83 

specific marker genes were faithfully preserved in 3D reconstructions, including CD3E (T cells), CD79A (B cells), Gad1 /Gad2 84 

(inhibitory neurons), and Slc17a7 (excitatory neurons), confirming high transcriptomic fidelity. 85 

2.3 D EEP SPATIAL reveals immune–vascular organization in the breast cancer microenvironment 86 

In real-world applications, spatial datasets typically consist of multiple physical sections acquired at fixed intervals. To validate 87 

DEEP SPATIAL under such conditions, we applied it to a 15-slice imaging mass cytometry (IMC) dataset of breast cancer 88 

(BRCA), with 2 μm thickness and 20 μm inter-slice gaps (Fig. 3a) 11 . Independent clustering and annotation across slices 89 

confirmed consistent cell-type identities and marker expression prior to reconstruction (Figs. S4–S6). Using D EEP SPATIAL , we 90 

reconstructed a continuous 3D tumor microenvironment spanning 280 μm along the z-axis (Fig. 3a). 91 

Virtual slicing analysis showed that synthetic slices from the 3D volume closely matched the original IMC data, with fine-scale 92 

tissue architecture faithfully preserved (Fig. 3b). Sagittal and coronal virtual cross-sections further revealed that D EEP SPATIAL 93 

effectively eliminated gaps from physical sectioning, yielding continuous tissue structures with clear epithelial, basal-like 94 

epithelial, and stromal compartments (Figs. S8a–d). Cell-type proportions were highly consistent between original and 95 

reconstructed data (Fig. 3c). At the protein level, cell-type-specific markers, including CD20 (B cells), CD3 (T cells), and CK5 96 

(basal epithelial cells), exhibited nearly identical spatial patterns (Fig. 3d). 97 

Vascular architecture is a key feature of the BRCA tumor microenvironment 31, 32 . While original slices only show fragmented 98 

vessels, the full 3D reconstruction revealed a continuous, hierarchical vascular network with fine distal branches (Figs. S7c–e). 99 

Cell neighborhood analysis demonstrated that endothelial cells were significantly co-localized with T cells, B cells, and 100 

fibroblasts, indicating tight immune–vascular coupling (Fig. 3e). Spatial distribution along the z-axis further revealed that 101 

immune cells accumulated at 150–250 μm, while endothelial cells were enriched near 50 μm (Figs. S7f,g). Notably, immune 102 

clusters preferentially localized to distal vascular ends, revealing a spatially organized immune–vascular niche in BRCA 103 

(Fig. S7c). 104 

2.4 3D Spatial Reconstruction of openST spatial transcriptomics dataset Using D EEP SPATIAL 105 

Spatial transcriptomics has recently emerged as a powerful tool for studying disease and tumor microenvironments. The openST 106 

technology enables whole-transcriptome spatial profiling and supports 3D spatial characterization through multiple tissue 107 

sections 33 . Here, we applied the D EEP SPATIAL framework to reconstruct and complete 3D spatial structures from multi-layer 108 

openST data of metastatic lymph nodes in head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC) (Fig. 4a). 109 

Due to large and irregular inter-slice spacing that cannot support continuous whole-volume reconstruction, we split the dataset 110 

into four subgroups for independent 3D reconstruction (Figs. 4a–c, S5a,b). To avoid edge artifacts, all analyses were restricted 111 

to the maximal common region shared across all sections (Figs. 4b,c). D EEP SPATIAL generated smooth cellular transitions 112 

along the z-axis, with cell-type proportions varying continuously rather than discretely (Fig. 4b). At the transcriptomic level, 113 

despite the high dimensionality and inherent noise characteristic of such data, gene expression patterns were recovered with 114 

high fidelity. Canonical, biologically relevant marker genes were consistently preserved across cell types, including S100A7 in 115 

keratinized tumor cells, and SPP1 in CAM populations (Figs. 4c, S10). 116

method at single-cell resolution, to delineate diverse spatial architectures within metastatic lymph nodes. Tumor regions were 129

2.5 Scalable 3D reconstruction of a 39-million-cell mouse whole-brain spatial atlas 134 

A major challenge in large-scale 3D tissue reconstruction is the high computational cost from high-dimensional omics features 135 

and millions of cells. We applied D EEP SPATIAL to a CCF-aligned mouse whole-brain dataset with 129 sections (4,167,889 136 

cells) 10 (Fig. 5a), which expanded to 39,168,380 cells after 3D reconstruction. Notably, full reconstruction was completed 137 

within 41.6 hours on a mid-range server, demonstrating excellent efficiency and scalability (Table S3). 138 

We first examined cellular clustering in the 3D brain. UMAP embeddings showed similar cell-type and brain-region clustering 139 

between original and reconstructed data (Fig. 5c). A key advantage of true 3D reconstruction is z-axis continuity: relative 140 

to discontinuous raw sections, D EEP SPATIAL produced smooth spatial distributions across x/y/z axes, especially along the 141 

discrete-sampled z-axis (Figs. 5b,d, S14a,b). Cell-type proportions within each brain region were strongly correlated between 142 

original and reconstructed tissues, confirming high-fidelity preservation of local structure (Fig. 5e). 143 

Transcriptomic fidelity was maintained at the whole-brain scale. Major cell-type markers showed nearly identical expression 144 

levels and fractions: Slc17a7 (excitatory neurons), Cldn5 (vascular endothelial cells), Ptprc (immune cells) (Fig. 5f). Fine 145 

subpopulations were also preserved: Gad2 (GABAergic neurons), Hdc /Slc18a2 (histaminergic neurons), Gja1 /Gpr37l1 146 

(astrocytes), Sall1 /Ctss /Mafb (microglia), Rgs5 (pericytes) (Fig. S13). These results demonstrate that D EEP SPATIAL preserves 147 

cellular identity and transcriptome profile across millions of cells. 148 

To further validate the biological authenticity of our 3D reconstruction, we performed multi-planar virtual slicing, a critical yet 149 

challenging task for 3D tissue modeling. We integrated whole-brain datasets from two mice for comprehensive comparison: 150 

Animal 1 (129 coronal slices) served as the ground-truth for interpolation, while Animal 2 (23 horizontal slices) provided 151 

independent validation for orthogonal slicing (Fig. 6a). In the coronal plane, virtual slices from the 3D-reconstructed tissue 152 

exhibited near-perfect concordance with the GT coronal sections, accurately recapitulating both brain region anatomy and 153 

spatial patterns of canonical markers including Slc17a7 and Gfap (Fig. 6b). 154 

Notably, the reconstruction maintained high biological fidelity even in the challenging sagittal and horizontal planes. For 155 

horizontal slicing, we compared virtual slices from the reconstructed volume with both the genuine horizontal sections 156 

(Animal 2) and the horizontally stacked raw 129 coronal sections (Figs. 6c, S15a, b). The raw sparse coronal slices exhibited 157 

discontinuous, incomplete boundaries across large gaps in the horizontal direction, with severe structural fragmentation. In 158 

contrast, the D EEP SPATIAL -reconstructed 3D volume reproduced continuous, anatomically consistent horizontal sections 159 

highly similar to genuine experimental horizontal slices, both in brain region organization and spatial gene expression patterns 160 

(Fig. 6c). Comparable results were observed in sagittal virtual slicing (Figs. S16a, b). 161 

Beyond virtual sectioning, 3D reconstruction enables direct visualization of fine spatial architectures within individual brain 162 

regions. In the striatum, olfactory bulb, and hippocampus, we observed continuous volumetric organization and clear 3D spatial 163 

arrangements of fine cellular subtypes (Fig. 6d). Collectively, these results demonstrate that D EEP SPATIAL captures realistic, 164 

fine-grained 3D cellular architecture compatible with multi-directional virtual slicing and direct volumetric analysis, supporting 165 

its generalizability for atlas-scale 3D reconstruction. 166

## 3 Discussion 167 

The spatial omics community has established a highly mature ecosystem 35 of computational tools dedicated to resolving planar 168 

heterogeneity, batch correction 36 , and spatial clustering 37 . Moving beyond these critical yet plane-restricted achievements, 169 

DEEP SPATIAL is designed to tackle a completely different frontier: bridging the physical gaps inherently left by tissue 170 

sectioning. Our core contribution is fundamentally shifting the analytical paradigm from discrete 2.5D stacking to true 171 

topological continuity. This conceptual leap allows the seamless transformation of physically fragmented slices into an 172 

infinitely resolvable 3D atlas. Consequently, D EEP SPATIAL functions as an essential infrastructural layer; it does not compete 173 

with current spatial pipelines, but rather empowers them by providing the continuous 3D foundation necessary for authentic 174 

whole-organ analysis. 175 

The methodological superiority of D EEP SPATIAL lies in its departure from rigid spatial registration and discrete linear 176 

interpolation. Unlike alignment-centric methods that merely correct geometric distortions, or imputation frameworks that 177 

insert pseudo-2D planes, D EEP SPATIAL learns the underlying continuous dynamics of the tissue microenvironment. Our 178 

utilization of UOT establishes biologically rigorous cross-slice correspondences that naturally accommodate variations in tissue 179 

cross-sectional areas and cellular densities along the z-axis, relaxing the restrictive assumption of strict mass conservation. 180 

Coupled with a scalable multi-modal Transformer, the learned continuous vector field ensures that intermediate tissue states 181 

generated via ODE integration rigorously preserve both macroscopic cellular density and microscopic molecular gradients, 182 

completely eliminating the z-axis discontinuities inherent to previous 2.5D approaches. 183 

A key strength of D EEP SPATIAL lies in its true 3D reconstruction, which preserves biological continuity with high fidelity. This 184 

is evidenced by its superior spatial concordance with ground-truth 3D data compared to conventional 2.5D stacking approaches. 185 

Such fidelity enables the recovery of structures that are often missed due to sparse sectioning, most notably continuous vascular 186 

networks within tumors. In breast cancer IMC datasets, D EEP SPATIAL reconstructs intact vasculature from fragmented sections, 187 

revealing spatial patterns such as the preferential localization of immune cells at distal vascular termini 38 . D EEP SPATIAL is 188 

broadly compatible with both spatial proteomics (IMC) and transcriptomics platforms (including Deep-STARmap and openST), 189 

and remains robust under challenging conditions, such as irregular inter-slice spacing in HNSCC openST data, while preserving 190 

cell identity and marker expression profiles. Importantly, it also addresses key computational bottlenecks, as detailed in our 191 

comprehensive cost analysis (Table S3). For instance, the complete reconstruction of a 39-million-cell mouse whole-brain atlas 192 

was achieved in approximately 41.6 hours on a mid-range server. In stark contrast, conventional baseline methods such as 193 

SpatialZ would require an estimated 801 hours for the same scale, underscoring the exceptional efficiency of our approach. 194 

Collectively, the combination of biological fidelity, cross-platform applicability, and unprecedented computational scalability 195 

positions D EEP SPATIAL as a practical solution for large-scale three-dimensional modeling of tissue microenvironments. 196 

Despite these advancements, two primary limitations warrant consideration. First, the fidelity of the continuous 3D recon-197 

struction is intrinsically tied to the quality of the upstream 2D input data and the initial spatial alignment. D EEP SPATIAL is 198 

susceptible to technical noise inherent in spatial omics, such as imperfect cell segmentation, high transcriptomic dropout rates, 199 

or alignment artifacts, which can propagate through the generative ODE trajectory. Second, the accuracy of flow matching-based 200 

generation fundamentally depends on the spatial interval between adjacent slices. While the framework seamlessly interpolates 201 

standard gaps, extreme physical distances or abrupt, non-continuous morphological mutations (e.g., at sharp organ boundaries) 202 

remain challenging, potentially leading to over-smoothing or the generation of spurious transitional states. 203 

Beyond the scope of these immediate technical constraints, D EEP SPATIAL fundamentally redefines what can be extracted 204 

from spatial omics data. By providing whole 3D virtual tissue, the framework serves as a versatile computational platform 205 

for the development of next-generation downstream 3D analytical tools. This catalyzes a critical shift from traditional planar 206 

analyses to true volumetric quantification, unlocking the ability to rigorously map 3D cell-cell communication networks and 207 

topologically complex spatial domains. Furthermore, by expanding the continuous vector field to incorporate the temporal 208 

dimension, future iterations could seamlessly transition from 3D to true 4D spatio-temporal modeling. Such advancements 209 

will provide unprecedented mechanistic insights into highly dynamic biological processes, including organogenesis, tissue 210 

repair, and longitudinal tumor evolution. Ultimately, D EEP SPATIAL establishes a robust, continuous analytical infrastructure, 211 

propelling spatial omics into the era of comprehensive whole-organ mapping. 212 

## 4 Methods 213 

4.1 Problem formulation 214 

The fundamental objective of continuous 3D spatial omics reconstruction is to generate a cohesive 3D tissue volume, denoted as 215

4.2 Data alignment and setup 228 

To construct a coherent 3D microenvironment from sparsely sampled 2D sections, it is imperative to first align the discrete 229 

source slices into a common spatial coordinate system. For datasets equipped with a pre-registered coordinate framework 230 

(e.g., the Allen Mouse Brain CCFv3 39 ), we directly adopt the registered spatial coordinates. Conversely, for datasets lacking a 231 

pre-registered system, we apply pairwise spatial alignment algorithms, such as PASTE 16 , which integrate high-dimensional 232 

gene expression similarity and physical spatial proximity to robustly align cells across adjacent slices. 233 

Following this initial spatial alignment, we set up the computational domain to ensure numerical stability during neural network 234 

training and ODE integration. Specifically, we project the aligned physical coordinates into a standardized, dimensionless 235 

normalization space. The aligned 2D spatial coordinates x ∈ R2 and the physical depth z across all input slices (e.g., S0236 

and S1) are globally normalized to the range [0, 1] using a min-max scaling approach. We record the global extreme values 237 

and corresponding physical spans for the entire measured tissue volume to perform this linear transformation. This system 238 

setup guarantees that the continuous vector field vt is learned within a well-conditioned, isotropic coordinate system, strictly 239 

mitigating artifacts induced by arbitrary physical measurement scales. Upon generating the continuous volume V01 , the physical 240 

space is exactingly restored using the inverse transformation, mapping the normalized generated coordinates back to their 241 

absolute physical dimensions to assign precise, metric-accurate 3D coordinates to all virtual cells. 242
