Understanding the behavior of α- and β-cells within intact human islets is essential for 23 

elucidating mechanisms of metabolic control in diabetes. Current cell-type identification 24 

strategies rely on destructive labeling or on advanced imaging modalities such as 25 

Fluorescence Lifetime Imaging Microscopy (FLIM), which provide rich metabolic 26 

information but require specialized instrumentation and acquisition protocols. 27 

Here we show that structured intracellular intensity patterns derived from endogenous 28 

autofluorescence are sufficient to discriminate α and β cells in living human islets. Using 29 

rotation-invariant Local Ternary Pattern (LTP) descriptors combined with morphological 30 

features, we achieve highly accurate classification (AUC = 0.92), improving upon previously 31 

reported benchmarks. The resulting framework is lightweight, interpretable, and compatible 32 

with standard imaging configurations, enabling accessible and scalable analysis of label-free 33 

microscopy data. Interpretability analyses demonstrate that discrimination is driven 34 

predominantly by fine-scale intracellular intensity organization rather than global 35 

morphology. In the spectral window employed, cytoplasmic autofluorescence is prominently 36 

shaped by lipofuscin-rich granules. Consistent with prior reports of higher lipofuscin 37 

accumulation in β-cells, the dominant features identified here likely reflect differences in 38 

granule abundance and spatial organization between endocrine cell types. These findings 39 

indicate that endogenous intensity patterns encode sufficient structural information for 40 

reliable α/β discrimination, providing a biologically grounded and fully non-destructive 41 

framework for the identification of pancreatic islet cell types. 42

## Introduction 44 

At the core of blood glucose regulation lies the coordinated secretion of glucagon and insulin 45 

by α and β cells within the pancreatic islets 1–4 . The activity of α and β cells is finely 46 

regulated, as alterations in their number or function can lead to severe metabolic diseases, 47 

such as diabetes 5–7 . A defining challenge in metabolic research has been observing and 48 

reliably distinguishing these cell types as they function within a living system. The current 49 

gold standard for identification, immunohistochemistry, requires cell fixation—a destructive 50 

process that affects cell viability, thereby precluding in vivo analyses 8–10 . Alternative 51 

strategies based on genetically encoded reporters, typically delivered through viral 52 

transduction and driven by cell-type-specific promoters, enable live-cell identification but 53 

require exogenous manipulation and may alter the native cellular state 11,12 .54 

The effort to visualize live islets in vivo has faced fundamental trade-offs. Magnetic 55 

Resonance Imaging (MRI) offers spatial resolution but suffers from low sensitivity 3,13 , while 56 

nuclear imaging (PET/SPECT) offers high sensitivity but very low spatial resolution, 57 

preventing analysis of individual islets 14,15 . Label-free optical methods offer cellular-level 58 

detail, but most (like Raman Spectroscopy or OCM) are constrained by the shallow 59 

penetration depth of light 16–18 , making them unsuitable for islet samples. Two-Photon 60 

Microscopy (2PM) overcomes this by using longer-wavelength light, enabling deep-tissue 61 

(up to 1 mm) functional imaging with low phototoxicity 19–22 . A key application, Two-Photon 62 

Fluorescence Lifetime Imaging Microscopy (2PM-FLIM), has emerged as a powerful method 63 

for non-invasive, longitudinal studies 23–25 .64 

In our previous FLIM-based study, we demonstrated that lifetime-derived parameters enable 65 

reliable discrimination between α and β cells (AUC = 0.86), thereby establishing a 66 

benchmark for label-free functional identification 26 . Notably, feature-importance analysis in 67 

that work revealed that a substantial portion of the classification power was already 68 

associated with static autofluorescence intensity features, with intensity-related descriptors 69 

accounting for the majority of the model contribution. In the spectral range employed (420– 70 

460 nm under 740 nm excitation), cytoplasmic autofluorescence reflects NAD(P)H-related 71 

signals but is also prominently influenced by lipofuscin-rich granules. Previous studies, 72 

including our own analysis of the same dataset, reported a higher lipofuscin content in β-cells 73 

compared to α-cells 24,27,28 , suggesting that intensity-derived features may capture differences 74 

in lipofuscin granule abundance and spatial organization between endocrine cell types. These 75 

observations raise the possibility that spatially organized autofluorescence intensity patterns 76 

may encode α/β cell identity at the intracellular level. Despite the added metabolic 77 

information provided by fluorescence lifetime measurements, FLIM acquisition requires 78 

specialized instrumentation and extended acquisition and processing steps 29–34 , which may 79 

hinder its routine implementation in high-throughput or resource-constrained settings. 80 

Building upon these observations, the present study investigates whether the discriminative 81 

signal identified previously can be systematically recovered using intensity information 82 

alone. We show that accurate classification performance (AUC = 0.92), improving upon the 83 

previous benchmark, can be achieved using exclusively intensity-pattern and morphological 84

## Results 104 

From image collection to dataset creation 105 

The initial stage of the machine learning workflow builds on our previous work (Figure 1B-106 

C) and focuses on generating a high-quality single-cell training dataset. This process 107 

comprised three main experimental phases. First, live human islets were imaged using label-108 

free autofluorescence intensity microscopy, exciting at 740 nm and collecting emission in the 109 

420–460 nm range, which is dominated by NAD(P)H and lipofuscin signals. Second, 110 

NAD(P)H fluorescence lifetime imaging (FLIM) was acquired at the same focal plane to 111 

capture metabolic information. Third, islets were fixed and immunostained with antibodies 112 

against glucagon and insulin to enable definitive identification of individual α and β cells. 113

Machine Learning Pipeline and Model Design 189 

To address the non-linear separability of the data established above, we developed the 190 

machine learning pipeline outlined in Figure 3, specifically designed to ensure robustness, 191 

reproducibility, and fair model comparison. The dataset was first partitioned into training and 192 

held-out test sets using an 80/20 split, repeated across 10 independent, stratified random 193 

seeds. For each seed, nine machine learning classifiers were optimized using the training set 194 

via an inner 5-fold cross-validation scheme combined with Bayesian hyperparameter tuning 195 

(see Supplementary Table 1 ). Once optimal hyperparameters were identified, each model was 196 

retrained on the full training set, which was extensively augmented (200-fold) through 197 

random rotations and reflections to promote invariance to geometric transformations. From 198 

each cell image in both the augmented training set and the held-out test set, a 60-dimensional 199 

feature vector was extracted using a computationally lightweight process compatible with 200 

real-time applications ( see Supplementary Section S7 ). During cross-validation, non-201 

informative features exhibiting consistently low variance were pruned to improve model 202 

stability. The resulting feature vectors were assembled into a feature matrix, paired with a 203 

corresponding target vector encoding cell identity, derived from the immunofluorescence 204 

ground truth. Model performance was evaluated on the unseen held-out test set using AUC, 205 

Accuracy, Precision, Recall, and F1-score, and further assessed through stress tests involving 206 

random image rotations and reflections. To ensure reproducibility, the entire workflow was 207 

repeated independently for each of the 10 random seeds. For the final comparative analysis, 208 

models displaying excessive per-class imbalance (absolute difference > 0.2 in Precision, 209 

Recall, or F1-score) were excluded. The set of evaluated classifiers spanned linear models 210 

(Logistic Regression), kernel-based methods (Support Vector Machines), and tree-based 211 

ensembles (Random Forest, Light Gradient Boosting Machine, and XGBoost). In addition, to 212 

assess the benefit of model aggregation, we implemented meta-learning strategies, including 213 

two voting ensembles and a stacking ensemble. 214

Feature Importance and Biological Interpretation 297 

298 

To elucidate the predictive mechanisms underlying the Light Gradient Boosting Machine 299 

(LGBM) model, we conducted a comprehensive feature interpretability analysis combining 300 

permutation feature importance, partial dependence plots (PDPs), and Shapley Additive 301 

Explanations (SHAP). Permutation Feature Importance analysis (Figure 5A) revealed that the 302 

model’s predictive performance is predominantly driven by Local Ternary Pattern (LTP) 303 

texture features, with LTP Upper Pattern 7 (LTP Upper Pat 7 in Figure 8) emerging as the 304 

dominant predictor. These descriptors capture fine-scale subcellular texture, suggesting that 305 

intracellular spatial heterogeneity plays a central role in distinguishing cell types. While 306 

several shape-related features (specifically Hu Moments and Max Defect Depth) retained 307 

measurable importance, their contribution was markedly lower than that of the dominant 308 

texture-based feature, indicating that morphological cues alone are insufficient to fully 309 

explain the classification outcome. To further characterize the relationship between the most 310 

relevant features and the model’s predictions, Partial Dependence Plots were computed 311 

(Figure 5B). Among all examined features, LTP Upper Pattern 7 exhibited the strongest 312 

effect: the predicted probability of a cell being a β-cell increased sharply in a non-linear, 313 

sigmoidal manner as the feature value increased. This behavior identifies LTP Upper Pattern 314 

7 as a potent intensity-pattern biomarker. Importantly, this observation aligns with the 315 

biological evidence that β-cells exhibit a higher abundance of autofluorescent granules, 316 

resulting in a more heterogeneous intracellular intensity organization that LTP-based 317 

descriptors effectively capture.. Secondary contributors, including Max Defect Depth and 318 

Inertia Eigenvalue 1, displayed qualitatively similar trends, with increasing feature values 319 

associated with a higher probability of β-cell classification, albeit with substantially smaller 320 

effect sizes. These features provide complementary information related to cell boundary 321 

complexity and mass distribution, respectively. In contrast, features such as Hu Moment 2 322 

and LTP Upper Patterns 4 and 5 were primarily associated with the α cell class. Their PDPs 323 

exhibited flat or decreasing trends for β-cell probability, suggesting that specific global 324 

structural signatures (Hu moments) and alternative local texture patterns are more 325 

characteristic of α-cell morphology. While PDPs assume marginal independence between 326 

features, the consistency of these trends with SHAP-based analyses supports the robustness 327 

of these observations despite potential feature correlations. We performed a SHAP-based 328 

interpretability analysis aggregated across 10 independent random seeds to ensure stability 329 

and robustness of the feature attributions. The global SHAP summary plot (Figure 5C) 330 

closely mirrored the permutation importance results, confirming LTP Upper Pattern 7 as the 331 

dominant driver of model predictions. High values of this feature were uniformly associated 332 

with large positive SHAP values, strongly pushing predictions toward the β-cell class. This 333 

convergence of independent interpretability methods reinforces the conclusion that the 334 

subcellular texture captured by this LTP pattern constitutes a robust and biologically 335 

meaningful discriminator of β-cells. Conversely, secondary features such as Hu Moment 1 336 

and Hu Moment 2 showed higher values predominantly associated with negative SHAP 337 

contributions, aligning them with α-cell identity. 338

## Discussion 384 

In this study, we developed and validated a robust and accurate machine learning framework 385 

for the label-free classification of pancreatic islet cells based solely on standard 386 

autofluorescence intensity images. We demonstrate that reliable α/β cell discrimination can 387 

be achieved using widely accessible optical setups, thereby broadening the methodological 388 

flexibility of label-free islet analysis beyond lifetime-dependent approaches. Through a 389 

rigorous comparative evaluation, a Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LGBM) model 390 

emerged as the optimal solution, achieving performance statistically equivalent to more 391 

complex ensemble approaches while adhering to the principle of parsimony. Stress testing 392 

further demonstrated that the classifier maintains stable predictive behavior under common 393 

image transformations, confirming the robustness of the learned decision boundaries and the 394 

invariance of the underlying feature representation. 395 

Beyond classification performance, a central contribution of this work lies in its 396 

interpretability. Feature importance, partial dependence, and SHAP analyses consistently 397 

revealed that discrimination is driven predominantly by subtle intracellular intensity 398 

signatures, captured by Local Ternary Pattern (LTP) features 36,37 , rather than by global 399 

morphological descriptors. Notably, in the spectral window employed here, cytoplasmic 400 

autofluorescence includes contributions from NAD(P)H but is prominently characterized by 401 

bright lipofuscin-rich granules, which generate strong, spatially structured intensity patterns 402 

within the cytoplasm. Previous studies, including our prior work on the same dataset, showed 403 

that β-cells exhibit a significantly higher lipofuscin content compared to α-cells 27,28 . The 404 

dominant contribution of intensity-based descriptors observed here is therefore consistent 405 

with the heterogeneous spatial distribution of autofluorescent granules within the cytoplasm. 406 

In this context, the extracted intensity-pattern features likely capture differences in granule 407 

density and spatial organization, providing a quantitative and biologically grounded readout 408 

of α/β cell identity. More broadly, these findings suggest that descriptors of autofluorescence 409 

intensity patterns may act as proxies for underlying cellular states, linking image-derived 410 

features to subcellular organization and metabolic heterogeneity. 411 

By enabling accurate cell-type discrimination from standard autofluorescence intensity 412 

images alone, the proposed framework provides an alternative implementation that does not 413 

rely on lifetime-based imaging modalities, such as FLIM. This methodological simplification 414 

substantially broadens the applicability of label-free islet analysis and opens new 415 

opportunities to: 416 

- investigate α–β cell interactions and paracrine dysregulation without destructive 417 

labeling 8–10 ;418 

- monitor cellular alterations during islet transplantation, including immune infiltration 419 

or early apoptotic events; and 420 

- track functional heterogeneity within intact islets, facilitating the identification of 421 

specialized or “hub” cell populations and their network dynamics 38 .422

## Materials and Methods 450 

Data source 451 

All imaging data analyzed in this study were previously generated and published by 452 

Azzarello et al. 24 and made publicly available through the associated dataset 38 . No new 453 

biological samples were collected, and no additional wet-lab experiments were performed for 454 

the present study. Details regarding human islet isolation, donor characteristics, ethical 455 

approval, two-photon microscopy acquisition, FLIM measurements, and 456 

immunofluorescence-based cell-type annotation are fully described in Azzarello et al 24 . The 457 

present work exclusively involves computational re-analysis of previously published data. 458 

Ethical approval and consent procedures were obtained in the original study 24 . Fluorescence 459 

Lifetime Imaging (FLIM) data were excluded from this analysis, which relied solely on 460 

autofluorescence intensity images. 461 

Dataset Curation and Preprocessing 462 

A computational pipeline was developed to process the 1,932 raw NumPy image files and 463 

associated metadata into a curated dataset. Initially, metadata were cleaned to standardize 464 

annotations (e.g., normalizing glucose concentrations). 465 

First, a 'balanced' filtering strategy was implemented: only ‘beta’ cells previously defined by 466 

F. Azzarello et al. 24 as unsuitable for analysis were discarded. This filtering step was omitted 467 

for ‘alpha’ cells to mitigate class imbalance. A justification for the inclusion of alpha cells, 468 

along with details on the testing methodology, can be found in Supplementary Section S1.3 .469 

Corresponding results are presented in Supplementary Section S2.6. 470 

Second, an automated overlap detection algorithm was applied to remove spatially 471 

overlapping cell masks and prevent analytical bias. Cells were grouped by experimental 472 

context and pairwise overlaps were calculated; if the shared pixel area exceeded 10% of the 473 

smaller cell's area, both cells were discarded. 474 

Each cell passing curation underwent a standardized processing sequence. Intensity values 475 

were normalized to an 8-bit dynamic range (0–255), and background noise was eliminated 476 

via thresholding. The primary cell contour was identified, cropped to its tightest bounding 477 

box, and resized to fit within a standardized 150x150 pixel black canvas. This fixed 478 

dimension ensured that subsequent geometric augmentations would not clip the cell image. 479 

Following the beta cell filtering process, the final dataset comprised 1,271 single-cell images 480 

of beta cells—an increase of circa 400 images compared to previous work. 481 

Data Augmentation 482 

To enhance model robustness, mitigate overfitting, and address class imbalance, a multi-483 

faceted data augmentation strategy was implemented using the OpenCV library. The 484 

augmentation pipeline relied on two fundamental geometric transformations—horizontal 485

Feature Extraction 490 

A 60-dimensional feature vector was extracted from each standardized 150×150-pixel cell 491 

image to quantify intensity-pattern and morphological characteristics. The extraction pipeline 492 

was optimized for high-throughput computation. 493 

1. Morphological Features (40 dimensions): Shape descriptors were derived from the 494 

primary cell contour extracted using cv2.findContours. This vector comprised: 495 

> ●

22 classical morphometric features (e.g., area, perimeter, circularity); 496 

>  

7 Log-Transformed Hu Moments computed using cv2.HuMoments; 497 

>  

11 Fourier Descriptors, calculated using numpy.fft.fft, were derived by 498 

treating the contour as a complex vector. Robustness was ensured by zeroing 499 

the DC component (translation invariance), normalizing coefficients by the 500 

first fundamental frequency (scale invariance), and taking the absolute 501 

magnitude (rotation invariance). 502 

2. Texture Features (20 dimensions): Internal autofluorescence texture was quantified 503 

using a histogram of Rotation Invariant Uniform 2 (RIU2) Local Ternary Patterns 504 

(LTP) using a custom implementation built with the standard math library. This was 505 

computed on 8-bit grayscale images with a neighborhood of P=8, radius R=1, and 506 

tolerance threshold t=5. The calculation used an optimized Numba JIT 507 

implementation with a pre-computed Look-Up Table (LUT) for instantaneous pattern-508 

to-bin mapping. 509 

Model Training and Validation Strategy 510 

The dataset was partitioned into a training set (80%) and a held-out test set (20%), stratified 511 

by cell type (using train_test_split from sklearn.model_selection). Model training and 512 

hyperparameter optimization were performed exclusively on the training partition using a 5-513 

fold stratified cross-validation (CV) scheme (using the same package as before, 514 

StratifiedKFold). 515 

A nested directory structure was generated to manage data splits and prevent leakage. Within 516 

each CV fold: 517 

1. Augmentation: The training data were augmented randomly 20 times and the 518 

validation data 5 times, creating unique augmented datasets for each fold. 519 

2. Scaling: Feature vectors were standardized using a StandardScaler. To strictly prevent 520 

leakage, this "Local Scaler" was fitted only on the augmented training features of the 521

3. Feature Selection: A variance threshold (using VarianceThreshold from 524

4. Outlier Detection: Possible outliers in single training sets were detected and 528

Hyperparameter Optimization 530 

A Bayesian Optimization strategy using Gaussian Process regression (scikit-optimize) was 531 

employed to tune hyperparameters for each classification algorithm (e.g., RandomForest, 532 

XGBoost). The objective function maximized a combined_score, defined as the average of 533 

the Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUC) and Accuracy. The 534 

optimization ran for 40 iterations, evaluating performance via the 5-fold CV. The 535 

hyperparameter set yielding the highest mean CV score was selected. Two artifacts were 536 

saved: the optimization history and the Out-of-Fold (OOF) predictions, which were crucial 537 

for subsequent ensemble construction. For more information on the search spaces for each 538 

model, please refer to the Supplementary Table 1. 539 

Ensemble Model Construction 540 

Three distinct ensemble strategies were developed using the OOF predictions from the cross-541 

validation process as a meta-dataset. 542 

>  

Greedy Selection Ensemble: An optimized voting ensemble was built using a greedy 543 

forward selection algorithm. Starting with the single best-performing model, 544 

remaining models were iteratively added if they improved the ensemble's 545 

combined_score. The final list of selected models constituted the voting ensemble. 546 

>  

Overall Voting Ensemble: A simple ensemble constructed by averaging the 547 

probability predictions from all candidate models. 548 

>  

Overall Stacked Ensemble: A stacked generalization ensemble was created where 549 

OOF predictions served as the training set (X_meta) for a meta-learner. Various 550 

algorithms (Logistic Regression, Gradient Boosting Machine, K-Nearest Neighbors) 551 

were evaluated as meta-learners. The candidate yielding the highest average 552 

combined_score was selected and re-trained on the entire meta-dataset. 553

Optimal models, configured with the best hyperparameter combinations identified through 555 

Bayesian Optimization, were retrained on the entire training dataset after applying a 200-fold 556 

global augmentation. A Global Scaler was fitted exclusively on this augmented training set 557 

and subsequently applied to the unseen held-out test set to prevent data leakage. Outliers 558
