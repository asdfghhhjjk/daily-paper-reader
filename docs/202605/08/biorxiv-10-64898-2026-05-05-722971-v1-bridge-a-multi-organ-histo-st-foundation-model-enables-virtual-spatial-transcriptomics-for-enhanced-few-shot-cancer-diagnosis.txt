SUMMARY 14 

Recent studies have explored generating virtual spatial transcriptomics (ST) profiles from his-15 

tological images, offering a promising alternative to laboratory-measured molecular profiling. 16 

However, existing approaches predominantly rely on single-organ models and require sub-17 

stantial organ-specific training data, restricting their accuracy under challenging few-shot con-18 

ditions in clincical practice, where less than 10 slides are available for specific organs or tech-19 

niques. Here, we present BRIDGE, a multi-organ foundation model pre-trained on over 600,000 20 

paired histology-ST profiles across 13 human organs and three sequencing techniques. By ro-21 

bustly aligning morphological features and genomic information within a shared multi-organ 22 

latent space, BRIDGE can leverage common biological knowledge across distinct tissues to 23 

enable accurate and generalizable pan-cancer molecular profiling. Without additional organ-24 

specific fine-tuning, BRIDGE accurately predicts the spatial expression of 80 biomarker genes, 25 

achieving an average Pearson correlation coefficient (PCC) of 0.474—a 30% improvement 26 

over existing state-of-the-art models under three clinically challenging few-shot scenarios. With 27 

generated virtual ST, BRIDGE outperforms current state-of-the-art pathology foundation mod-28 

els in predicting cancer survival, achieving an average concordance index (C-index) of 0.724 29 

across six TCGA cohorts. Notably, BRIDGE maintains exceptional performance even in zero-30 

shot scenarios involving three cancer types not seen during its training, achieving an average 31 

C-index of 0.717, thereby demonstrating its strong generalization capability that transcends 32 

organ- and subtype-specific boundaries. Moreover, BRIDGE-generated virtual spatial tran-33 

scriptomes match the prognostic accuracy of bulk RNA-seq, highlighting their potential as a 34 

spatially informative alternative to laboratory sequencing. In general, BRIDGE represents a 35 

data-efficient tool in virtual ST that facilitates biomedical discoveries in clinical few-shot con-36 

texts and advances diagnosis of understudied cancers without sufficient samples. SPACE 37

# INTRODUCTION 38 

Histological imaging provides detailed morphological information crucial for clinical diagnostics, 39 

yet it lacks molecular resolution essential for understanding cancer biology and disease mech-40 

anisms. Spatial transcriptomics (ST) addresses this limitation by enabling gene expression 41 

analysis within the spatial context of pathological sections 1. However, the high cost, techni-42

availability of large-scale datasets. Thus, computationally integrating histological imaging with 44 

spatial transcriptomics to infer virtual profiling directly from routine histology slides presents a 45 

promising, scalable, and cost-effective alternative for cancer diagnosis (Fig. 1a). 46

# RESULTS 94 

We provide a schematic overview of the BRIDGE framework in Fig. 2. BRIDGE integrates 95 

generative and contrastive learning strategies to support two complementary spatial transcrip-96 

tomics inference tasks: direct prediction and retrieval-based inference, each offering distinct 97 

merits. Specifically, BRIDGE employs three interconnected learning objectives during training: 98 

(1) a contrastive loss, aligning visual and genomic modalities into a unified latent representation; 99 

(2) a generative loss, directly enhancing gene expression prediction accuracy from histological 100 

morphology; and (3) a reconstruction loss, preserving genomic information fidelity within the 101 

shared latent representation. For prediction tasks, BRIDGE leverages its pretrained histology 102 

image encoder and dedicated image-to-gene prediction module to infer spatially resolved gene 103 

expression profiles directly from previously unseen histological images. For retrieval tasks, 104 

BRIDGE utilizes reference gene expression datasets—multi-organ ST, single-organ ST, or 105 

single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq)—to identify and retrieve the most similar genomic 106 

profiles based on morphological features extracted from query histology images. To further 107 

demonstrate BRIDGE’s biological and clinical utility, we apply these inferred spatial gene ex-108 

pression profiles to cancer survival analyses using large-scale histological images from The 109 

Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA), illustrating BRIDGE’s potential for precision cancer prognosis 110 

in clinical settings. 111

BRIDGE Demonstrates Robustness Under Extreme Low-data Conditions. Data scarcity 139 

poses a severe challenge for spatial transcriptomics prediction, particularly evident in under-140 

studied cancers. For example, BIG-600K includes only two slides each for ovary and nasophar-141 

ynx (from different donors). With one slide used for training and the other for testing, substan-142 

tial donor-specific variability introduced significant batch effects, negatively impacting baseline 143 

methods: graph-based models yielded negative PCC for ovary, while DeepSpaCE and ST-144 

Net produced negative PCC for nasopharynx (Fig. 3b). Despite these difficulties, multi-organ 145 

BRIDGE consistently delivered robust predictions (PCC > 0.30) even under these stringent 146 

low-data conditions. Such robustness arises from BRIDGE’s effective leveraging of shared bi-147 

ological knowledge across multiple tissues learned during heterogeneous multi-organ training. 148 

BRIDGE Effectively Mitigates Batch Effects from Technical Variation and Multiple 149 

Laboratories. Another critical challenge arises from batch effects due to differences in se-150 

quencing technologies and laboratory conditions. For instance, the skin dataset was imbal-151 

anced, comprising 25% older Spatial Transcriptomics (ST) slides (low sampling resolution) 152 

and 75% newer 10X Visium slides (higher sampling resolution). Prostate exhibited the op-153 

posite imbalance (93.5% older ST vs. 6.5% newer 10X). Consequently, baseline methods 154 

struggled to accurately predict gene expression on minority-platform slides (Fig. 3b). Similarly, 155 

lung, intestine, and liver datasets combined multiple laboratory sources (five, four, and three 156 

sources, respectively), amplifying batch effects from varying donor populations, instruments, 157 

and protocols. Remarkably, multi-organ BRIDGE effectively addressed these batch effects by 158 

leveraging mutual biological and technical insights from other organs sharing similar character-159 

istics, consistently outperforming baseline methods and demonstrating strong generalizability 160 

across realistic clinical scenarios. 161

BRIDGE Effectively Retrieves High-fidelity Spatially Resolved Gene Expression 173 

Profiles Across Diverse Retrieval Scenarios 174 

In addition to direct prediction, BRIDGE supports retrieval-based inference of spatial gene ex-175 

pression by matching histological images to similar genomic profiles in its aligned latent space 176 

(Fig. 2c). Compared with direct prediction, retrieval-based inference can better preserve bio-177 

logically meaningful gene-gene relationships by leveraging existing high-quality transcriptomic 178 

reference datasets. Furthermore, aggregating retrieved gene expression data from diverse 179 

sources can mitigate batch effects and discrepancies introduced by variability in tissue origins, 180 

donor populations, and sequencing platforms (Fig. 4a). To comprehensively assess BRIDGE’s 181 

retrieval capabilities, we compared it with BLEEP [14], a state-of-the-art retrieval-based spatial 182 

transcriptomics imputation method. Unlike BLEEP—which relies on simplistic retrieval pools 183 

composed of consecutive tissue sections from a single donor—we systematically evaluated 184 

BRIDGE across biologically and clinically relevant retrieval configurations. Specifically, we de-185 

fined retrieval pools based on three critical dimensions: (1) organ specificity (single-organ vs. 186 

multi-organ), (2) sequencing modality (ST-seq vs. scRNA-seq), and (3) patient heterogene-187 

ity (mixed-patient vs. donor-paired), resulting in four distinct configurations (Fig. 4a). Due to 188 

current data unavailability, the “Single-organ Paired-patient scRNA-seq Pool” requiring paired 189 

scRNA-seq and ST-seq data from identical donors) remains a promising future direction. 190 

Organ-agnostic Retrieval Enables Robust Profiling Without Prior Information. A key 191 

clinical challenge is that the precise organ origin of tissue specimens may initially be unknown 192 

or ambiguous. Additionally, tissues across distinct organs often share similar cell types, bi-193 

ological functions, or disease-related gene expression signatures. Therefore, constructing a 194 

multi-organ retrieval pool is biologically meaningful, potentially enabling robust retrieval with-195 

out requiring prior organ-specific knowledge. We first evaluated BRIDGE using the Multi-organ 196 

Mixed-patient ST-seq pool—representing the entire BIG-600K dataset encompassing diverse 197 

tissue origins and donor populations (Fig. 4b,c & Supplementary Fig. S4). BRIDGE consis-198 

tently outperformed BLEEP, achieving an average slide-level PCC of 0.448 compared to 0.206 199 

(BLEEP). Visualization of the biomarker gene NONO, associated with tumor progression and 200 

prognosis in lung cancer [18, 19], further highlighted BRIDGE’s superior spatial retrieval accu-201 

racy, achieving a gene-level PCC exceeding 0.61 and significantly surpassing BLEEP (Fig. 4c 202 

& Supplementary Fig. S6). These results underscore BRIDGE’s capacity to leverage cross-203 

organ biological similarities, facilitating reliable, organ-agnostic spatial gene expression infer-204 

ence broadly applicable in clinical contexts. 205

BRIDGE Delivers Accurate Cancer Prognosis and Robustly Generalizes in Zero-255 

shot Scenarios 256 

Accurate survival analysis—predicting critical clinical outcomes such as patient mortality and 257 

disease progression—is essential for personalized treatment planning, clinical decision-making, 258 

and effective patient risk stratification. [25] Here, we systematically evaluated BRIDGE’s abil-259 

ity to infer patient survival outcomes from routine clinical histology slides, using large-scale 260 

cancer cohorts from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) (Supplementary Table S4). Specifi-261 

cally, we assessed BRIDGE’s performance across six diverse cancer cohorts, including three 262 

cancer types unseen during training: Urothelial Bladder Carcinoma (BLCA), Esophageal Carci-263 

noma (ESCA), and Stomach Adenocarcinoma (STAD). Additionally, we assessed BRIDGE on 264 

Breast Invasive Carcinoma (BRCA-HER2+ subtype), Triple-Negative Breast Cancer (BRCA-265 

TNBC subtype, unseen subtype during training), and Lung Adenocarcinoma (LUAD). Notably, 266 

although BRCA-HER2+ and LUAD were included in training, the amount of training data rep-267 

resented is less than one-tenth of the corresponding TCGA testing cohorts, approximating a 268 

few-shot evaluation scenario. This rigorous evaluation allowed us to comprehensively investi-269 

gate BRIDGE’s generalization capabilities under both zero-shot (unseen cancer types) and few-270 

shot (limited training data for known cancer types) clinical contexts. We further benchmarked 271 

BRIDGE against (i) state-of-the-art pathology-image foundation models and (ii) conventional 272 

bulk RNA sequencing—the clinical reference for cancer diagnostics as it captures established 273 

diagnostic and prognostic gene signatures [26–32]. 274 

BRIDGE Achieves Robust Zero-shot Transfer To Unseen Cancer Types. A key chal-275 

lenge in clinical prognosis is accurately predicting outcomes for cancers absent from training 276 

datasets. Although BRIDGE was not trained on bladder, oesophageal, or gastric tumours, 277 

it nevertheless achieved competitive accuracy on these cancers. A plausible explanation is 278 

that the model transfers cross-organ transcriptional patterns that are partly conserved among 279 

anatomically or functionally related tissues—for example, kidney and bladder(anatomical and 280 

functional connections), intestine and oesophagus (joint digestive function), and liver, intestine 281 

and stomach (closely integrated roles in digestion and nutrient metabolism)—rather than rely-282 

ing solely on cancer-specific signals. BRIDGE achieved an impressive average concordance 283 

index (C-index) of 0.717 across these three unseen cancer cohorts (Table 1, Fig. 5b&c), clearly 284 

underscoring its robust zero-shot generalization capability. This finding significantly expands 285 

BRIDGE’s clinical utility, particularly for under-investigated cancers with limited available data. 286

# METHODS 391 

Construction of the BIG-600K Dataset 392 

In this section, we present detailed information on data sources, numerical compositions, and 393 

selection criteria employed during the construction of the BIG-600K dataset. Comprehensive 394 

statistics, including sampled organs, number of whole-slide images (WSIs), and spatial tran-395 

scriptomic spots, are provided in Fig. 1b and Supplementary Table S2. 396 

Data Sources. The BIG-600K dataset integrates spatial transcriptomics (ST) data from 397 

three primary sources: (1) publicly available experimental datasets gathered from peer-reviewed 398 

biomedical journals and preprint platforms such as bioRxiv (https://www.biorxiv.org/); (2) 399 

sequencing outputs from reputable biotechnology companies, notably 10x Genomics (https: 400 

//www.10xgenomics.com/); and (3) open-access repositories including Dryad (https://da 401 

tadryad.org/stash), Human Cell Atlas (https://www.humancellatlas.org/), Mendeley Data 402 

(https://data.mendeley.com/), and Zenodo (https://zenodo.org/). Relevant datasets were iden-403 

tified using keywords such as “spatial transcriptomics.” These sources were selected for their 404 

provision of high-quality, clinically relevant data suitable for robust evaluations and analyses. 405 

Data Selection Criteria. We employed strict inclusion criteria to ensure data suitability: 406 

(1) samples derived exclusively from human tissues; (2) high-resolution histological images 407 

enabling precise delineation of cellular morphology; (3) comprehensive genomic coverage, 408 

typically involving thousands of genes measured per sample; and (4) precise lab-generated 409 

spatial coordinates, facilitating accurate alignment between histological features and spatial 410 

transcriptomic data with minimal manual adjustments. 411 

Data Preprocessing 412 

In this section, we describe the complete preprocessing pipeline. 413 

Histology Image Processing. To align histology images effectively with spatial transcrip-414 

tomics data, we applied several preprocessing steps to whole-slide images (WSIs). Specifi-415 

cally, WSIs were cropped into patches sized 224 × 224 pixels, centered around lab-generated 416 

sequencing coordinates with a physical diameter of 150 μm. Given the variability in spot sizes 417 

(55–100 μm, sequencing method-dependent [42]), this cropping window ensured sufficient con-418 

textual information. Subsequently, image patches predominantly containing background areas 419 

were filtered out using instrument-provided tissue indicators and additional manual quality con-420 

trols. To mitigate color variability resulting from batch effects, structure-preserving color normal-421 

ization [43] was uniformly applied to all patches using a reference slide. Furthermore, various 422 

data augmentation techniques—including random cropping, flipping, rotation, and Gaussian 423 

blurring—were employed to enhance the robustness of the visual encoder within the bi-modal 424 

framework [44]. 425 

Spatial Transcriptome Data Processing. We standardized gene expression data across 426 

datasets by unifying gene identifiers. Given discrepancies in gene annotation formats (e.g., 427 

Human Ensembl gene IDs versus gene names), we constructed a comprehensive mapping 428 

dictionary using Visium-generated “features.tsv” files, supplemented by Ensembl’s BioMart 1429

Framework Architecture of BRIDGE 441 

BRIDGE introduces a novel bi-modal architecture designed to overcome inherent limitations 442 

associated with conventional prediction-only or retrieval-only approaches for inferring spatial 443 

gene expression from histology images. Existing prediction-based methods often overlook 444 

complex molecular details extending beyond visual tissue features, resulting in predictions mis-445 

aligned with biological measurements. Conversely, retrieval-based methods typically rely on 446 

rigid alignment between reference and query histology images, making them susceptible to 447 

domain shifts and technical variations. Moreover, previous retrieval approaches primarily use 448 

contrastive learning, potentially oversimplifying modality relationships by neglecting modality-449 

specific information. BRIDGE addresses these limitations by integrating multiple learning ob-450 

jectives across distinct modules to effectively capture intra- and inter-modality relationships. 451 

Specifically, BRIDGE comprises three main components: Visual Encoder, Genome Encoder, 452 

and Gene Prediction and Reconstruction Decoder, each serving distinct roles in processing 453 

and aligning histological and genomic information. 454 

Visual Encoder. We utilized a DenseNet-121 neural network [48], pre-trained on Ima-455 

geNet [49], as the primary image encoder. Image patches I ∈ RB×3×224 ×224 (with batch size B) 456 

were encoded into original and augmented feature vectors I f, I af ∈ RB×1024 . These vectors were 457 

projected into a shared 128-dimensional embedding space via a multilayer perceptron (MLP), 458 

generating image embeddings I e, I ae ∈ RB×128 .459 

Genome Encoder. For encoding gene expression data, we employed TabNet [50], a spe-460 

cialized architecture for tabular data. The input gene vector G ∈ RB×7730 was masked ran-461 

domly (25% of non-zero values set to zero), yielding G m ∈ RB×7730 . TabNet encoded both 462 

original and masked gene vectors into genomic feature vectors G f, G mf ∈ RB×512 , subsequently 463 

projected into the shared embedding space via another MLP, resulting in gene embeddings 464 

Ge, G me ∈ RB×128 .465 

Gene Prediction and Reconstruction Decoder. The gene prediction head employed 466 

fully connected layers to map visual features I f directly to predicted gene expression profiles 467 

ˆGI ∈ RB×7730 . Concurrently, inspired by masked language modeling (MLM), the reconstruction 468 

head mapped masked genomic features G mf back to gene expression profiles ˆGG ∈ RB×7730 ,469 

reinforcing the robustness of the genome encoder. 470

Data Splits and Training Strategy 495 

To comprehensively evaluate the performance of BRIDGE, we designed two distinct evaluation 496 

scenarios: (1) a challenging few-shot evaluation setting comprising seven WSIs from seven 497 

different organs, and (2) a more comprehensive multi-organ evaluation setting involving sixteen 498 

WSIs from ten human organs. 499 

Few-shot Evaluation Setting. We reserved one WSI for testing from each of the seven 500 

organs characterized by challenging few-shot conditions: nasopharynx, ovary, skin, prostate, 501 

lung, intestine, and liver. This resulted in a total of 7 WSIs containing 22,526 image-gene pairs 502 

(spots). 503 

Multi-organ Evaluation Setting. To further demonstrate BRIDGE’s generalizability across 504 

diverse organs and tissues, we also evaluated performance on a larger-scale multi-organ dataset 505 

encompassing ten human organs: brain, breast, heart, liver, lung, nasopharynx, ovary, prostate, 506 

skin, and intestine. For organs with more than 20 WSIs, two slides were reserved for testing; 507 

otherwise, one slide was reserved. This resulted in a comprehensive evaluation set consisting 508 

of 16 WSIs, comprising a total of 99,874 image-gene pairs (approximately 20% of the training 509 

dataset). 510 

Single-Organ vs. Multi-Organ Training. We trained BRIDGE under two distinct config-511 

urations: single-organ training, where the model was trained individually for each organ, and 512 

multi-organ training, where training involved data from multiple organs simultaneously. Due 513 

to dataset size differences, the single-organ models converged after 20 epochs, whereas the 514 

multi-organ model (approximately 500K data pairs per epoch) converged after 50 epochs. 515 

Technical Configurations. BRIDGE was trained using eight NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3090 516 

GPUs with a total batch size of 384. We employed gradient accumulation (step size of 2), linear 517 

warmup, and cosine annealing learning rate schedules, with a peak learning rate of 1 × 10 –3 518 

for a maximum of 50 epochs. 519
